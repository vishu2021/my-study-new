Q1. (a) Identify five ethical traits on which one can plot the performance of a civil servant.

Justify their inclusion in the matrix.

(b) Identify ten essential values that are needed to be an effective public servant.

Describe the ways and means to prevent non-ethical behaviour in the public servants.- 2021

(a) Here are five ethical traits on which one can plot the performance of a civil servant, along with their justification and recent examples:

  1. Integrity: This refers to the quality of being honest, transparent, and ethical in one's conduct. A civil servant who demonstrates integrity is trusted by the public, and this trust is critical to the functioning of a democratic government.

    Recent examples of civil servants who demonstrated integrity include former Indian Administrative Service (IAS) officer Durga Shakti Nagpal, who took a stand against illegal sand mining in Uttar Pradesh, and former Kerala cadre IAS officer T.V. Anupama, who exposed corruption in the local government.

  2. Accountability: This refers to the willingness of a civil servant to take responsibility for their actions and decisions. Civil servants who demonstrate accountability are more likely to be responsive to the needs of the public and to act in the public interest.

    Recent examples of civil servants who demonstrated accountability include former Karnataka Chief Secretary Arvind Jadhav, who resigned after being criticized for poor flood management, and former Tamil Nadu Chief Secretary Girija Vaidyanathan, who stepped down after facing criticism over her handling of the Sterlite protests.

  3. Professionalism: This refers to the ability of a civil servant to perform their duties in a competent and efficient manner. Civil servants who demonstrate professionalism are more likely to provide high-quality services to the public and to uphold the rule of law.

    Recent examples of civil servants who demonstrated professionalism include Delhi Health Secretary Padmini Singla, who oversaw the establishment of COVID-19 vaccination centers in the city, and former Maharashtra Director General of Police Hemant Nagrale, who played a key role in the investigation of the Mukesh Ambani bomb scare case.

  4. Empathy: This refers to the ability of a civil servant to understand and respond to the needs of the public, particularly those who are marginalized or disadvantaged. Civil servants who demonstrate empathy are more likely to design policies and programs that benefit the most vulnerable sections of society.

    Recent examples of civil servants who demonstrated empathy include Kerala Health Secretary Rajan Khobragade, who worked to provide free medical care to COVID-19 patients, and former Gujarat cadre IAS officer Alka Sirohi, who worked to improve access to education for girls in rural areas.

  5. Respect for diversity: This refers to the ability of a civil servant to appreciate and celebrate diversity in all its forms. Civil servants who demonstrate respect for diversity are more likely to design policies and programs that are inclusive and sensitive to the needs of all sections of society.

    Recent examples of civil servants who demonstrated respect for diversity include former Tamil Nadu Additional Director General of Police K. Jayanth Murali, who promoted gender sensitivity in the police force, and former Karnataka Additional Chief Secretary Rashmi V. Mahesh, who worked to promote the rights of transgender people.

In conclusion, these five ethical traits - integrity, accountability, professionalism, empathy, and respect for diversity - are critical to the performance of civil servants in a democratic society. By demonstrating these traits, civil servants can build trust with the public, provide high-quality services, and design policies and programs that are sensitive to the needs of all sections of society.

(b) Ten essential values for effective public servants:

  1. Integrity: A public servant must be honest, transparent and ethical in their conduct and decision-making processes.

  2. Accountability: Public servants must be accountable for their actions and decisions, and accept responsibility for any mistakes or failures.

  3. Fairness: Public servants must treat all citizens equally and without bias, regardless of their social status, political affiliation, or economic power.

  4. Respect: Public servants must treat all citizens with respect, dignity, and empathy, and acknowledge their rights and freedoms.

  5. Empathy: Public servants must have the ability to understand and share the feelings of citizens, especially those who are disadvantaged or marginalized.

  6. Professionalism: Public servants must have the required knowledge, skills, and experience to carry out their duties and responsibilities in a competent and efficient manner.

  7. Collaboration: Public servants must work together with other stakeholders, including citizens, civil society organizations, and private sector entities, to achieve common goals and objectives.

  8. Innovation: Public servants must be creative and adaptive, and be willing to explore new ideas and approaches to address emerging challenges and problems.

  9. Courage: Public servants must have the courage to stand up for what is right, even if it means going against the status quo or facing opposition.

  10. Service: Public servants must be dedicated to serving the public interest, and prioritize the needs and aspirations of citizens over their own personal interests or preferences.

Ways and means to prevent non-ethical behaviour in public servants:

  1. Training and education: Provide regular training and education to public servants on ethics, integrity, and professionalism, and make them aware of the consequences of non-ethical behaviour.

  2. Code of conduct: Develop and implement a code of conduct for public servants, which clearly outlines the standards of behaviour and conduct expected of them.

  3. Monitoring and evaluation: Establish a system of monitoring and evaluation to ensure that public servants are adhering to the code of conduct and ethical standards.

  4. Whistleblower protection: Provide protection to whistleblowers who report instances of non-ethical behaviour, and ensure that they are not victimized or penalized for doing so.

  5. Transparency and accountability: Ensure that public servants are transparent and accountable in their decision-making processes and actions, and that they are held accountable for any non-ethical behaviour.

  6. Leadership: Promote ethical leadership at all levels of government, and set a good example for others to follow.

  7. Public engagement: Encourage public engagement and participation in the decision-making processes, and ensure that citizens are aware of their rights and responsibilities.

Recent example:

In India, the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC) has been tasked with preventing corruption and promoting ethical behaviour in public servants. The CVC conducts regular training programs for public servants on ethics and integrity, and has developed a code of conduct for public servants. The commission also monitors and investigates instances of corruption and non-ethical behaviour, and provides protection to whistleblowers who report such incidents.

In addition, the Indian government has established a portal called "e-Sahyog" to facilitate public engagement and participation in the decision-making processes, and has implemented a number of transparency and accountability measures, such as the Right to Information Act, to promote transparency and accountability in governance.

(a) Digital technology has been a significant game-changer in the decision-making process in recent years.

While some argue that it is a reliable source of input for rational decision-making, others believe that it has its limitations.

Let's discuss this issue in detail:

Positive impact:

  1. Digital technology allows for faster and more efficient data collection, analysis, and processing, which can provide decision-makers with accurate and timely information for making informed decisions.

  2. It enables data visualization, which helps decision-makers understand complex data sets quickly.

  3. Digital technology also provides a platform for collaborative decision-making by enabling stakeholders to share data and insights.

Negative impact:

  1. Digital technology is only as reliable as the data that feeds it, and data can be biased or inaccurate.

  2. Decision-makers may rely too heavily on digital technology and overlook other important factors that cannot be quantified or easily measured.

  3. Digital technology may also create information overload, making it difficult for decision-makers to sift through and prioritize the data.

Example:

During the COVID-19 pandemic, digital technology played a crucial role in providing decision-makers with real-time information about the virus's spread and its impact on the economy. Governments worldwide relied on digital dashboards to track the number of cases and deaths, hospital capacity, and other critical indicators. However, the data was often inconsistent and incomplete, leading to inaccurate projections and ineffective policies. The reliance on digital technology also overshadowed other critical factors, such as the social and economic impacts of lockdowns.

Preventing non-ethical behavior in public servants:

  1. Clear guidelines and policies on ethical behavior should be established and enforced.

  2. Public servants should receive regular training on ethical behavior and its importance in decision-making.

  3. There should be a system in place for reporting unethical behavior without fear of retaliation.

  4. Public servants should be held accountable for their actions and face consequences for unethical behavior.

  5. An open and transparent culture should be encouraged, where ethical behavior is valued and rewarded.

Example:

In India, the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC) is responsible for preventing corruption and unethical behavior in public servants. The CVC provides guidance on ethical conduct, investigates complaints of corruption, and takes appropriate action against offenders. Additionally, the government has established a whistleblower protection law to protect individuals who report corrupt practices. These measures help ensure that public servants act ethically and are held accountable for their actions.

(b) Public officials often face ethical dilemmas in their daily work, and they need to have innovativeness and creativity to resolve them effectively.

Here are some points to discuss the importance of innovativeness and creativity in resolving ethical dilemmas with a suitable example:

  1. Innovativeness and creativity allow public officials to look beyond conventional solutions to ethical dilemmas and come up with innovative solutions that take into account the unique circumstances of each situation.

  2. For instance, a public official may face a dilemma where a decision needs to be made between two options, both of which have ethical implications. In such a case, the official may need to think creatively to find a solution that takes into account both ethical concerns and the practical considerations.

  3. A recent example of a public official using innovativeness and creativity to resolve an ethical dilemma is the case of an Indian police officer, Sanjukta Parashar. She is known for her innovative methods of dealing with insurgency and terrorism in the north-eastern region of India.

  4. Parashar faced a dilemma when she was assigned the task of counter-insurgency operations in the region. The traditional approach of using force and violence had not been effective in resolving the conflict, and the use of excessive force had resulted in human rights violations and loss of lives.

  5. Parashar used innovativeness and creativity to come up with a new approach to resolve the conflict. She started interacting with the local people, building relationships with them, and understanding their concerns. She also encouraged the surrender of militants and offered them rehabilitation and support to rejoin mainstream society.

  6. Parashar's approach was innovative and creative, as it went beyond the conventional methods of using force and violence. Her approach was effective, as it helped to reduce violence and restore peace in the region.

This is a crucial issue as it affects the lives of vulnerable people who have fled their homes seeking safety and protection. The following points examine the statement and the ethical dimensions involved:

  1. Violation of the right to life and liberty: Sending refugees back to a country where they face persecution or human rights violations can put their lives and liberty at risk. This violates their basic right to life and liberty, which is a fundamental principle of democracy and human rights.

  2. Violation of the principle of non-refoulement: The principle of non-refoulement is a fundamental principle of international law, which prohibits the forced return of refugees to a country where they face persecution or serious human rights violations. This principle is recognized in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the 1951 Convention relating to the Status of Refugees. Violating this principle is a breach of international law and ethics.

  3. The ethical responsibility to protect refugees: As a democratic and open society, nations have a moral obligation to protect refugees and provide them with safety and security. This ethical responsibility is based on the principle of humanity and respect for human rights.

  4. Negative impact on the country's reputation: Turning refugees back to the country where they face persecution or human rights violations can have a negative impact on the country's reputation in the international community. It can undermine its credibility as a defender of human rights and democratic values.

Recent examples of this ethical dilemma include the Rohingya refugee crisis in Myanmar, where thousands of refugees fled to neighboring countries to escape persecution, and the Syrian refugee crisis, where many countries have closed their borders and refused to accept refugees, despite the ongoing conflict and human rights violations in Syria.

(b) Yes, impartiality and non-partisanship are essential qualities for a successful civil servant. Here are some points that support this:

  1. Objective decision-making: Civil servants are responsible for making decisions that affect the lives of people. To ensure that decisions are based on merit and are not influenced by any personal biases or political affiliations, it is crucial for civil servants to be impartial.

  2. Public trust: Civil servants are the face of the government for the public. Their impartiality ensures that the public has trust in the government and its institutions. This is essential for maintaining a healthy democracy.

  3. Fairness: Non-partisan civil servants can ensure fairness in the implementation of policies and programs. This is especially important when it comes to the distribution of resources and services to the public.

  4. Political neutrality: Civil servants are expected to serve the government of the day, irrespective of which political party is in power. Political neutrality ensures that civil servants are not influenced by any particular ideology or political agenda.

Examples of the importance of impartiality and non-partisanship can be seen in the civil services of various countries. For instance:

  1. In the UK, the Civil Service Code lays down the principles of integrity, honesty, impartiality, and objectivity that all civil servants must adhere to.

  2. In India, the All India Services (Conduct) Rules, 1968, lay down the standards of conduct for civil servants. These rules include the requirement of political neutrality and impartiality.

  3. In the United States, the Hatch Act of 1939 prohibits federal employees from engaging in political activities while on duty. This ensures that civil servants remain non-partisan and politically neutral.

In recent times, there have been instances where civil servants have been accused of being partisan or biased towards a particular ideology or political party. This has led to public mistrust in the government and its institutions. Therefore, it is crucial for civil servants to remain impartial and non-partisan in their roles to maintain public trust and uphold the principles of democracy.
