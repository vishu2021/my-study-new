Reasons for Development of New Building  of Parliament of India

The development of a new building for the Parliament of India may be motivated by several reasons.

Here are some key points explaining why such a development might be undertaken:

  1. Expansion of Legislative Capacity:

  • The existing Parliament building, which was completed in 1927, was designed to accommodate a much smaller number of Members of Parliament (MPs) compared to the present strength.

  • The development of a new building can allow for an increased number of MPs, addressing the need for expansion of legislative capacity.

  1. Technological Upgrades:

  • The existing Parliament building may lack modern infrastructure and technological advancements required for efficient functioning in the digital age.

  • A new building can be designed with state-of-the-art facilities, including advanced audio-visual systems, integrated IT infrastructure, and enhanced connectivity to facilitate smoother legislative proceedings.

  1. Enhanced Accessibility:

  • Accessibility is a crucial aspect of democratic governance.

  • The existing Parliament building may have limitations in terms of accessibility for persons with disabilities, making it necessary to develop a new building that adheres to universal accessibility standards.

  • This would ensure equitable participation of all MPs and staff members, as well as visitors to the Parliament.

  1. Safety and Security:

  • Security concerns have evolved significantly over the years, necessitating the development of buildings with enhanced safety measures.

  • A new Parliament building can be designed with advanced security features, including robust surveillance systems, secure entry and exit points, and dedicated areas for screening and verification of individuals, ensuring the safety of the lawmakers, staff, and visitors.

  1. Infrastructure Upgrades:

  • The existing Parliament building may face challenges in terms of aging infrastructure, including electrical systems, plumbing, and structural integrity.

  • By developing a new building, the opportunity arises to create a modern, energy-efficient structure with upgraded infrastructure, reducing maintenance costs and ensuring a more sustainable and resilient facility.

  1. Symbolic Representation:

  • The architecture and design of a Parliament building represent the country's democratic values and aspirations.

  • A new building offers the opportunity to create an iconic structure that reflects the evolving identity of India as a nation, projecting a strong and modern image both domestically and internationally.

  1. Improved Functionality:

  • The design of the existing Parliament building may not cater optimally to the evolving needs of the legislative process.

  • A new building can be tailored to provide improved functionality, including dedicated spaces for committee meetings, specialized chambers for joint sessions, flexible seating arrangements, and efficient circulation patterns, enhancing the overall efficiency and effectiveness of parliamentary proceedings.

  1. Historical Preservation:

  • As the existing Parliament building holds historical significance and architectural value, the development of a new building can allow for its preservation and restoration.

  • The original building can be repurposed for cultural or educational purposes, while the new structure caters specifically to the requirements of a modern Parliament.

It is important to note that the decision to develop a new building for the Parliament of India involves various factors, including consultations with stakeholders, feasibility studies, and cost-benefit analyses, to ensure an informed and prudent approach to the project.

Q1. How have the recommendations of the 14th France Commission of India enabled the States to improve their fiscal position? - 2021

The recommendations of the 14th Finance Commission of India have had a significant impact on the fiscal position of the states in India. Here are some key points outlining how the recommendations have enabled the states to improve their fiscal position:

  1. Increased share of taxes: The Commission recommended increasing the share of states in the divisible pool of taxes from 32% to 42%. This has provided the states with more resources to fund their development programs and schemes.

  2. Greater fiscal autonomy: The increased share of taxes has given the states greater fiscal autonomy, allowing them to decide how to allocate resources based on their specific needs and priorities.

  3. Performance-based incentives: The Commission recommended the use of performance-based incentives to encourage states to improve their fiscal management and implementation of various government programs. This has helped to promote fiscal discipline and accountability among the states.

  4. Non-lapsable fund: The Commission recommended the creation of a non-lapsable fund called the State Disaster Response Fund (SDRF) to be used by the states in the event of a natural disaster. This has ensured that the states have adequate funds to deal with disasters without having to depend on the central government for assistance.

  5. Grants for local bodies: The Commission recommended increasing the grants to local bodies such as panchayats and municipalities, providing them with greater financial resources to deliver public services at the grassroots level.

  6. Health and education: The Commission recommended an increase in funding for health and education sectors, which are critical for the social and economic development of the states.

  7. Greater transparency: The Commission recommended greater transparency in the distribution of funds to the states, ensuring that the funds are distributed in a fair and equitable manner.

Overall, the recommendations of the 14th Finance Commission have enabled the states to improve their fiscal position, providing them with greater resources, autonomy, and accountability.

This has helped to promote sustainable and inclusive development across the country.
