1. Cost of running Parliament/day in India is increasing day by day.

The Price of Democracy: Examining the Rising Cost of Parliament in India

Introduction

The Parliament of India is the supreme temple of our democracy, where elected representatives debate laws, scrutinize the government, and voice the people's concerns. This sacred function demands resources. However, data and public discourse increasingly highlight a worrying trend: the cost of running Parliament per day is rising significantly. While investing in robust democratic institutions is vital, this escalating expenditure amid widespread public austerity demands scrutiny, accountability, and a search for greater efficiency.

The Evidence: A Growing Fiscal Burden

While a single official "cost per day" figure is complex, the rising trend is clear from budgetary allocations and reports. The Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs budget, which covers salaries, allowances, and operational costs for MPs, has seen consistent increases. According to the Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR), the cost to the exchequer for an MP has risen substantially over the last decade.

The major cost drivers are:

  • Salaries and Allowances:
    MPs receive salaries, constituency allowances, office expenses, and travel concessions. These are periodically revised upwards.

  • Infrastructure and Security:
    Maintaining the sprawling Parliament complex, its staff, and providing VIP security to all MPs involves enormous recurring costs.

  • Disruptions and Low Productivity:
    The most critical, non-financial cost is lost time. Frequent adjournments, protests, and wasted sessions mean that the cost per productive hour of legislative business becomes exorbitantly high. The 2023 Monsoon Session saw roughly 80% productivity in the Lok Sabha, implying 20% of resources were spent on non-work.

A Balanced Analysis: Understanding the Cost Drivers

The rising cost is a multi-dimensional issue with both legitimate and concerning aspects.

  1. Legitimate Needs of a Growing Democracy:
    India is a vast nation. MPs require adequate resources to serve large constituencies, maintain offices in Delhi and their home states, and travel frequently. As democracy deepens, administrative costs naturally rise. Providing secure working conditions is also non-negotiable.

  2. The Accountability Deficit:
    The concern arises when rising costs do not correlate with increased productivity, transparency, or quality of governance. Public frustration mounts when high expenditures coincide with pandemonium in the House, fewer sitting days, and rushed passage of bills without debate. As former President Dr. K.R. Narayanan observed, "The Parliament is not merely a law-making body but a watch-dog of the nation's finances." It must apply the same scrutiny to itself.

  3. The Hidden Cost of Disruption:
    The real "cost" is not just rupees spent, but opportunity cost. When Parliament is stalled, crucial laws on the economy, social welfare, and national security are delayed. This paralysis has a tangible impact on national progress and public welfare.

Consequences: Erosion of Public Trust

The perception of a high-cost, low-output Parliament has serious consequences:

  • Cynicism Towards Democracy:
    Citizens, especially the poor, may see Parliament as a self-serving elite club, undermining faith in the democratic system.

  • Misallocation of Scarce Public Resources:
    Funds spent on avoidable costs could be redirected to health, education, or infrastructure.

  • Weakens Scrutiny:
    A dysfunctional Parliament fails in its core duty of holding the executive accountable, leading to potential governance deficits.

Towards a More Efficient and Accountable Parliament

Ensuring value for money in parliamentary functioning requires institutional and behavioural reforms.

  1. Link Pay to Performance and Attendance:
    Institute a system where a part of the daily allowance is linked to attendance and participation in debates in the House and committees. The PRISM model (Proportionate Reduction in Salaries for Misconduct) proposed by some experts can be debated.

  2. Enforce Discipline and Increase Productive Hours:
    Strictly enforce the Rules of Procedure to minimize disruptions. The Speaker/Chairman must have more power to suspend members causing willful obstruction. Mandate a minimum number of sitting days (e.g., 120 days) annually through law, as recommended by the National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution (NCRWC).

  3. Promote Digital Efficiency and Transparency:
    Adopt a paperless Parliament system to cut printing and logistics costs. Live telecast and archiving of all proceedings, including Committee meetings (where possible), should be mandatory to enhance transparency. Rationalize security protocols based on genuine threat assessments.

  4. Empower Department-Related Standing Committees (DRSCs):
    Make referring bills to DRSCs mandatory for detailed scrutiny. This decentralized work improves law quality and utilizes MPs' time more effectively outside the noisy chamber.

  5. Public Disclosure and Audit:
    Publish an annual "Cost of Parliament" report detailing all expenditures, sitting days, bills passed, and hours spent on productive work. This will allow citizens to make an informed assessment.

Conclusion

Democracy is priceless, but its functioning must be cost-effective. The goal is not to cheapen Parliament but to enhance its value. By combining greater fiscal responsibility with a renewed commitment to decorum, productivity, and public service, India's Parliament can justify its costs. It must transform from a perceived high-maintenance institution to a high-output one, truly embodying the will and wisdom of the people it represents. Only then will the rising cost be seen not as a burden, but as a wise investment in a thriving, deliberative democracy.

2. Wasting Tax Payer Money on changing Names, recently Changing name of Mahatma Gandhi NREGA

The Cost of Renaming: A Question of Priority in Public Spending

Introduction

Public funds, collected from the hard-earned income of citizens, are a sacred trust meant for national development and public welfare. Their use demands prudence, purpose, and a focus on tangible outcomes. In recent years, however, a recurring debate has centered on the significant expenditure incurred in renaming existing schemes, institutions, and places. The recent proposal to change the name of the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) to "Mahatma Gandhi Rural Employment Guarantee Act" is the latest flashpoint. While symbolic gestures have a place in nation-building, the use of taxpayer money for name changes, especially during economic strain, raises critical questions of priority and fiscal responsibility.

The Scale and Pattern of Renaming

The trend of renaming is not isolated. Over the past decade, several cities (Allahabad to Prayagraj), historical sites (Mughal Gardens to Amrit Udyan), and schemes have undergone name changes. Each such action incurs substantial, albeit often unpublicized, costs. Changing the name of a flagship scheme like MGNREGA necessitates:

  • Administrative Overhaul:
    Updating all official records, government portals, software codes, and documentation across central, state, and district levels.

  • Physical Rebranding:
    Re-printing lakhs of job cards, office signboards, official manuals, and publicity material in thousands of villages.

  • Communication Costs:
    Issuing clarifications, re-training officials, and managing public confusion over the unchanged law but changed abbreviation.

While no official consolidated figure for such exercises is released, experts like former Finance Secretary S.C. Garg have noted that these are "non-trivial" expenditures that divert bureaucratic energy and funds from core implementation.

A Multi-Dimensional Debate: Symbolism vs. Substance

The issue demands a balanced analysis from multiple perspectives.

  1. The Case for Renaming (The Symbolic Argument):

    • Proponents argue it rectifies historical wrongs, sheds colonial legacies, and honors indigenous culture and national icons. It is seen as an act of cultural assertion and intellectual decolonization.

    • Regarding MGNREGA, it is argued that the name "Mahatma Gandhi" was already there, and the change is minor, aiming for better branding.

  2. The Case Against: The Question of Priority and Cost:

    • Misplaced Priorities:
      When millions seek adequate work under MGNREGA, wage delays are reported, and asset quality is questioned, spending on a name change appears disconnected from the scheme's ground realities. As Mahatma Gandhi himself said, "Recall the face of the poorest man you have seen, and ask yourself if this step will be of any use to him."

    • Fiscal Opportunity Cost:
      The crores spent on rebranding could have been used to bridge wage gaps, improve monitoring, or create more durable assets like ponds and roads under the same scheme. This represents a direct diversion from welfare to symbolism.

    • Erosion of Institutional Memory:
      Frequent renaming of established programs creates confusion among beneficiaries (often poor and less literate) and fragments administrative continuity.

Consequences: Beyond the Fiscal Cost

The ripple effects of such decisions extend beyond the balance sheet:

  • Public Trust Deficit:
    It fuels the perception of a government more focused on political symbolism than pragmatic problem-solving, eroding public trust, especially among the scheme's beneficiaries.

  • Administrative Distraction:
    It burdens the already stretched ground-level bureaucracy with non-essential clerical work, taking focus away from implementation.

  • Politicization of Development:
    It risks reducing seminal, legally-backed social security schemes, born out of bipartisan consensus, into political footballs, undermining their long-term stability.

Striking a Balance: A Framework for Responsible Governance

To ensure public funds are used optimally, a transparent and principled approach is needed.

  1. Establish a Cost-Benefit and Public Consultation Protocol:
    Any proposal for renaming a major scheme or institution must be preceded by a publicly disclosed cost assessment and a wide consultation, including with beneficiaries. The justification must overwhelmingly demonstrate public good, not just political sentiment.

  2. Legislate a "Substance Over Symbol" Fiscal Rule:
    Parliament can consider guidelines that prioritize funding for core functions of a scheme. Any ancillary expenditure like renaming should be capped at a minuscule percentage of the scheme's total annual budget and require specific approval.

  3. Focus on Outcome-Based Evaluation:
    The performance of any welfare scheme should be judged solely on outcome metrics—person-days of employment generated, timely wage payments, quality of assets created, and reduction in distress migration—not on its name.

  4. Honor Icons Through Action, Not Just Nomenclature:
    The best way to honor a legacy like Mahatma Gandhi's is to strengthen the implementation of the scheme that bears his name—ensuring it embodies his principles of self-reliance, rural empowerment, and care for the last person. This is a more meaningful tribute.

  5. Transparency in Expenditure:
    The government must proactively place before Parliament and the public a detailed account of the expenditure incurred in any renaming exercise, allowing for democratic accountability.

Conclusion

In a nation where fiscal resources are perpetually stretched against immense developmental needs, every rupee must fight poverty, build infrastructure, and enhance human capital. Renaming exercises, while seemingly minor, represent a leakage of focus and funds from these critical battles. Democracy demands that the state's energy and the citizen's money be invested primarily in improving lives, not just altering labels. By adhering to a strict principle of "substantive delivery over symbolic change," India can ensure its public spending truly serves the public, honoring the spirit of its icons through impactful action, not just nomenclatural homage.

3. You are watching someone else win, when you are loosing. How could possibly be a wise decision.

The Wisdom in Watching: Finding Strength in Another's Victory

Introduction

In a world obsessed with winning, the experience of watching someone else succeed while we face loss can feel like salt on a wound. Our instinct is to turn away, consumed by envy or self-pity. Yet, counter-intuitively, this very act of observing a rival's triumph can be a profound and wise decision. It is a conscious choice that embodies emotional maturity, strategic thinking, and a growth mindset, transforming a moment of personal defeat into a powerful catalyst for future success.

Understanding the Wisdom: Beyond the Sting of Defeat

The wisdom lies not in enjoying the loss, but in harnessing the moment for self-improvement. Watching a winner offers an unparalleled, real-time case study in success. When we lose a competition, a job opportunity, or a political election, our competitor's victory holds a mirror to our own shortcomings and a map to a better path. As the ancient Indian philosopher Chanakya stated in the Arthashastra, "A wise man learns from the mistakes of others; a fool learns from his own." Observing a winner is an accelerated way to learn from what worked, not just what failed.

The Multi-Dimensional Utility of Observation

Choosing to watch and analyse a rival's victory serves multiple strategic purposes:

  1. A Masterclass in Strategy and Skill:
    In sports, business, or academics, watching the victor provides a live demonstration of superior technique, tactics, and temperament. A defeated athlete studying their opponent's winning move, or a failed start-up analysing a competitor's successful business model, gains insights no theoretical book can provide.

  2. Cultivating Grace and Resilience:
    Publicly acknowledging another's win, especially in fields like politics, builds a reputation for sportsmanship and emotional resilience. After the 2014 and 2019 general elections, leaders who gracefully conceded and engaged constructively in their role as opposition preserved their dignity and political relevance. It shows strength of character, a quality admired by all.

  3. Building Alliances and Networks:
    A gracious loser who genuinely congratulates the winner can transform a rival into a potential future ally. In coalition politics or corporate mergers, yesterday's opponent can be tomorrow's partner. Burning bridges in defeat is shortsighted; building rapport is wise.

  4. A Reality Check and Motivation:
    Watching someone else win provides a sobering, objective benchmark. It shatters complacency and ignites a powerful, internal motivation to improve. The rise of Indian athletes like Neeraj Chopra or P.V. Sindhu has inspired countless others to train harder, having watched their path to Olympic glory.

When It Is Not Wise: The Exceptions

This wisdom has boundaries. It is unwise if observation becomes an obsession leading to hatred, if it involves learning unethical practices, or if it causes a paralyzing loss of self-belief. The goal is constructive analysis, not destructive comparison.

Relevance to Contemporary India

India today is a competitive arena—from bustling start-up ecosystems and highly competitive exams like the UPSC to vibrant democratic contests. In such an environment, the ability to learn from others' successes is crucial. A student failing an exam can gain by studying the strategy of a topper. A political party can reform its approach by analysing the winning campaign of its opponent, as seen in various state elections where ground strategies are meticulously adopted by losers in subsequent polls.

Cultivating the Wise Response: A Practical Framework

Turning the sting of loss into a learning opportunity requires conscious effort:

  1. Separate Emotion from Analysis:
    Acknowledge the pain of loss, but then consciously shift to a neutral, analytical mode. Ask: What did they do differently? What can I adapt?

  2. Practice Graceful Acknowledgement:
    A simple, sincere congratulation disarms negativity and opens doors. It is a sign of confidence, not weakness.

  3. Document the Lessons:
    Systematically note down the observable factors behind the other's success—preparation, execution, timing, communication.

  4. Focus on Self-Improvement, Not Imitation:
    The goal is not to become a copy, but to integrate valid insights into one's own unique style and strategy.

  5. Maintain Perspective:
    One loss is a chapter, not the whole story. History is replete with figures like Mahatma Gandhi or Abraham Lincoln, who faced repeated defeats but used them as stepping stones, learning from the political successes of their contemporaries to ultimately forge their own path to victory.

Conclusion

Therefore, watching someone else win while we lose is not an act of masochism or surrender. It is an act of strategic patience and intellectual humility. It is the wisdom of the student who remains in the class after failing a test, determined to understand the correct answers. In the long race of life and nation-building, this ability to learn, adapt, and persevere with grace is what separates fleeting competitors from enduring champions. The wise see in another's victory not a reminder of their loss, but a blueprint for their own future triumph.

The Axe with a Wooden Handle: The Paradox of Self-Destructive Loyalty

Introduction

The ancient fable presents a profound paradox: a forest, where the trees, in a tragic act of misjudgment, repeatedly vote for the axe that is destroying them. They are swayed because its handle is made of wood, making it appear as one of their own. This allegory is a powerful lens to examine a recurring human and societal flaw: the tendency of communities to support forces that ultimately undermine their own survival, misled by superficial commonality and clever deception.

Deconstructing the Allegory: The Players and the Ploy

In this story, each element symbolizes a critical aspect of real-world politics and social dynamics.

  • The Forest:
    Represents a community, an ecosystem, or a nation—a collective entity whose strength lies in unity and preservation.

  • The Trees:
    Symbolize the individual members of this collective, the voters, the citizens, whose choices determine their shared fate.

  • The Axe:
    Embodies a destructive force—a political ideology, a leader, or a policy—that thrives by weakening the very system it operates within. Its core nature is to cut and destroy.

  • The Wooden Handle:
    This is the masterstroke of deception. It represents tokenistic identity, false solidarity, and manufactured empathy. The axe does not share the trees' destiny; it merely uses a part of them as a tool to wield greater power over them.

Manifestations in Contemporary India: The Fable Comes Alive

This allegory is not confined to forests; it plays out in various spheres of Indian society.

  1. Identity Politics and Voting Patterns:
    Often, communities vote for leaders or parties that invoke caste, regional, or religious identity (the wooden handle) but whose governance or economic policies ultimately perpetuate their social stagnation or economic deprivation (the cutting edge). The promise of representation masks the reality of poor education, healthcare, and job creation.

  2. Crony Capitalism and Public Policy:
    Large corporations (the axe) can lobby for policies—like environmental clearances or tax breaks—that benefit them but harm the larger public good (the forest). They justify this by highlighting their contribution to GDP and employment (the wooden handle of shared economic growth), even if their actions lead to long-term resource depletion or inequality.

  3. Media and Misinformation:
    Sensationalist media outlets (the axe) can undermine democratic discourse and social harmony for profit or ideology. They convince their audience (the trees) that they are "on their side," speaking truth to power (the wooden handle), while actually deepening polarization and eroding trust in institutions.

  4. The Environmental Paradox:
    As the literal interpretation, communities dependent on forests sometimes support unsustainable extraction for short-term gains, convinced by contractors or middlemen (the axe with a local face) that it is development, only to face ecological ruin later.

Why Do the Trees Keep Voting for the Axe? The Psychology of Deception

The trees are not foolish; they are misled. The axe employs clever strategies:

  • Creating a False "Us vs. Them":
    It convinces the trees that the real danger is from outside—other forests, the wind, the termites—diverting attention from its own blade.

  • Promising Short-Term Gain:
    It argues that a few sacrificial trees will bring prosperity to the rest, or that the cutting will lead to "progress" and "clearing."

  • Emotional Over Rational Appeal:
    It speaks the language of pride, fear, and loyalty, which often overpowers logical assessment of long-term interest.

Consequences: A Shrinking Forest

The inevitable result is a depleted, weakened collective. In societal terms, this translates to eroded democratic norms, deepening inequality, environmental degradation, and loss of social capital. The story warns that when form is mistaken for substance, survival is compromised.

Felling the Deception: Cultivating Collective Wisdom

To break this cycle, the trees—the citizenry—must develop discernment.

  1. Look Beyond the Handle, Judge by the Blade:
    Citizens must evaluate leaders and policies by their long-term outcomes and core actions, not just their symbolic identity or persuasive rhetoric. As Dr. B.R. Ambedkar warned, "In politics, we will be recognizing the principle of one man one vote and one vote one value. In our social and economic life, we shall, by reason of our social and economic structure, continue to deny the principle of one man one value." We must be vigilant against this contradiction.

  2. Strengthen Institutional Roots:
    A robust rule of law, independent judiciary, free press, and active civil society are the natural immune system of the forest. They can expose the true nature of the axe and check its power.

  3. Promote Critical Thinking and Education:
    An educated populace, skilled in questioning and analysis, is less susceptible to manipulation. Education must foster the ability to distinguish between genuine solidarity and tokenistic gestures.

  4. Focus on Collective, Long-Term Interest:
    The discourse must shift from narrow, short-term benefits to the health of the entire ecosystem. Policies must be judged on universal welfare and sustainability.

Conclusion

The fable of the axe and the trees is a timeless cautionary tale for any democracy. It reminds us that the greatest threats often come cleverly disguised as friends. For India, a nation of immense diversity and potential, the path to a thriving future lies in recognizing that true solidarity is based on shared destiny and genuine empowerment, not on superficial sameness. The trees must learn to unite not by the material of the handle, but by the shared goal of a flourishing, enduring forest. Our collective wisdom must lie in voting for the gardener, not the axe.

The Citizenship (Amendment) Act (CAA), 2019: An Analysis of Law, Politics, and Protests

Introduction

The Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019, is one of the most significant and contentious laws passed by the Indian Parliament in recent times. It amended the Citizenship Act of 1955 to provide a special pathway for Indian citizenship to non-Muslim migrants from three neighbouring countries—Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Bangladesh—who entered India on or before December 31, 2014. The law ignited a nationwide debate on India’s constitutional values, secular principles, and humanitarian obligations, making it a defining socio-political issue.

The Core Provisions of the CAA

The CAA introduced a new provision (Section 6B) in the citizenship law. It states that Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan who entered India without proper documents before the end of 2014 shall not be treated as “illegal migrants” and will be eligible for expedited citizenship. The law reduces the residency requirement for naturalization for this specific group from 11 years to 5 years. Crucially, it excludes Muslims from its scope.

The Ruling Party (BJP) Stand and Objectives

The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) championed the law, fulfilling a long-standing promise in its manifestos. The government’s stated objectives were:

  1. Humanitarian Protection:
    To provide refuge to religiously persecuted minorities from the three Islamic republics, arguing that Muslims cannot face religious persecution there.

  2. Rectifying Historical Wrongs:
    To address the perceived injustice of partition and offer a homeland to co-religionists left behind.

  3. Legal Clarity:
    To create a legal framework for long-term residents, many of whom live in limbo.

Home Minister Amit Shah framed it as an act of humanitarian justice, stating it was for those who faced “persecution on religious grounds.”

Public Outcry and Major Criticisms

The Act triggered massive and sustained protests across India, led by students, civil society, and opposition parties. The core criticisms were:

  • Political Dimension:

  • International Dimension:
    It drew criticism from foreign governments and human rights bodies but was also seen domestically as an assertion of sovereign right.

A New Chapter: The Abrogation of Article 370

Introduction

Article 370 of the Indian Constitution was a unique provision that granted the state of Jammu and Kashmir a special autonomous status. On August 5, 2019, the Government of India revoked this Article through a Presidential Order and a parliamentary resolution, fundamentally altering the constitutional relationship between Jammu & Kashmir and the Union of India. This historic decision, accompanied by the bifurcation of the state into two Union Territories—Jammu & Kashmir, and Ladakh—remains one of the most significant and debated political actions in recent Indian history.

Article 370: Features and the Framework of Autonomy

Article 370 was a temporary, transitional provision in Part XXI of the Constitution. Its key features were:

  1. Limited Legislative Power of Parliament:
    The Indian Parliament could only make laws for J&K on subjects mentioned in the Instrument of Accession (Defence, Foreign Affairs, and Communications). For other matters, the state's concurrence was required.

  2. Separate Constitution: Jammu & Kashmir had its own constitution.

  3. Residency and Property Rights:
    It allowed the state legislature to define "permanent residents" with special rights over property ownership and government jobs, barring outsiders from settling or owning land.
    This arrangement was intended to be temporary, as stated in the Article's title. Over decades, through presidential orders, many central laws were extended to the state, diluting its original scope.

The Process of Abrogation and Constitutional Reasoning

The government's move involved several steps. The President issued The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019, which superseded the 1954 order that had defined the relationship. This rendered Article 370 inoperative. A recommendation from the Governor (as the state was under President's Rule) was used in place of the concurrence of the state assembly. Parliament then passed the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019, to bifurcate the state.
The government argued that Article 370 was always meant to be temporary, that it fostered separatism, hindered development, and denied residents rights enjoyed by other Indians. Home Minister Amit Shah stated in Parliament that the move was for "Ek Nishan, Ek Vidhan, Ek Pradhan" (One Symbol, One Constitution, One Head).

Normalisation and Developmental Push Post-2019

The government has since focused on a "normalisation" agenda, claiming significant improvements:

  • Security Situation:
    Claims of a decline in terrorist incidents and stone-pelting, attributed to stricter security measures and the crackdown on separatist networks.

  • Development and Governance:
    A push for infrastructure projects (roads, electricity), new industrial policies to attract investment, and claims of more efficient implementation of central welfare schemes like PM-KISAN, Ayushman Bharat, and Saubhagya.

  • Political Activity:
    After a period of restriction, mainstream political activity has resumed, with panchayat and District Development Council (DDC) elections held successfully. However, mainstream regional leaders faced detentions initially.

Consequences and Critical Perspectives

The move has had multifaceted consequences, viewed through different lenses:

  1. Legal and Constitutional Challenge:
    Petitions challenging the abrogation are pending before the Supreme Court, focusing on the procedural validity of using President's Rule to effect such a permanent change.

  2. Political Integration vs. Alienation:
    Supporters hail it as the final and full integration of J&K with India, removing a symbol of differential treatment. Critics argue it was done without consulting the people of J&K, undermining the principles of federalism and democratic consent, potentially deepening alienation.

  3. Socio-Economic Impact:
    While new investment policies promise growth, concerns remain about demographic change due to the new domicile rules allowing non-residents to settle and get jobs. The unique land rights of the region have been altered.

  4. Human Rights and Civil Liberties:
    The initial period saw severe restrictions on communication, movement, and detention of political leaders. While many restrictions have been eased, the memory of the lockdown impacts the social fabric.

  5. Geopolitical Ramifications:
    It escalated tensions with Pakistan and altered the dynamics of the Kashmir issue in international forums.

The Road Ahead: From Integration to Inclusion

The true test of the abrogation will be in its long-term outcome. The path forward must focus on:

  • Restoring Full Democratic Processes:
    Ensuring early, free, and fair assembly elections to re-establish a robust democratic mandate from the people.

The abrogation of Article 370 is a watershed moment that has redrawn the constitutional map of India. As India moves forward, the focus must shift from the act of abrogation to the quality of its aftermath. However, critics argue it has led to "judicial overreach," where courts encroach on policy-making, a domain of the elected government. To strengthen this vital pillar, urgent reforms are needed:

  3. Case Management and Filling Vacancies:
    Tackling the massive backlog (over 4.8 crore cases pending across all courts) requires filling vacancies swiftly, leveraging technology for efficiency, and alternative dispute resolution mechanisms.

  4. Ethical Code and Discipline:
    A clear, enforceable code of conduct for judges, with a credible in-house mechanism to address complaints, is crucial to maintain the highest ethical standards.

The Indian judiciary stands as a formidable fortress of rights and justice. Its activism has often been democracy's saving grace. The goal must not be to curb its power but to reform its processes, ensuring it is both independent and accountable, strong and transparent. Universal Basic Income vs. Subsidies: The Path to Eradicating Poverty

Introduction

The fight against poverty is central to India's development agenda. Two primary strategies dominate this debate: the traditional subsidy-based approach and the innovative concept of Universal Basic Income (UBI). Subsidies provide targeted support on specific goods (food, fuel, fertilizer), while UBI proposes giving all citizens a regular, unconditional cash transfer. Choosing between them requires examining India's fiscal capacity (tax-to-GDP ratio) and the effectiveness of each in lifting people out of poverty.

The Fiscal Backbone: India's Tax-to-GDP Ratio

The resources available for any welfare scheme depend on the government's revenue. India's tax-to-GDP ratio has hovered around 16-17%, significantly lower than the average for emerging market economies (around 21%). This limits the fiscal space for massive new entitlements like a full-scale UBI. Both subsidies and UBI would need to be funded from this constrained pool, making efficiency in spending paramount. Increasing this ratio through broader tax bases and better compliance is a prerequisite for any enhanced welfare model.

The Subsidy Model: Tangible Support with Leakages

India has a long history of subsidy-based schemes to alleviate poverty, such as the Public Distribution System (PDS) for food grains, LPG subsidy (PMUY), and fertilizer subsidies.

  • How They Eradicate Poverty:
    By making essential goods affordable, they increase the real income of the poor, ensuring basic nutrition and energy security. Schemes like the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) provide a different form of subsidy—a guaranteed wage, creating productive assets.

  • Advantages:
    They address specific, urgent needs (hunger, cooking fuel). When well-targeted (like through the Aadhaar-linked Direct Benefit Transfer), they can reach the intended beneficiaries directly.

  • Challenges:
    Subsidies suffer from significant leakages, inclusion/exclusion errors, and market distortions. They can be inflexible (a person needing medicine gets rice instead) and create a dependency culture without building long-term capacity.

Universal Basic Income (UBI): The Case for Cash and Choice

UBI, as outlined in the 2016-17 Economic Survey, proposes a regular, fixed cash payment to every individual, unconditionally.

  • How It Could Eradicate Poverty:
    By providing a direct income floor, it would immediately reduce absolute poverty. It empowers individuals with choice and dignity, allowing them to spend on their most pressing needs—be it nutrition, health, education, or starting a small business. It could streamline welfare by replacing a maze of subsidies with one efficient transfer.

  • Advantages:
    It minimizes administrative costs and leakages, respects individual agency, and can be a powerful tool for women's empowerment if paid directly to them.

  • Challenges:
    The primary hurdle is fiscal cost. A modest UBI could cost 4-5% of GDP, demanding a major re-prioritization of all existing welfare spending. There are also concerns about potential misuse of funds (on alcohol, gambling) and the risk of inflation if not carefully managed.

This is not a simple either/or choice but a question of design and transition.

The choice between UBI and subsidies is a choice between trusting the citizen with cash and trusting the state with provision. For India, with its limited fiscal space and vast poverty, the optimal path is not a radical swap but a thoughtful evolution. It involves streamlining and digitizing existing subsidies for efficiency while cautiously introducing elements of direct, targeted cash transfers. The goal must be a hybrid model that ensures no one falls below a basic dignity floor while investing in the public goods that enable people to climb out of poverty permanently. As Mahatma Gandhi emphasized, true welfare is about enabling self-reliance. The right mix of UBI and smart subsidies can be a powerful step in that direction.

Conclusion: The Last Stand

The fate of the Aravalis is a test case for India’s commitment to sustainable development. Their extinction would not be a silent geological event but a loud socio-economic catastrophe, leading to more water crises, worse air quality, and increased desertification. As environmentalist Sunderlal Bahuguna reminded us, “Ecology is permanent economy.” By choosing stringent protection, scientific restoration, and smart alternatives, we can ensure that these ancient guardians continue to stand tall, safeguarding our present and our future. The time to act is now, before the last hill is reduced to dust.

Corruption: The Catalyst Crippling India’s Governance and Justice

Corruption in India is often seen as a mere obstacle, but its real danger lies in how it acts as a catalyst—speeding up decay, deepening dysfunction, and poisoning the very systems of governance and justice. Together, they create a cycle where corruption fuels more corruption, undermining democracy and development.

Catalyst in Government Machinery: Greasing the Wheels of Dysfunction

In the administrative system, corruption does not just slow things down—it alters outcomes. It works as a catalyst in several ways:

  1. Speed Money for Basic Services:
    Citizens often pay bribes to "speed up" work—getting a ration card, a land record, or a police report. This normalises corruption as essential for even rightful services.

  2. Policy Manipulation:
    Large-scale corruption in projects like the 2G spectrum or coal block allocations showed how rules could be bent to benefit private players, with politicians and bureaucrats acting as catalysts for illicit gains.

  3. Erosion of Trust:
    When corruption becomes routine, public faith in government declines. People start believing that nothing works without bribes. As Mahatma Gandhi warned, "The spirit of democracy cannot be imposed from without. It has to come from within." Corruption destroys that spirit from within.

A 2023 survey by Transparency International noted that nearly 50% of Indians paid bribes for public services. This is not just about money; it is about corruption catalysing a culture of lawlessness.

Catalyst in Judiciary: When Justice is Delayed and Denied

In the judiciary, corruption is even more dangerous because it attacks the last resort for justice.

  1. Case Management Manipulation:
    Corruption can influence which judge hears a case, cause deliberate delays, or affect outcomes. This perverts the course of justice.

  2. Undermining Fairness: When people believe judgments can be bought, they lose faith in the legal system. They may resort to extra-legal means, catalyzing further lawlessness.

  3. Weakening the Rule of Law: Dr. B.R. Ambedkar said, "The Constitution is not a mere lawyer's document; it is a vehicle of life." Corruption in the judiciary stalls this vehicle, denying life to constitutional values.

The Vicious Cycle: How One Feeds the Other

The catalytic effect becomes cyclical:

  • A corrupt official delays a file → the citizen approaches the court → if corruption exists there too, justice is denied → public trust in both institutions falls → corruption increases further.
    This cycle reinforces the idea that the system is broken, and only money or influence can fix it.

Breaking the Cycle: Practical Solutions

To neutralize this catalyst, we need systemic reforms:

  1. Transparency through Technology:

    • Expand online service delivery (like e-governance portals) to reduce human contact in government services.

    • Implement e-courts and random, automated case allocation to minimize manipulation.

  2. Strengthening Accountability:

    • Enforce the Right to Information Act robustly and protect whistleblowers.

    • Set up independent complaint authorities for both bureaucracy and judiciary.

  3. Speeding Up Justice:

    • Fill judicial vacancies urgently and promote Alternative Dispute Resolution to reduce case backlog.

  4. Political and Administrative Will:

  5. Public Awareness and Participation:

    • Citizens must reject petty corruption and use tools like social audits to demand accountability.

Conclusion: From Catalyst to Cure

Corruption as a catalyst has deeply damaged India’s governance and judiciary, but it is not irreversible. By using technology to bring transparency, ensuring strict accountability, and fostering a culture of integrity, we can turn the tide. The goal must be to transform corruption from a catalyst of decay into a remembered relic of the past. Only then can India’s government machinery run for the public, and its judiciary stand as a pillar of truth and justice.

The Inner Landscape of Modern India: Five Psychosocial Toxins and Their Antidotes

Introduction

In the relentless pursuit of economic growth and societal progress, contemporary India faces a silent, internal epidemic. Beyond the visible challenges lie corrosive mental habits—Chronic Stress, Anxiety, Overthinking, Maladaptive Day-Dreaming, and Hopelessness—that are eroding the nation’s psychological and physiological vitality. As Swami Vivekananda cautioned, “The greatest sin is to think yourself weak.” These traits, often normalised or overlooked, constitute a formidable barrier to realising individual and collective potential, demanding urgent awareness and practical redressal.

1. Chronic Stress: The Pervasive Slow Burn

Chronic stress is the body’s emergency response system stuck in perpetual ‘on’ mode. In today’s India, it is fuelled by hyper-competition, urban chaos, job insecurity, and academic pressure. Psychologically, it manifests as constant irritability, mental exhaustion, and a feeling of being overwhelmed. Physiologically, it triggers a flood of cortisol and adrenaline. This leads to allostatic load—the wear and tear on the body. It raises blood pressure, increasing risks of hypertension and heart disease, which account for over 28% of all deaths in India according to the Global Burden of Disease Study. It weakens immunity, disrupts digestion (aggravating conditions like IBS), and impairs sleep.

Remedy: Cultivating Sattva.
The solution lies in deliberate regulation. As the Bhagavad Gita advocates, “Yoga is the journey of the self, through the self, to the self.” Incorporating daily pranayama (anulom vilom) and mindful movement like yoga directly calms the nervous system. Companies must adopt ‘right to disconnect’ policies, mirroring global trends. Schools should integrate mindfulness periods, not just academic drills, to build resilience from a young age.

2. Anxiety: The Fortress of Fear

Anxiety is chronic worry about future uncertainties. It is rampant among India’s youth facing an uncertain job market and among adults navigating rapid social change. It creates a mind perpetually braced for threat, leading to avoidance, restlessness, and social withdrawal. Physically, it causes muscle tension (headaches, back pain), a racing heart, and digestive issues. It keeps the sympathetic nervous system hyperactive, exhausting the body’s resources.

Remedy: Grounding in the Present.
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) techniques are highly effective but need wider dissemination through affordable tele-counselling platforms, a gap the National Tele-Mental Health Programme (NTMHP) aims to fill. On a personal level, the simple practice of grounding—noting 5 things you can see, 4 you can feel—can halt a panic spiral. Community support groups, inspired by traditional satsang models, can provide a powerful sense of shared experience and reduce stigma.

3. Overthinking: The Paralysis of Analysis

Overthinking, or rumination, is the mental hamster wheel—replaying past events or worrying about decisions without conclusion. In a culture with high stakes in career and familial choices, it is endemic. It paralyzes decision-making, fuels regret, and is a core driver of depression. Physiologically, this relentless mental noise sustains low-grade stress, disrupts sleep architecture, and fatigues the prefrontal cortex, the brain’s decision-making centre.

Remedy: From Thought to Action.
The antidote is decisive action. As the former President, Dr. A.P.J. While constructive daydreaming fosters creativity, its maladaptive form is an excessive, compulsive retreat into fantasy to avoid real-life challenges. It is the bedrock of severe depression and a major risk factor for suicide—a grave concern in India, which reports high numbers of suicides among young adults. Physiologically, it is linked to increased inflammation, weakened immunity, and worse outcomes in chronic illnesses. It represents a shutdown of the body’s motivational systems.

Hope is built through small, tangible wins. The concept of ‘Seva’ (selfless service), championed by leaders like Mahatma Gandhi, provides profound antidote. Volunteering at a local NGO or community clean-up fosters connection and purpose. On a personal level, practicing gratitude—noting three small things one is thankful for each day—scientifically shifts focus from deficit to abundance.

The journey from these internal toxins to holistic health is not merely individual but societal. It requires a shift in our national ethos—from glorifying relentless burnout to valuing sustainable well-being; from stigmatising mental struggle to normalising support. As Chanakya noted in the Arthashastra, “The root of happiness is health.” A true ‘Viksit Bharat’ (Developed India) must be built on the foundation of a ‘Swastha Bharat’, where minds are resilient, focused, and hopeful. By integrating timeless wisdom with contemporary science, and by fostering environments—in homes, schools, and workplaces—that promote psychological flexibility over rigid perfection, India can transform its inner landscape. This will unleash the boundless potential of its greatest resource: the mindful, healthy, and empowered citizen.
