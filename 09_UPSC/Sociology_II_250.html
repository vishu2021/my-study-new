(i) Perspectives on Study of Indian Society

The study of Indian society is approached through various perspectives that help in understanding its complexities and nuances.
Some of the major perspectives on the study of Indian society are:

  1. Conflict theory:
    • This perspective emphasizes the role of power and inequality in shaping social relations and structures.
    • It highlights the struggles between different groups for access to resources and the maintenance of power.
    • For example:
      • the tension between the dominant upper castes and lower castes can be studied through this perspective, where the former holds more power and resources and therefore, dominates the latter.
  2. Feminism:
    • This perspective focuses on the study of gender and patriarchy in society.
    • It highlights the inequalities and discrimination faced by women and the need for gender equality.
    • For example:
      • the study of dowry and female infanticide in India can be studied through this perspective, which emphasizes the patriarchal nature of Indian society.
  3. Globalization:
    • This perspective focuses on the study of the impact of globalization on the social, cultural, and economic aspects of society.
    • It highlights the interconnectedness of different societies and the need to understand the global context of social change.
    • For example:
      • the study of the impact of globalization on the Indian economy and culture can be studied through this perspective.
  4. Postcolonialism:
    • This perspective focuses on the study of the impact of colonialism and imperialism on the social, cultural, and economic aspects of society.
    • It highlights the continued effects of colonialism and the need to decolonize the mind and society.
    • For example:
      • the study of the impact of British colonialism on Indian society and its legacy can be studied through this perspective.
  5. Structural-Functionalism:
    • This perspective views society as a system composed of various interconnected parts, each performing a specific function to maintain social order and stability.
    • It focuses on the role of institutions and social structures in promoting social cohesion and harmony.
    • For example:
      • the caste system in India can be studied through this perspective, where each caste performs a specific function and contributes to the overall functioning of society.

These perspectives provide different ways of understanding and analyzing Indian society, and each has its strengths and limitations.
They also provide a framework for studying different aspects of society, such as religion, education, politics, and economy.

 

(a) Indology (G.S. Ghure)

Indology is an approach to the study of Indian society and culture that emerged during the colonial period, particularly during the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
G.S. Ghurye (Govind Sadashiv Ghurye - 1893 - 1983), a renowned sociologist, is known for his contributions to Indology.
Some of the key features of Indology as propounded by Ghurye are:

  1. Caste System:
    • Ghurye emphasized the importance of the caste system in Indian society, and argued that it is a fundamental social institution that shapes all aspects of Indian life, from marriage to occupation to religious practices.
  2. Continuity and Change:
    • Ghurye emphasized the idea that Indian society is both static and dynamic.
    • On one hand, he argued that the caste system and other traditional institutions have remained relatively unchanged for centuries.
    • On the other hand, he acknowledged that Indian society has undergone significant changes over time, particularly in response to colonialism and modernization.
  3. Ideal Types:
    • Ghurye used the concept of 'ideal types' to describe the typical characteristics of different caste groups in Indian society.
    • For example:
      • He described the Brahmins as being intellectual and spiritual, while the Kshatriyas were seen as being warriors and administrators.
  4. Sanskritization:
    • Ghurye coined the term 'Sanskritization' to describe the process by which lower castes and tribes adopt the customs, beliefs, and practices of the upper castes in order to gain social mobility.
    • For example:
      • A lower-caste individual might adopt vegetarianism, teetotalism, and other upper-caste customs in order to elevate their social status.

Overall, Ghurye's Indological approach helped to establish the study of Indian society as a distinct field of inquiry, and his work continues to influence sociologists and anthropologists today.

 

(b) Structural functionalism (M. N. Srinivas)

M.N. Srinivas (Mysore Narasimhachar Srinivas - 1916 - 1999), a renowned Indian sociologist, is credited with developing the theory of "structural functionalism" as a way to study Indian society.
Some of the key points of his theory and examples are:

  1. Caste System:
    • Srinivas argued that caste was not only a social institution but also a structural and functional system in Indian society.
    • He suggested that the caste system was a way of ensuring that people perform their roles and responsibilities in society.
  2. Dominant Caste:
    • Srinivas also introduced the concept of the "dominant caste" in Indian society.
    • He argued that in every village or region, there is a particular caste group that has more power, wealth, and status than others.
  3. Joint Family System:
    • Srinivas argued that the joint family system was another important feature of Indian society.
    • He suggested that the joint family system was a way of ensuring the continuity of family values, traditions, and social norms.
  4. Rural-Urban Dichotomy:
    • Srinivas also explored the rural-urban dichotomy in Indian society.
    • He argued that the rural areas were characterized by traditional values, customs, and practices, while urban areas were more influenced by modernization and westernization.
  5. Sanskritization:
    • Srinivas introduced the concept of "Sanskritization" to explain the process by which lower castes try to emulate the customs, rituals, and practices of the upper castes to gain higher social status.

Overall, Srinivas's theory of structural functionalism was an attempt to understand the complexities of Indian society and its cultural diversity.

    • He argued that the state is an instrument of the ruling class and that politics is dominated by the interests of the capitalist class.
  4. Dependency theory:

  5. Capitalism and imperialism:
    • Desai's work emphasized the links between capitalism and imperialism in understanding Indian society.
    • He argued that the development of capitalism in India was shaped by the interests of imperialism, which exploited India for its resources and labor.

Examples of A.R. Desai's work include his book "Rural Sociology in India" which focused on the study of agrarian relations, and his book "Peasant Struggles in India" which analyzed the dynamics of peasant movements in India.

 

(ii) Impact of colonial rule on Indian society

The impact of colonial rule on Indian society was significant and multifaceted.
Here are some of the key points and examples:

  1. Economic Exploitation:
    • The British used India's resources for their own benefit, leading to economic exploitation.
    • For example:
      • raw materials were extracted from India to fuel Britain's industrial revolution, resulting in deindustrialization and unemployment in India.
  2. Land Revenue System:
    • The British introduced the land revenue system which was exploitative and led to the impoverishment of peasants.
    • The revenue demand was fixed on the basis of potential, and it was beyond the capacity of the cultivators to pay it.
  3. Changes in Caste System:
    • The British introduced modern education and a new legal system, which challenged the caste system and led to the rise of new social classes.
    • For example:
      • educated Dalits (formerly known as untouchables) and lower-caste groups challenged the Brahminical hegemony and demanded social and economic rights.
  4. Impact on Women:
    • British rule had a mixed impact on women's status.
    • On the one hand, the British introduced laws banning practices such as sati and child marriage, which were harmful to women.
    • On the other hand, the British educational system reinforced patriarchal values and limited women's access to education and public spaces.
  5. Political Centralization:
    • The British imposed a centralized political system, which weakened local institutions of governance and led to the concentration of power at the top.
    • This had long-lasting effects on Indian democracy and governance.
  6. Modernization:
    • The British introduced modern institutions such as railways, postal services, and telegraph, which contributed to the modernization of Indian society.
    • However, these institutions were also used for the colonial exploitation of India.

Overall, colonialism had a deep impact on Indian society, leading to changes in the economy, social structure, gender relations, politics, and culture.

B. Social Structure :

Social Structure

Social structure refers to the organized patterns of social relationships and social institutions that constitute a society.
It provides the framework for the organization of social life, and shapes the opportunities and constraints that individuals experience.
Here are some key points about social structure with examples:

  1. Social stratification:
    • Social stratification refers to the hierarchical arrangement of social groups based on factors such as wealth, income, occupation, education, and social status.
    • In India, the caste system is an example of social stratification.
  2. Social institutions:
    • Social institutions are structured sets of norms and rules that guide behavior in specific areas of social life.
    • Examples include the family, education system, religious institutions, and the economy.
  3. Social networks:
    • Social networks are the web of relationships and connections among individuals and groups.
    • These networks can be formal or informal, and can have significant impacts on individual opportunities and social mobility.
  4. Gender roles:
    • Gender roles are the set of expectations and behaviors associated with being male or female in a given society.
    • In many traditional societies, men and women have distinct gender roles that shape their experiences and opportunities.
  5. Ethnicity and race:
    • Ethnicity refers to the shared cultural identity of a group of people, while race refers to biological characteristics that are socially constructed and assigned meaning.
    • Both ethnicity and race can shape social structure, as groups may experience discrimination and exclusion based on these factors.
  6. Power and authority:
    • Power and authority are central features of social structure, as they shape the distribution of resources and opportunities within society.
    • Examples of sources of power and authority include political leaders, economic elites, and religious figures.

Overall, social structure is a crucial concept for understanding how societies are organized, and how they shape individual experiences and opportunities.

(b) Agrarian social structure — evolution of land tenure system, land reforms

The agrarian social structure in India has undergone several changes over time, particularly in relation to land tenure systems and land reforms.
Here are some key points on the evolution of the land tenure system and land reforms in India:

  1. Pre-colonial period:
    • The land tenure system in India during the pre-colonial period was characterized by a complex web of relationships between the rulers, the zamindars (landlords), and the peasants.
    • The zamindars were granted land rights by the rulers, and they, in turn, extracted rent from the peasants who worked on the land.
    • In the pre-colonial period, land was owned by the king or the ruler.
    • The land was cultivated by peasants, who paid a share of their produce as revenue to the king.
    • The revenue system was known as the 'ryotwari' system.
  2. Colonial period:
    • The colonial period saw significant changes in the land tenure system in India.
    • The British introduced the Permanent Settlement Act in 1793, which granted zamindars permanent rights over the land in exchange for fixed annual payments to the British.
    • This led to the concentration of landownership in the hands of a few zamindars, who often exploited the peasants by charging high rents and taxes.

 

Independence and land reforms:

After independence, land reforms became a key policy priority for the Indian government.
The first major land reform legislation was the Zamindari Abolition Act of 1950, which abolished the zamindari system and transferred ownership of the land to the tenants who worked on it.
This was followed by several other land reform measures, such as the Land Ceiling Act, which placed a limit on the amount of land that could be owned by an individual, and the Tenancy Acts, which gave security of tenure to tenants and regulated rents.

 

Land reforms:

After India gained independence, the government introduced various land reforms to improve the condition of the peasants.
The aim was to abolish intermediaries like zamindars and provide land to the landless and marginal farmers.
Some of the important land reform acts are:

  1. Land Acquisition Act (1894):
    • This act allowed the government to acquire land from private owners for public purposes.
  2. Zamindari Abolition Act (1950):
    • This act abolished the zamindari system and transferred the ownership of land to the tenants.
  3. Tenancy Acts (various states):
    • These acts protected the rights of tenants and regulated the rent that landlords could charge.
  4. Ceiling on Land Holdings Act (1961):
    • This act fixed a limit on the amount of land that a person could own.
  5. Land Acquisition Act (2013):
    • In 2013, the government introduced a new Land Acquisition Act, which made it mandatory to obtain the consent of farmers before acquiring their land and provide compensation at market rates.

 

Impact of land reforms:

  1. The impact of land reforms on the agrarian social structure in India has been mixed.
  2. While land reforms have helped to reduce the concentration of landownership and improve the status of tenants, they have also faced significant implementation challenges and resistance from powerful landowners.

  2. Low barriers to entry:

  4. Limited job security:

Examples of informal sector activities:

Ageing

As societies experience a decrease in fertility rates and an increase in life expectancy, the proportion of elderly people in the population increases, resulting in an aging population.
This demographic shift has significant economic, social, and political consequences.
The following are some aspects related to the emerging issue of ageing:

  1. Demographic Changes:
    • Aging population is a result of demographic changes, including a decrease in fertility rates and an increase in life expectancy.
  2. Economic Impact:
    • As the population ages, the workforce shrinks, and the number of dependents increases, which can put pressure on the economy and government budgets.
    • The cost of providing healthcare, pensions, and social welfare to the elderly also increases.
  3. Social Impact:
    • The aging population may lead to changes in family structures, where the number of people living alone increases, and there is an increase in demand for care services.
    • The elderly may also experience social isolation and a decline in social status.
  4. Health and Healthcare:
    • Older people are more susceptible to health problems and may require more healthcare services, including long-term care.
    • The aging population may also lead to an increase in chronic illnesses and age-related diseases.
  5. Policy Responses:
    • Governments need to develop policies and programs to address the challenges of an aging population, including health and social care, pension provision, and intergenerational equity.
    • These policies may include encouraging later retirement ages, promoting healthy aging, and investing in social care.
    • Examples:
      • Japan has the highest proportion of elderly citizens in the world, which has led to a declining workforce and increased pressure on social services and healthcare.
      • In Europe, the aging population has led to reforms in pension systems, such as increasing the retirement age and encouraging people to save for their retirement.
      • In India, the aging population is expected to triple by 2050, which will put pressure on the healthcare system and require the government to develop policies to address the needs of the elderly.

Child & Infant Mortality

Child and infant mortality are two related but distinct concepts that refer to the death of children and infants before they reach a certain age.
Here are some aspects related to these topics, along with examples:

Child Mortality:

  1. Definition:

    • Child mortality refers to the death of children under the age of 5.

  2. Causes:

    • The main causes of child mortality are preventable diseases such as pneumonia, diarrhea, malaria, measles, and HIV/AIDS. Malnutrition is also a major contributor to child mortality.

  3. Global Statistics:

    • According to the World Health Organization (WHO), 5.2 million children under the age of 5 died in 2019. Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia account for more than 75% of all child deaths globally.

  4. Prevention:

    • Child mortality can be prevented through a range of interventions, including immunization, improved nutrition, access to clean water and sanitation, and early treatment of illnesses.

    • Example:

      • In Nigeria, the under-5 mortality rate was 117 deaths per 1,000 live births in 2018.

      • To combat this high rate, the Nigerian government has implemented several strategies, including the introduction of routine immunization programs, increasing access to healthcare facilities, and promoting better nutrition practices.

 

Infant Mortality:

  1. Definition:

    • Infant mortality refers to the death of infants under the age of 1.

  2. Causes:

    • The main causes of infant mortality are similar to those of child mortality, with the addition of congenital anomalies and preterm birth complications.

  3. Global Statistics:

    • According to the WHO, 2.4 million infants died in 2019. Again, Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia account for the majority of infant deaths globally.

  4. Prevention:

    • Like child mortality, infant mortality can be prevented through interventions such as immunization, improved nutrition, access to clean water and sanitation, and early treatment of illnesses.

    • In addition, prenatal care and skilled attendance during childbirth can reduce the risk of infant mortality.

    • Example:

      • In the United States, the infant mortality rate was 5.6 deaths per 1,000 live births in 2019.

      • To address this issue, the government has implemented initiatives to improve access to healthcare, reduce maternal smoking, and promote safe sleep practices for infants.

    • Discrimination based on caste is pervasive and affects many aspects of life, including education, employment, and access to public services.
