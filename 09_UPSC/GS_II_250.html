1. Indian Constitution

Historical Underpinnings:

CoI was adopted on January 26, 1950, and is the Supreme Law of the Land.
The Historical Underpinnings of CoI are shaped by several factors that influenced the drafting and implementation of the Constitution.
Here are some key factors of the historical underpinnings of CoI:

  1. British Colonial Rule:
    • BCR in India had a significant impact on CoI.
    • The British introduced a constitutional framework that provided for Limited Self-Government in India.
    • This framework served as the foundation for CoI.
  2. Indian Independence Movement:
    • was a major factor that influenced CoI.
    • The leaders of the Independence Movement, including Mahatma Gandhi, Jawaharlal Nehru and B.R. Ambedkar, played a crucial role in the drafting of the Constitution.
  3. Government of India Act, 1935:
    • was a constitutional reform passed by the British Parliament that introduced several reforms in the Governance of India.
    • provided for the establishment of a federal system of government  in India and served as the basis for CoI.
  4. Constituent Assembly:
    • was formed in 1946 to draft CoI.
    • was composed of representatives from all the provinces and princely states of India, and it worked for three years to draft the Constitution.
  5. Preamble:
    • an introductory statement that means the introductory part of a constitution that usually states the reasons for and intent of the law.
    • outlines the fundamental values and principles that the Constitution embodies.
    • declares India to be a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic and Republic Nation.
    • based on the 'Objectives Resolution', drafted and moved by Pandit Nehru, and adopted by the Constituent Assembly.
    • The Objective Resolution stated that the citizens of India must be guaranteed justice, freedom and equality.
    • is called the preface of Indian Constitution because it contains the philosophy on which the entire constitution is built.
  6. Fundamental Rights:
    • CoI guarantees several fundamental rights to its citizens, such as
    • the right to equality,
    • the right to freedom of speech and expression,
    • the right to freedom of religion, and
    • the right to life and liberty.
    • were inspired by the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and were included to ensure that the citizens of India are protected from any form of discrimination or oppression.
  7. Universal Declaration of Human Rights:
    • This is an international document adopted by the United Nations General Assembly that enshrines the rights and freedoms of all human beings.
    • UDHR establishes the equality and dignity of every human being and stipulates that every Government has a core duty to enable all people to enjoy all their inalienable rights and freedoms.
    • All of us have a right to speak freely and participate in decisions that affect our lives.
  8. Federal Structure:
    • CoI provides for a federal structure of government, where power is divided between the central government and the state governments.
    • was inspired by the American Constitution and was included to ensure that the diverse regions of India are represented in the governance of the country.
    • aims to balance the distribution of power and provide autonomy to the states while maintaining the unity and integrity of the country.
    • reflects the diverse nature of India and allows for the accommodation of regional aspirations and identities within a larger national framework.
  9. Directive Principles of State Policy:
    • CoI includes several Directive Principles of State Policy, which are guidelines for the government to follow in the governance of the country.
    • are based on the ideals of social justice and economic equality and serve as a blueprint for the development of the country.
    • outlined in Part IV.
    • are not enforceable by courts but are fundamental in the governance of the country.
    • serve as a roadmap for the state to pursue socio-economic development, promote social justice and improve the overall well-being of the citizens.
  10. Universal Adult Franchise:
    • CoI introduced Universal Adult Franchise, which gave every adult citizen of India the right to vote.
    • was based on the principle of democracy and was a major departure from the colonial era, where only a small minority of the population had the right to vote.
    • is a cornerstone of India's democracy, ensuring that every adult citizen has the right to participate in the electoral process and contribute to the selection of representatives who govern the country.
    • a fundamental right that empowers citizens to shape the direction and policies of the nation.

Overall, the historical underpinnings of CoI reflect the aspirations and struggles of the Indian people for freedom, democracy and social justice.

Key Events led to Historical Underpinnings of CoI:

The historical underpinnings of the Constitution of India were influenced by several key events that shaped India's political and constitutional landscape.
Here are the key events that led to the historical underpinnings of the Constitution of India:

  1. Government of India Act (1858):
    • The British Crown took over the administration of India from the East India Company through the Government of India Act, 1858.
    • This act marked the beginning of direct British rule in India and led to centralization of power in the hands of the British government.
  2. Indian National Congress and Early Demands (1885-1906):
    • The formation of the Indian National Congress in 1885 marked the beginning of the organized political movement in India.
    • The Congress initially demanded representation and reforms within the existing colonial framework.
    • This period saw the emergence of leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji, Gopal Krishna Gokhale and Bal Gangadhar Tilak, who advocated for Indian self-rule.
  3. Partition of Bengal and Swadeshi Movement (1905-1908):
    • The British decision to partition Bengal in 1905 ignited widespread protests and led to the Swadeshi Movement, a movement advocating for the use of Indian-made products and boycotting British goods.
    • The movement united Indians across regions and communities and fueled a sense of nationalism and self-reliance.
    • Dadabhai Naoroji, Gopal Krishna Gokhale, Mahadev Govind Ranade, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Ganesh Vyankatesh Joshi and Bhaswat K. Nigoni began organizing Swadeshi Movement to promote Indian nationalism.
  4. Morley-Minto Reforms (1909):
    • The Morley-Minto Reforms (also known as Indian Councils Act 1909) introduced limited electoral representation for Indians in the legislative councils.
    • Though a conservative measure, it marked the first instance of Indians having a share in the legislative process, leading to greater political awareness and demands for self-governance.
  5. Lucknow Pact (1916):
    • The Lucknow Pact between the Indian National Congress and the Muslim League marked a significant development in India's nationalist movement.
    • It aimed to bridge the gap between Hindus and Muslims by demanding constitutional reforms, including increased representation for Indians in legislatures.
  6. Home Rule Movement (1916-1918):
    • The Home Rule Movement, initiated by Annie Besant and Bal Gangadhar Tilak, aimed to demand self-government and political reforms within the British colonial framework.
    • The movement brought together different sections of society and contributed to the growth of nationalist sentiment.
  7. Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (1919):
    • The Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms introduced some democratic elements, including the introduction of dyarchy (dual governance) in the provinces and the establishment of a bicameral legislature.
    • However, it fell short of Indian aspirations for self-rule, leading to widespread discontent and protests.
  8. Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (1919):
    • The Jallianwala Bagh massacre, where British troops fired upon a peaceful gathering in Amritsar, resulted in widespread condemnation and marked a turning point in India's struggle for independence.
    • This event intensified the demand for self-rule and exposed the repressive nature of British rule.
  9. Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-1922):
    • The Non-Cooperation Movement, led by Mahatma Gandhi, called for boycotting British institutions, including educational institutions, law courts and the civil services.
    • It emphasized the principles of non-violence and civil disobedience and mobilized large sections of the Indian population against British rule.
  10. Simon Commission (1927):
    • The appointment of the Simon Commission, a British parliamentary committee, to propose further constitutional reforms for India, led to widespread protests.
    • Indians demanded that the commission include Indian members, which exposed the flaws in the colonial system and united Indians in their demands for self-rule.
  11. Round Table Conferences (1930-1932):
    • The British government convened a series of Round Table Conferences to discuss constitutional reforms for India.
    • While the conferences failed to produce a consensus, they highlighted the need for greater Indian representation and led to the recognition of communal electorates.
  12. Salt March and Civil Disobedience Movement (1930-1934):
    • The Salt March, led by Mahatma Gandhi, was a pivotal event in the Civil Disobedience Movement.
    • Gandhi and his followers marched to the Arabian Sea coast to produce salt in defiance of the British salt monopoly.
    • This act of civil disobedience inspired mass protests across the country and highlighted the demand for full independence.
  13. Government of India Act (1935):
    • The Government of India Act, 1935, was a significant piece of legislation enacted by the British government that had a profound impact on the constitutional development of India.
    • This introduced Federal Structure, Provincial Autonomy, Diarchy, Limited Franchise, Separate Electorates, Central Government Powers, Provincial Governors and Judicial Reforms.
  14. August Offer (1940):
    • The August Offer, presented by the British government during World War II, promised greater self-governance and a future Indian constitution.
    • However, it fell short of Indian expectations for complete independence, leading to the rejection of the offer and increased demands for freedom.
  15. Quit India Movement (1942):
    • The Quit India Movement, launched by the Indian National Congress under the leadership of Mahatma Gandhi, called for an immediate end to British rule in India.
    • The movement witnessed widespread participation and civil disobedience, leading to mass arrests and repression by the British authorities.
  16. Constituent Assembly and Drafting Process (1946-1949):
    • The Constituent Assembly of India, formed in 1946, was entrusted with the task of drafting the Constitution.
    • It included representatives from various regions and communities and engaged in extensive debates and discussions on the provisions of the Constitution.
    • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar chaired the drafting committee and played a significant role in framing the Constitution.
  17. Mountbatten Plan and Indian Independence (1947):
    • Lord Mountbatten, the last Viceroy of India, proposed a plan for the partition of India into two separate nations, India and Pakistan, along religious lines.
    • This partition led to mass communal violence and the displacement of millions of people. India gained independence from British rule on August 15, 1947.
  18. Adoption of the Constitution (January 26, 1950):
    • The Constitution of India was adopted by the Constituent Assembly on January 26, 1950, marking the official establishment of the Republic of India.

These key events collectively shaped the political landscape of India, nurtured the desire for self-rule, and influenced the framing of the Constitution of India.
They reflected the aspirations, struggles, and demands of the Indian people, laying the foundation for a democratic and inclusive constitutional framework.

Evolution of CoI:

CoI has evolved over time through various amendments, judicial interpretations and societal changes.
Here are some of the major events that shaped the evolution of CoI:

  1. Adoption of the Constitution:
    • CoI was adopted on January 26, 1950, and it provided a framework for governance based on democracy, socialism, secularism and federalism.
    • It has since undergone several amendments and interpretations.
  2. First Amendment:
    • The First Amendment to the Constitution was passed in 1951, and it introduced several changes to the fundamental rights provisions of the Constitution.
    • It added reasonable restrictions on freedom of speech and expression, introduced restrictions on property rights and added a new right to property.
  3. Emergency Provisions:
    • CoI provides for emergency provisions that can be invoked during times of war or internal strife.
    • These provisions were used during the India-China War of 1962 and the India-Pakistan War of 1971.
  4. Judicial Activism:
    • The judiciary has played a crucial role in shaping the evolution of CoI.
    • The Supreme Court of India has interpreted the Constitution in several landmark cases, such as Kesavananda Bharati vs State of Kerala (1973), which upheld the basic structure doctrine of the Constitution.
  5. Constitutional Amendments:
    • CoI has been amended several times to reflect changing societal norms and political realities.
    • Some of the major amendments include:
      • the 42nd Amendment (1976), which introduced the term "secular" in the Preamble, and
      • the 73rd and 74th Amendments (1992), which introduced the concept of Panchayati Raj and urban local bodies, respectively.
  6. Women's Rights:
    • CoI has evolved to include provisions for women's rights.
    • The 73rd and 74th Amendments (1992) provided for reservation of seats for women in local bodies, and
    • the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act of 2005 gave equal inheritance rights to daughters in Hindu families.
  7. LGBTQ+ Rights:
    • CoI has evolved to include provisions for LGBTQ+ rights.
    • The Supreme Court of India decriminalized homosexuality in 2018, and
    • the Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act of 2019 provided legal recognition to transgender persons.
  8. Right to Information:
    • RTI Act of 2005 gave citizens the right to access information from public authorities, which was a significant step towards transparency and accountability in governance.

Overall, the evolution of CoI reflects the changing societal norms, political realities and judicial interpretations over time.
The Constitution has adapted to these changes, and its provisions continue to serve as a framework for democratic governance in India.

Features of CoI:

CoI has several distinctive features that make it unique compared to other constitutions in the world.
Here are some of the key features of CoI:

  1. Written Constitution:
    • CoI is a written constitution, which means that it is a codified set of laws that are documented and can be referred to by anyone.
    • This ensures that the law is uniform and consistent throughout the country.
  2. Federal System:
    • CoI is based on a federal system of government, where power is divided between the central government and the state governments.
    • The distribution of power between the two levels of government is clearly defined in the Constitution.
  3. Parliamentary System:
    • CoI follows the parliamentary system of government, where the executive is responsible to the legislature.
    • The Prime Minister and other ministers are members of the Parliament and are accountable to it.
  4. Fundamental Rights:
    • CoI guarantees fundamental rights to all citizens, which include the
    • right to freedom of speech and expression,
    • right to equality,
    • right to life and liberty,
    • right against exploitation, and
    • right to religion.
  5. Directive Principles of State Policy:
    • CoI also lays down the Directive Principles of State Policy,which  are guidelines for the government to follow in order to achieve social and economic justice.
    • Although these principles are not enforceable by courts, they are still considered an important part of the Constitution.
  6. Independent Judiciary:
    • CoI provides for an independent judiciary, which is responsible for interpreting the Constitution and ensuring that the laws are in line with it.
    • The Supreme Court of India is the highest court in the country and has the power of judicial review.
  7. Single Citizenship:
    • CoI provides for single citizenship, which means that all citizens of India are treated equally regardless of which state they belong to.
  8. Secularism:
    • CoI guarantees secularism, which means that the state does not promote any particular religion and is neutral towards all religions.
    • This is reflected in the Preamble, which states that India is a "secular" country.
  9. Reservation:
    • CoI provides for reservation of seats in government jobs and educational institutions for certain sections of society, such as
    • Scheduled Castes,
    • Scheduled Tribes, and
    • Other Backward Classes.
    • This is done to promote social justice and to ensure that these sections of society have access to opportunities.

Overall, the features of CoI reflect the country's commitment to democracy, equality, and social justice.
The Constitution provides a framework for governance that ensures that power is distributed among different levels of government, and that the rights and interests of all citizens are protected.

Amendments of CoI:

CoI has been amended several times since its adoption in 1950.
The amendments have been made to reflect changing societal norms, political realities, and judicial interpretations.
Here are some of the major amendments of CoI:

  1. 1st (1951):
    • The First Amendment introduced several changes to the fundamental rights provisions of the Constitution.
    • added reasonable restrictions on freedom of speech and expression, introduced restrictions on property rights, and added a new right to property.
  2. 7th (1956):
    • reorganized the states on a linguistic basis.
    • also provided for the creation of new states and allowed the Parliament to alter the boundaries of existing states.
  3. 24th (1971):
    • removed the right to property as a fundamental right and made it a legal right instead.
    • also added a new article to the Constitution that allowed the Parliament to amend any part of the Constitution.
  4. 42nd (1976):
    • introduced several changes to the Constitution, including the insertion of the term "secular" in the Preamble.
    • also gave more power to the central government, restricted the power of the judiciary, and made certain fundamental rights non-justiciable.
  5. 44th (1978):
    • nullified some of the changes made by the Forty-second Amendment.
    • It restored the power of the judiciary, made certain fundamental rights justiciable, and reduced the power of the central government.
  6. 52nd (1985):
    • extended the reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies by another 10 years.
  7. 73rd (1992):
    • introduced the concept of Panchayati Raj, which gave more power to the local bodies in rural areas.
    • provided for the creation of three tiers of Panchayati Raj institutions, namely,
    • Gram Panchayats,
    • Panchayat Samitis, and
    • Zilla Parishads.
  8. 74th (1992):
    • introduced the concept of urban local bodies, which gave more power to the local bodies in urban areas.
    • provided for the creation of
    • municipalities,
    • municipal councils, and
    • municipal corporations.
  9. 95th (2009):
    • The Ninety-fifth Amendment introduced the concept of a Goods and Services Tax (GST), which replaced several indirect taxes with a single tax.

Overall, the amendments to CoI reflect the country's changing societal norms and political realities. 
The amendments have ensured that the Constitution remains relevant and adapts to the changing needs of the country.

Basic Structure of CoI:

The "Basic Structure" doctrine is a legal principle that has been established by the Supreme Court of India,
which states that certain provisions of CoI are beyond the amending power of the Parliament.
Here are some key features of the Basic Structure of CoI:

  1. Supremacy of the Constitution:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine upholds the supremacy of CoI over all other laws and actions of the government.
    • It states that the Constitution is the highest law of the land and that all other laws and actions must be in line with its provisions.
  2. Separation of Powers:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the principle of separation of powers between the legislature, executive and judiciary.
    • It holds that the Parliament cannot amend the Constitution in a way that violates this principle.
  3. Federalism:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the federal nature of the Indian Constitution, and holds that any attempt to destroy or alter the federal character of the Constitution is unconstitutional.
  4. Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic and Republican Nature:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the Sovereign, Democratic, Secular, Democratic and Republican nature of the Indian Constitution, and holds that any attempt to alter these fundamental features is unconstitutional.
    • The terms "Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, and Republic Nation" are mentioned in the Preamble of CoI, highlighting the foundational principles and values of the country.

Here are some key points explaining each of these principles:

  1. Sovereign:
    • India is a sovereign nation, which means it has independent authority and is not subject to the control or domination of any foreign power.
    • It has the right to govern itself and make decisions in its own interest without external interference.
  2. Socialist:
    • India follows the socialist principle, which aims to establish a more equitable society by reducing economic inequalities.
    • The government plays a role in promoting social welfare, ensuring economic justice, and reducing disparities through policies focused on social and economic upliftment.
  3. Secular:
    • India is a secular nation, which means it does not promote or favor any particular religion as the state religion.
    • The state remains neutral in matters of religion and treats all religions equally.
    • It ensures religious freedom and respects the diverse religious beliefs and practices of its citizens.
  4. Democratic:
    • India is a democratic nation, where the power to govern is vested in the hands of the people.
    • The people exercise this power through their right to vote and participate in free and fair elections.
    • The government is elected by the people and is accountable to them. It upholds the principles of equality, justice, and individual rights.
  5. Republic:
    • India is a republic, which means it has an elected head of state.
    • The President of India, who is the head of state, is elected by an electoral college comprising elected representatives.
    • The President serves as the ceremonial head and symbolizes the unity and integrity of the nation.
    • These principles in the Preamble of the Constitution reflect the aspirations and values that guide the governance and functioning of the Indian state.
    • They provide a foundation for the ideals of justice, equality, freedom, and social progress, which form the core of India's constitutional framework.
  6. Rule of Law:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the importance of the rule of law in the Indian Constitution, and holds that any attempt to undermine or weaken the rule of law is unconstitutional.
  7. Judicial Review:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the importance of the power of judicial review, which is the power of the judiciary to review the actions of the government and declare them unconstitutional if they violate the Constitution.
    • It holds that any attempt to limit or curtail this power is unconstitutional.
  8. Fundamental Rights:
    • The Basic Structure doctrine recognizes the fundamental rights guaranteed under the Constitution, and holds that any attempt to abrogate or dilute these rights is unconstitutional.

Overall, the Basic Structure doctrine is a key feature of CoI that helps to ensure that the fundamental principles and
values enshrined in the Constitution are protected from arbitrary and unconstitutional changes by the government.

    • The Union of India has the power to make laws on subjects that are listed in the Union List,

    • The Union of India has the power to implement and enforce laws that are made by the Parliament.

    • The Union of India is responsible for managing the finances of the country.
    • It is responsible for preparing the budget, collecting taxes and managing public debt.

    • The Union of India has the power to establish and maintain a system of courts and tribunals to interpret and enforce the laws.

  5. Defence Functions:
    • The Union of India is responsible for ensuring the defence and security of the country.
    • It is responsible for maintaining the armed forces, and for protecting the country from external threats.
  6. Foreign Policy Functions:
    • The Union of India is responsible for formulating and implementing foreign policy.
    • It establishes diplomatic relations with other countries, and represents the country in international forums such as the United Nations.
  7. Emergency Functions:
    • The Union of India is responsible for managing situations of national emergency, such as war or natural disasters.
    • It has the power to declare a state of emergency and to take extraordinary measures to deal with the situation.

Overall, these functions of the Union of India help to ensure that the country is governed in a cohesive and effective manner, and that the interests of all citizens are protected.
