1. Cauvery Water Dispute: Sharing a River, Shaping the Future

“Thousands have lived without love, not one without water.”
— W. H. Auden

Introduction

The Cauvery River is more than water; it is the lifeblood for farmers in Karnataka and Tamil Nadu. The dispute over sharing its water is one of India’s longest and most complex interstate conflicts. It shows the tension between geography, law, politics, and human need. Finding a fair solution is key not just for peace, but for shaping a future of cooperation in managing India’s scarce water.

Roots of the Conflict: Geography vs. Politics

The problem lies in a mismatch. The Cauvery’s water is mostly from rain in Karnataka (the upstream state), but the large, historic farmlands that need it are in Tamil Nadu (the downstream state). British-era agreements (1892, 1924) first set sharing rules. After states were reorganised in 1956, Karnataka questioned these old deals, leading to decades of disagreement. It is a classic clash: an upstream state’s need for development versus a downstream state’s right to existing use.

A Timeline of Litigation and Turmoil

The dispute has moved through all levels of governance:

  • 1990s Tension:
    The conflict turned serious in the 1990s, leading to protests and violence in both states.

  • Tribunal Award (2007):
    The Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal gave a final order on how much water each state gets. Tamil Nadu was to get 419 billion cubic feet (BCF), Karnataka 270 BCF.

  • Supreme Court Order (2018):
    The Court upheld the award but slightly reduced Tamil Nadu’s share, recognising Karnataka’s drinking water needs. It also declared the Cauvery a “national asset.”

  • Recent Events (2023):
    Low rainfall in Karnataka led to a crisis. The state argued it could not release water to Tamil Nadu, leading the Supreme Court to tell the central government to create a plan. This shows the problem repeats during weak monsoons.

The Human and Economic Cost

This is not just a legal fight. It affects millions.

  • Farmers’ Distress:
    Farmers in both states depend on the water for crops like sugarcane and paddy. Water shortage ruins harvests, leading to debt and despair.

  • Urban Thirst:
    Cities like Bengaluru also depend on the river. This adds a growing urban demand to the old farm-based dispute.

  • Social Rifts:
    The issue sparks regional pride and anger, damaging the social harmony between two neighbouring states. It is often used for political gain during elections.

Why a Permanent Solution is Elusive

Several deep issues keep the dispute alive:

  1. Variable Monsoon:
    The sharing formula is based on average rainfall. In a bad monsoon year, there is simply not enough water to share as ordered.

  2. Lack of Trust:
    Decades of conflict have created deep mistrust. States often do not share data on water storage fairly.

  3. Changing Needs:
    Population growth, urbanisation, and climate change are making water scarcer, increasing the pressure on the existing agreement.

A Multi-Pronged Path to Resolution

A lasting solution needs more than just dividing water. It needs a new approach to managing the entire river basin.

  1. Beyond Division to Cooperation:
    Shift focus from “sharing water” to “sharing benefits.” Encourage joint projects for farming less water-intensive crops in both states. The “Cauvery Family” initiative by experts is a model where stakeholders from both states dialogue directly.

  2. Demand Management:
    Promote water-saving techniques like drip irrigation. Move farmers away from water-heavy crops like paddy and sugarcane in water-scarce areas. The ‘Per Drop More Crop’ scheme supports this.

  3. Supply Augmentation:
    Improve groundwater recharge, build small check dams, and rejuvenate tanks (traditional water bodies) across the basin to store more rain.

  4. Strengthening Institutions:
    The Cauvery Water Management Authority (CWMA), set up after the Supreme Court order, needs more power and better real-time data sharing to make trusted decisions.

  5. Legal Finality and Compassion:
    All parties must accept the final legal award. At the same time, during a severe drought, a compassionate, science-based review of releases is needed, as the river’s flow is not constant.

Conclusion

The Cauvery dispute is a test of Indian federalism and our wisdom in managing nature’s gifts. It teaches us that rivers cannot be divided by lines on a map; they must be shared by a common vision. The solution lies not in fighting over a shrinking pie, but in making the pie bigger through cooperation, conservation, and climate-smart agriculture. As the Supreme Court noted, the river is a “national asset,” reminding us that states are trustees for its health. By moving from conflict to collaboration, Karnataka and Tamil Nadu can transform the Cauvery from a symbol of discord into a model of shared prosperity, shaping a future where water unites rather than divides.

2. Satluj–Yamuna Link Canal Case: Water, Federalism, and National Unity

Introduction

The Satluj-Yamuna Link (SYL) Canal dispute between Punjab and Haryana is more than a fight over water. It is a deep test of India’s federal system, its constitutional principles, and its ability to balance regional rights with national unity. This long-running conflict shows how water sharing can become a powerful symbol of identity and politics.

Origins of the Dispute: A Story of Reorganization

The root of the problem lies in history. In 1966, Punjab was reorganized, and the new state of Haryana was created. Haryana, being a downstream state, was promised a share of the Ravi and Beas river waters through the proposed SYL Canal. An agreement was signed in 1981. However, Punjab later argued that giving away water would harm its own farmers, especially as its own water needs grew. The building of the canal started but soon became a flashpoint for protest.

The Legal and Political Battlefield

The case has seen many twists in the courts and in politics.

  • Legal Journey:
    The Supreme Court, in a major 2004 ruling, ordered Punjab to complete the canal. Punjab responded by passing a law in 2004 to cancel all its water-sharing agreements, which the Supreme Court struck down in 2016, calling it unconstitutional.

  • Political Volatility:
    The SYL issue is highly emotional in Punjab. It was a central issue in the politics of the 1980s and 1990s and remains sensitive today. No state government finds it easy to agree, fearing public anger. In Haryana, it is seen as a matter of rightful claim and economic survival for its southern regions.

Core Issues: Beyond Just Water

The dispute highlights several deeper challenges:

  1. Federal Tension:
    It pits the state’s power (under the State List) over water resources against the Centre’s duty (under the Union List) to settle inter-state disputes and protect national integrity. The Constitution’s Article 262 gives Parliament the final say, but politics often blocks a solution.

  2. Changing Water Reality:
    Punjab argues that the original agreements were based on older, more plentiful water data. Today, with falling groundwater levels and changing climate, the total water available is less, making old sharing formulas seem unfair.

  3. Sovereignty vs. Solidarity:
    The debate in Punjab is often framed as the state’s right to control its own resources. For the nation, it is about upholding agreements and ensuring fair sharing between sister states.

The Stakes: What is at Risk?

The ongoing deadlock has serious costs:

  • Farmers’ Distress:
    Farmers in southern Haryana remain deprived of assured irrigation, affecting their income and livelihood.

  • Inter-State Harmony:
    The dispute breeds suspicion and rivalry, damaging the social fabric of the region.

  • Trust in Institutions:
    If courts’ orders are not implemented, it weakens the rule of law and the credibility of dispute-resolution mechanisms in the country.

The Path to Resolution: A Way Forward

A lasting solution needs a mix of legal respect, political courage, and technical wisdom.

  1. Accept the Legal Finality:
    All parties must first respect the Supreme Court’s orders. Continued defiance sets a bad precedent for India’s federal system.

  2. Technical Re-assessment:
    A fair, transparent, and scientific review of the current river water data by an independent national body (like the National Water Development Agency) is needed. This can provide a fresh, fact-based foundation for talks.

  3. Focus on Demand Management:
    Both states must seriously promote water-saving agriculture. Shifting away from water-intensive crops like paddy, especially in Punjab, can save huge amounts of water. Schemes like ‘Per Drop More Crop’ are crucial.

  4. Political Dialogue with Statesmanship:
    The Central government must lead a sincere, continuous dialogue, not just during crises. It should act as a neutral facilitator, building trust between the two states.

  5. Compensation and Cooperation:
    Explore models where Haryana supports Punjab in improving water-use efficiency, and the Centre funds projects for better water conservation in both states, creating a “win-win” scenario.

Conclusion

The SYL Canal dispute is a reminder that rivers flow by nature, but sharing them requires statesmanship. The solution lies not in a zero-sum game where one state wins and another loses, but in creating a new pact of cooperation. It calls for respecting the Constitution, adopting scientific water management, and placing national unity above regional politics. As India grows, its states must see shared water not as a object of conflict, but as a common trust. By resolving the SYL issue with wisdom and fairness, India can strengthen its federal bonds and set a powerful example of how a diverse nation manages its precious resources for the collective good.

3. The Inter-State River Water Amendment Bill

Introduction

Water is a state subject, but rivers flow across borders. India’s history of long and bitter inter-state water disputes, like those over the Cauvery and Ravi-Beas rivers, shows a system in need of reform. The proposed Inter-State River Water Disputes (ISRWD) Amendment Bill aims to fix this by speeding up solutions and improving governance. Its success is key to India’s federal harmony and water security.

Why Change is Needed: The Old System’s Flaws

The current law, the ISRWD Act of 1956, has major problems:

  1. Long Delays:
    Setting up a tribunal and getting a final verdict often takes 15-20 years. For example, the Krishna Water Disputes Tribunal took 17 years to give its final award. Justice delayed is justice denied for farmers.

  2. No Time Limit:
    The law does not set a strict deadline for each step of the process, leading to endless arguments and appeals.

  3. Poor Data Sharing:
    States often disagree on basic water flow data, making fair decisions difficult. Trust is low.

  4. Weak Enforcement:
    After a tribunal gives its order, there is no strong mechanism to ensure states follow it, as seen in the SYL Canal case.

Key Proposed Changes in the Amendment Bill

The new bill tries to solve these problems with four main ideas:

  1. A Single, Permanent Tribunal:
    Instead of forming a new tribunal for each dispute, there will be one standing tribunal with multiple benches. This will save time and use expertise better.

  2. Strict Timelines:
    The bill sets a total deadline of two and a half years to settle a dispute from start to finish.

  3. Transparent Data:
    It proposes a dedicated agency to collect and share correct water data from all states, creating a common fact base.

  4. Decision-Making Committee:
    A small group of experts will be formed first to try and solve the dispute through talks before it goes to the tribunal.

The Potential Benefits: A Step Forward

If it works, the bill could change how India manages water disputes.

  • Faster Justice:
    Quicker solutions would reduce farmer suffering and economic loss.

  • Better Federal Trust:
    A clear, time-bound process can reduce political tension between states.

  • Focus on Management:
    With disputes settled faster, states can focus on saving water and using it wisely, rather than fighting in courts.

Challenges and Concerns

However, experts have pointed out some risks and gaps in the bill:

  • Will Timelines Work?
    Past tribunals got delayed because states delayed giving information. The new bill must have strong rules to prevent such delays.

  • Centre’s Role:
    Critics worry the bill gives the central government too much power in choosing tribunal members, which could affect fairness.

  • Root Causes Unaddressed:
    The bill focuses on solving legal fights but does little to change the main problem: overuse and waste of water in agriculture. Without this, new disputes will keep arising.

  • Enforcement Power:
    It is still not clear if the new tribunal will have more power to make sure its orders are followed by states.

The Bigger Picture: Beyond Legal Fixes

For the bill to truly succeed, it must be part of a larger national water strategy.

  1. Promote Water Conservation:
    National campaigns are needed to shift farmers to crops that use less water (like millets) and to adopt drip irrigation. The ‘Per Drop More Crop’ scheme is vital here.

  2. River Basin Management:
    We need to manage each river as one whole unit, not as pieces owned by different states. Joint projects for groundwater recharge and pollution control can build cooperation.

  3. Use Technology:
    Satellite data and AI can be used to monitor river water and reservoir levels in real time, creating transparent data for all.

  4. Political Will:
    State governments must show the courage to accept fair sharing formulas, even if they are politically difficult at home.

Conclusion

The Inter-State River Water Disputes Amendment Bill is a necessary and welcome attempt to fix a broken system. Its focus on speed and transparency addresses real flaws. However, law alone cannot create water where there is scarcity. The ultimate goal is not just to settle disputes faster in courtrooms, but to prevent them in the first place through wise water use and cooperative federalism. By combining this legal reform with a national mission for water conservation, India can transform its water disputes from a story of conflict into one of shared stewardship, securing both harmony and sustenance for its future generations.

4. Agricultural schemes of the government in the era of farmer suicide in India

Introduction: The Paradox of Plenty and Pain

Indian agriculture presents a stark paradox. It is the bedrock of the nation’s food security and employs nearly half the population, yet it is also the epicentre of a deep-seated crisis marked by economic distress and, tragically, farmer suicides. According to the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), over 10,000 persons involved in the farming sector died by suicide in 2022 alone. This grim backdrop forms the critical lens through which the efficacy and reach of government agricultural schemes must be evaluated. The central question is whether these well-intentioned policies are acting as a lifeline or proving inadequate against a tsunami of structural challenges.

The Multifaceted Crisis: Why Do Farmers Struggle?

To assess the schemes, one must first understand the problems they aim to solve. The agrarian distress is multi-dimensional:

  • Economic Fragility:
    Small and marginal holdings (86% of total) are often unviable. Farmers face rising input costs (seeds, fertilisers, pesticides) and volatile market prices for outputs.

  • Debt Trap:
    With low income, farmers borrow from informal sources at exorbitant interest rates for both cultivation and consumption. Crop failure plunges them into an inescapable debt cycle. As noted by economist M.S. Swaminathan, “The real crisis of agriculture is the absence of a profitable price for the farmer.”

  • Climate Vulnerability:
    Erratic monsoons, droughts, and unseasonal rains have become more frequent, making agriculture a high-risk enterprise with little safety net.

  • Structural Issues:
    Fragmented landholdings, poor irrigation access, and inadequate storage and supply chain infrastructure lead to massive post-harvest losses.

Farmer suicides are the most tragic symptom of this confluence of risks, where a single crop failure or price crash can spell catastrophe.

The Policy Arsenal: Key Government Schemes

Successive governments have launched numerous schemes. Major current initiatives include:

  • Pradhan Mantri Kisan Samman Nidhi (PM-KISAN):
    A landmark direct benefit transfer scheme providing ₹6,000 per year to eligible farmer families. It offers crucial income support, reaching over 110 million beneficiaries. However, critics argue the amount is too small to significantly alter farm economics.

  • Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY):
    This crop insurance scheme aims to protect farmers against yield losses. It has seen substantial enrolment, but challenges like delayed claim settlements, low payout ratios in some states, and issues in accurate yield assessment have raised concerns about its effectiveness.

  • Soil Health Card Scheme:
    A proactive initiative to issue cards to farmers recommending nutrient management. It promotes sustainable farming and rationalises input cost. Its success, however, depends on widespread adoption and follow-up.

  • PM-AASHA (Annadata Aay SanraksHan Abhiyan):
    A scheme to ensure Minimum Support Price (MSP) through Price Support Scheme, Price Deficiency Payment, and procurement. Its implementation has been uneven, with procurement largely focused on wheat, rice, and a few other crops in select states.

  • Agriculture Infrastructure Fund (AIF):
    A recent ₹1 lakh crore corpus to finance post-harvest management infrastructure like cold storages and processing units. It targets a critical gap but is a medium-to-long-term solution.

The Gap Between Intent and Ground Reality

While the schemes are comprehensive on paper, their impact is diluted by several factors:

  • Implementation Deficits:
    Leakages, bureaucratic delays, and complex procedures often prevent benefits from reaching the neediest. For instance, tenant farmers and landless labourers are frequently excluded from schemes like PM-KISAN.

  • One-Size-Fits-All Approach:
    National schemes sometimes fail to account for regional variations in crops, climate, and landholding patterns.

  • Addressing Symptoms, Not Roots:
    Income support and insurance provide relief but do not fundamentally alter the unequal terms of trade against agriculture or create sustainable income streams. The continuing demand for legal guarantee of MSP, as seen in the 2020-21 farmers’ protest, underscores this gap.

  • Overemphasis on Cereals:
    Procurement policies have led to water-intensive rice and wheat cultivation in unsuitable regions, causing ecological damage and skewing crop diversification.

Towards Holistic Solutions: A Way Forward

A neutral analysis suggests that schemes are necessary but not sufficient. A balanced strategy requires integrating policy schemes with deeper reforms:

  • Beyond MSP: Diversification and Value Addition:
    Encourage a shift to high-value horticulture, millets, and pulses through assured procurement and building robust Farm Producer Organisations (FPOs). Schemes for food processing, as under AIF, need aggressive promotion.

  • Water Resource Management:
    Schemes like Per Drop More Crop must be integrated with community-led groundwater management and investment in micro-irrigation to combat climate stress.

  • Land Lease Reforms:
    Legally recognising tenant farmers will bring them into the safety net of government schemes and formal credit.

  • Agri-Tech Revolution:
    Leveraging schemes to promote technology like drones for spraying, and apps for real-time market prices (e.g., e-NAM) can enhance efficiency and market access.

  • Non-Farm Rural Employment:
    As recommended by the M.S. Swaminathan Committee, creating gainful employment in rural non-farm sectors is vital to reduce the pressure on agriculture. MNREGA can be dovetailed with farm asset creation.

Conclusion: From Survival to Sustainability

The era of farmer suicides is a sombre reminder that the soul of India’s villages is under siege. Government agricultural schemes represent a vital commitment to address this emergency, acting as essential shock absorbers. PM-KISAN provides dignity, PMFBY offers risk coverage, and infrastructure schemes lay the foundation for the future. However, to truly transform agriculture from a struggle for survival into a sustainable enterprise, these schemes must be the first step, not the last. They need to be seamlessly implemented, locally adapted, and combined with bold structural reforms that empower the farmer as an entrepreneur. Only then can the fields of India yield not just crops, but also prosperity and hope, ensuring that the annadata (food provider) is no longer in distress. The goal must be a system where farming is a choice of aspiration, not a compulsion of desperation.

5. Effectiveness of the schemes

Nurturing Growth: The Effectiveness and Success of Government Schemes in India

Introduction: The Scale of Intent

The Indian state, through its development architecture, launches a vast number of schemes every year. These are designed to improve lives, from health and education to livelihoods and infrastructure. With over 740 Central Sector and Centrally Sponsored Schemes, the intent is clear: to use public funds for public good. However, the journey from policy on paper to change on the ground is complex. The true measure of a scheme is not its announcement but its effectiveness—its reach, impact, and ability to solve the problem it was made for. This essay examines this gap between intent and outcome, the reasons behind it, and the path to greater success.

The Spectrum of Success: Notable Achievements

Several schemes have shown significant positive results, becoming models of good design and implementation.

  • The Jan Dhan-Aadhaar-Mobile (JAM) Trinity:
    This technological backbone has been a game-changer. Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY) brought over 51 crore people into the formal banking system. Linked with Aadhaar and mobile, it enabled direct benefit transfer (DBT). As of 2023, DBT has saved over ₹2.7 lakh crore by removing fake beneficiaries, ensuring help reaches the real ones. This is a clear success in efficiency.

  • Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM):
    Aimed at ending open defecation, SBM built over 10 crore toilets. More than a infrastructure project, it became a people's movement (Jan Andolan). The World Health Organization estimated it could prevent over 3 lakh deaths. This shows how a clear goal with public participation can work.

  • Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana (PMUY):
    Providing LPG connections to poor women changed lives. It gave over 9.5 crore connections, promoting health, gender equality, and easing women's work. Despite some issues with refill costs, its scale and direct benefit are widely accepted.

These successes share common traits: clear targets, use of technology, and strong political will.

The Persistent Challenges: Why Schemes Often Fall Short

For every success, there are schemes that struggle. The reasons are well-known but hard to fix.

  • Poor Design and One-Size-Fits-All:
    Schemes designed in Delhi may not fit local needs in Kerala or Assam. A uniform approach ignores India's great diversity in language, culture, and geography.

  • Leakage and Corruption:
    Before DBT, leakages in welfare schemes were huge. While DBT has helped, leakages still happen in schemes involving physical goods (like food grains under PDS) or work (like MNREGA), through fake muster rolls and quality issues.

  • Last-Mile Delivery Failure:
    The "last mile" is the final step to the beneficiary. It often fails due to a lack of aware citizens, overworked frontline workers (like ASHA and anganwadi workers), and local power structures that hijack benefits. As former President A.P.J. Abdul Kalam said, "Good policies can fail due to weak implementation."

  • Evaluation and Data Gaps:
    There is often no timely, honest assessment of a scheme's impact. Data is sometimes fudded to show success. Without knowing what is not working, it cannot be fixed.

  • Silence on Root Causes:
    Schemes often treat symptoms, not the disease. For example, numerous agricultural schemes (like loan waivers) give temporary relief but do not fix the core problem of unremunerative farming.

The Human and Institutional Dimension

The success of a scheme depends on two key actors: the citizen and the official.

  • Lack of Awareness:
    Many beneficiaries do not know their rights or the details of schemes made for them. This stops them from demanding proper service.

  • Administrative Capacity:
    Implementing agencies are often short of staff, training, and motivation. Frequent transfers of officers break continuity. There is also a tendency to focus on spending the budget (input) rather than achieving the outcome.

  • Political Interference:
    Schemes are sometimes used for vote banks, leading to biased implementation. Changes in government can also lead to renaming or stopping good schemes, wasting earlier efforts.

The Way Forward: Building Blocks for Effective Schemes

To make schemes more effective, a multi-pronged approach is needed.

  • Participatory Design:
    Involve local communities and civil society in designing schemes. This will ensure they are practical and meet real needs. For example, the success of MGNREGA in some states is linked to active social audits by village assemblies (Gram Sabhas).

  • Focus on Outcomes, Not Outlays:
    Shift the focus from how much money was spent to what was achieved. Use independent third-party evaluations to measure real impact.

  • Empower the Frontline:
    Train and empower frontline officials. Give them stability and the tools to do their job. The use of tech apps by ASHA workers is a good step in this direction.

  • Leverage Technology Further:
    Expand the JAM Trinity's use. Use GIS for planning, apps for monitoring, and online grievance portals for feedback. For example, the "UMANG" app aims to provide all government services in one place.

  • Behavioural Change and Awareness:

6. Atal Bhujal Yojana scheme : A Scheme for India's Water Future

Atal Bhujal Yojana: Reviving Springs of Sustainability

Introduction: A Nation Facing a Water Crisis

Water is the source of life, but in India, it is becoming a source of deep crisis. India has 18% of the world's population but only 4% of its freshwater. Of this, 90% is used for agriculture, much of it drawn from underground aquifers. For decades, we have pumped out more water than rain can refill. The result is a national emergency of falling groundwater levels. The Atal Bhujal Yojana (ATAL JAL), launched in 2019, is the Government of India's flagship scheme to tackle this very problem. It is not just a water supply scheme; it is a mission to change how we manage our most precious resource.

What is Atal Bhujal Yojana?

Atal Bhujal Yojana is a Central Government scheme with a total outlay of ₹6,000 crores. The name honors former Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee. The word 'Bhujal' means groundwater. The scheme is co-funded by the World Bank (50%) and the Government of India (50%). It is a unique program because it does not focus on building new infrastructure like dams or canals. Instead, its main goal is to improve groundwater management in seven high-stress states: Gujarat, Haryana, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh. It covers 8,220 water-stressed villages. The core idea is simple: to help villages use water wisely so that there is enough for today and for future generations.

The Big Problem: Why is Groundwater Disappearing?

To understand Atal Bhujal Yojana, we must see the problem it fights.

  • Over-Exploitation:
    Farmers grow water-intensive crops like rice and sugarcane in dry areas because of government support prices and free electricity for pumps. This drains aquifers fast.

  • Lack of Community Role:
    Groundwater is treated as private property. There is no sense of shared ownership. People think, "If I don't pump the water, my neighbor will."

  • Poor Recharge:
    Traditional water bodies like ponds, lakes, and step-wells (baolis) are either dried up or built over. Rainwater flows away instead of sinking into the ground.

  • No Data, No Management:
    Villages often have no clear information about how much water they have, how much they use, and what is a safe limit.

This has led to a grim situation. According to the 2022 CGWB report, over 33% of India's assessment units are 'Over-Exploited' or 'Critical'. This means water is being used faster than it can be renewed.

The ATAL JAL Strategy: A New Way of Thinking

The scheme's power lies in its new approach, built on two pillars:

  • Community-Led Management:
    This is the heart of the scheme. It forms Water User Associations (WUAs) and Gram Panchayats as the key decision-makers. The plan is to make villages prepare a Water Security Plan (WSP). This plan will map their water sources, identify problems, and decide on solutions like which crops to grow, how to recharge water, and what rules to follow. The government provides money and help to implement this plan.

  • Incentive-Based Funding:
    States and villages will receive funds only when they show results. This is called Performance-Based Incentive. If a village successfully forms a WUA, prepares a WSP, and shows improvement in water levels, it gets more funds. This rewards good work and ensures accountability.

Early Signs and Challenges

The scheme is still young, but there are early lessons.

  • Positive Signs:
    In some villages in Haryana and Maharashtra, farmers are coming together to discuss crop patterns. There is more talk about drip irrigation and shifting to less thirsty crops like millets. The creation of thousands of Water User Associations is a big step towards community ownership.

  • Major Challenges:

    • Changing Old Habits:
      Convincing farmers to change crops or use less water is very difficult. It affects their income.

    • Weak Local Bodies:
      Many Gram Panchayats lack the technical skill to make good Water Security Plans.

    • State Coordination:
      Getting different state departments (agriculture, water, electricity) to work together for one goal is a big administrative task.

    • Measuring Success:
      It is hard to measure small improvements in groundwater levels quickly. Real results may take 5-10 years to see clearly.

The Road Ahead: Making ATAL JAL a Success

For Atal Bhujal Yojana to truly succeed, it must become a people's movement. Here is what can be done:

  • Link with Other Schemes:
    ATAL JAL must work closely with PM-KISAN, MGNREGA, and Per Drop More Crop. For example, MGNREGA can be used to build recharge structures in ATAL JAL villages. PM-KISAN incentives can be tweaked to promote millet farming.

  • Build Strong Local Leaders:
    Train local youth and farmers to become "Water Champions." They can explain the crisis better than any officer.

  • Use Technology and Data:
    Give villages simple tools like smartphone apps to track their water levels and usage. Make water data public and easy to understand.

  • Focus on Women:
    Women bear the burden of fetching water. They must be made central leaders in the Water User Associations. Their voice can drive real change.

  • Patient, Long-Term View:
    Governments and people must understand that refilling aquifers is a slow process. The scheme needs constant support, not just for five years, but for decades.

Conclusion: More Than a Scheme, A Necessary Revolution

Atal Bhujal Yojana is more than just another government plan. It represents a necessary revolution in how India thinks about water. It shifts the focus from endless extraction to wise management, from government control to community ownership. In a time of climate change and increasing droughts, its success is critical for our food security, our farmers' future, and our nation's health. As the ancient Sanskrit saying goes, "Jal Hi Jeevan Hai" (Water is life). Atal Bhujal Yojana is an attempt to protect that life. Its true test will be when villages across India start seeing groundwater not as a private resource to be mined, but as a common treasure to be saved. For a water-secure India, this scheme is not just important; it is essential.

7. Apolitical Education

Title: Nurturing Minds: The Power and Potential of Apolitical Education

Introduction: The Purpose of Education

Education is the foundation of a civilized society. Its primary goal is to enlighten the individual, foster critical thinking, and prepare responsible citizens. However, education systems worldwide, including in India, often become battlegrounds for political ideologies. The idea of 'Apolitical Education' does not mean ignoring politics as a subject of study. Instead, it means keeping the educational environment free from partisan political influence, manipulation, and indoctrination, ensuring it remains a neutral space for the pursuit of truth and knowledge.

What Does "Apolitical Education" Mean?

An apolitical education system is one where:

  • Curriculum is based on evidence and scholarship, not on the ruling party's ideology.

  • Academic appointments are made on merit, not political loyalty.

  • Classrooms are safe spaces for open debate on all ideas, without fear.

  • Students learn to think, not what to think. They study political theories, history, and governance objectively to form their own informed opinions.

In short, it is about separating the pursuit of knowledge from the agenda of those in power. As philosopher John Dewey said, "Education is not preparation for life; education is life itself." This life must be lived in freedom.

Why is Political Neutrality in Education Important?

A politically neutral education system is crucial for a healthy democracy.

  • Promotes Critical Thinking:
    When students are exposed to multiple viewpoints, they learn to analyze, question, and reason. This creates informed voters who cannot be easily misled.

  • Ensures National Unity:
    Education that favors one group's history or beliefs over another's can create social division. A neutral approach respects India's diverse culture and promotes unity.

  • Fosters Innovation:
    True research and innovation happen in environments free from dogma. Political pressure can stifle scientific temper and original thought.

  • Builds Trust:
    When institutions like schools and universities are seen as unbiased, they maintain public trust. Recent debates around textbook revisions and university appointments have shown how political interference can damage this trust.

The Challenges and Misconceptions

Achieving apolitical education is difficult.

  • The Myth of Complete Neutrality:
    Some argue that everything is political and perfect neutrality is impossible. While true, the effort must be to minimize partisan bias.

  • Historical Narratives:
    History is often the first casualty. Different governments try to highlight or hide events to build a preferred national story. The ongoing debate over certain chapters in NCERT textbooks is a case in point.

  • Campus Politics:
    Student political groups, while part of democratic life, sometimes disrupt academic life, enforce ideological conformity, and create fear.

  • Appointments and Funding:
    Governments can influence institutions by appointing loyalists as vice-chancellors or by controlling funds for research.

The Way Forward: Protecting the Sanctity of Learning

To safeguard education's core purpose, we need practical steps.

  • Independent Regulatory Bodies:
    Strengthen bodies like the University Grants Commission (UGC) to be truly autonomous. Academic decisions should be made by committees of scholars, not bureaucrats or politicians.

  • Transparent Curriculum Frameworks:
    The process of making syllabi and textbooks should be open, involving experts from various fields and ideologies. The National Curriculum Framework (NCF) should be a document of academic consensus.

  • Promoting Democratic Values in Curriculum:
    Instead of party politics, teach the values of the Constitution—justice, liberty, equality, fraternity, and secularism. This is the common ground for all Indians.

  • Empowering Teachers:
    Train teachers to facilitate discussions on sensitive topics with balance and fairness. Their role is to guide, not preach.

  • Encouraging Civil Debate:
    Schools and colleges should have forums, like structured debates and seminars, where students can engage with different political ideas respectfully, learning the art of disagreement.

Conclusion: Education as the Common Ground

In a diverse and argumentative democracy like India, the education system must be the neutral common ground. An apolitical education is not an education without politics; it is an education that empowers the student to understand politics without being used by it. It is the bedrock upon which a reasoned, tolerant, and progressive society is built. As Dr. S. Radhakrishnan noted, "The aim of education is not the acquisition of information... but the development of that bent of mind, that attitude of reason, that spirit of democracy which will make us responsible citizens." Protecting our classrooms from partisan capture is essential to protect the future of our democracy itself.

8. Politics and the degradation of the education system

Title: Un-raveling Minds: Politics and the Erosion of India's Education System

Introduction: The Corrupted Classroom

Education is the soul of a nation, meant to enlighten minds and build a progressive society. However, in India and many parts of the world, this sacred space is increasingly compromised by political interference. When partisan agendas invade schools and universities, the core mission of education—fostering critical thinking and imparting unbiased knowledge—is degraded. This essay explores how politics undermines educational integrity and the path to reclaiming it.

The Faces of Political Interference

Political influence degrades education through multiple channels:

  • Curriculum as a Battleground:
    History and social science textbooks are often rewritten to highlight certain ideologies while erasing or distorting inconvenient facts. This creates a biased narrative for young minds. Recent debates over revisions in NCERT textbooks, where chapters on Mughal history, the Emergency, and social movements were dropped or altered, exemplify this trend.

  • Appointments Over Merit:
    Key positions like Vice-Chancellors and college principals are frequently filled based on political loyalty rather than academic excellence. This undermines institutional leadership and autonomy, as seen in the controversies surrounding appointments in several central universities.

  • Campus as Political Theatre:
    University campuses, which should be hubs of free debate, often become arenas for violent clashes between student groups backed by rival political parties. This disrupts academic life and creates an atmosphere of fear, as witnessed in incidents at JNU, DU, and Jadavpur University.

  • Resource Allocation as Patronage:
    Funding for research, infrastructure, and grants can be skewed to favour institutions in politically important regions or those aligned with the ruling party, compromising equitable development.

Consequences: A Compromised Future

The fallout from this degradation is severe and multi-generational:

  • Erosion of Critical Thinking:
    Students fed a filtered version of reality become passive recipients of information, not curious inquirers. This produces a citizenry less capable of questioning authority or solving complex problems.

  • Social Fragmentation:
    When education promotes a majoritarian or partisan view, it alienates minority communities and fractures social harmony. It breeds intolerance instead of the constitutional values of fraternity and secularism.

  • Decline in Academic Standards:
    When politics trumps merit, research quality suffers, global rankings fall, and the nation’s intellectual capital diminishes. India’s absence from top ranks in global university lists is a telling sign.

  • Loss of Trust:
    Public faith in educational institutions as fair and neutral spaces is eroded. As Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam warned, “Education that does not build character is a positive danger to society.”

Restoring the sanctity of education requires systemic reforms and a collective will:

  • Autonomous Regulators:
    Strengthen independent bodies like the University Grants Commission (UGC) and National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT). Their committees must be dominated by respected academics, not political appointees.

  • Transparent, Participatory Curriculum Design:

  • Insulating Appointments:
    Establish clear, merit-based criteria for selecting institutional heads through independent search committees, as mandated by the National Education Policy (NEP) 2020.

  • Promoting Democratic Culture on Campus:
    Encourage civil debate and dialogue through structured seminars. University administrations must firmly prevent violence and ensure campuses remain safe for all students.

  • Empowering Teachers and Students:
    Teachers must be trained as facilitators of discussion, not propagandists. Student councils should focus on welfare and academic issues, not serve as mere extensions of external political parties.

Conclusion: Education Above All

The degradation of education by politics is a slow poisoning of the nation’s future. A strong democracy needs citizens who can think for themselves, not merely echo partisan slogans. The classroom must be a temple of learning, protected from the fleeting passions of political wars. Reforming this system is not just an administrative task but a moral imperative. As Nelson Mandela rightly said, “Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world.” We must ensure this weapon is used to build, not break, our society.

9. Frequent change in educational curriculum

A Shifting Paradigm: Navigating the Maze of Frequent Educational Curriculum Changes in India

Introduction: A Shifting Foundation

The curriculum is the blueprint of an education system. It defines what a student learns, shaping their knowledge, skills, and worldview. In India, this blueprint is often redrawn, sometimes with confusing frequency. While updating curriculum is necessary to keep pace with a changing world, frequent and poorly planned changes can do more harm than good. This creates a cycle of instability for students, teachers, and the entire education process, defeating the very purpose of learning.

The Drivers of Frequent Change

Several factors cause this constant churn:

  • Political and Ideological Shifts:
    A change in government often leads to revisions in subjects like history, civics, and literature to align with a particular ideological narrative. For example, the recent National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT) textbook rationalization has sparked debate over the removal and modification of certain historical and scientific content.

  • Centralized Policy Implementation:
    Top-down mandates from national bodies like NCERT are applied uniformly across states, often without considering regional contexts or giving enough time for proper transition.

  • Reaction to Global Trends:
    There is pressure to quickly adopt new educational fads or teaching methods (like smart classes, coding) to appear modern, sometimes without the needed infrastructure or teacher training.

  • Lack of Long-Term Vision:
    Curriculum changes are sometimes made as a symbolic gesture of reform, lacking a clear, evidence-based long-term roadmap for student outcomes.

The Negative Impacts of Instability

Frequent, unplanned changes create serious problems:

  • Confusion for Students:
    Changing syllabi in the middle of a student's academic journey creates gaps in learning and unfairness. For instance, a chapter taught one year may be removed the next, disadvantaging some batches in competitive exams.

  • Burden on Teachers:
    Teachers are forced to constantly re-prepare lesson plans and study new materials, often with inadequate training. This reduces their effectiveness and adds to their stress.

  • Wastage of Resources:
    Textbooks and learning materials become obsolete quickly, leading to financial waste for schools, governments, and parents.

  • Compromised Learning Depth:
    Constant change encourages a superficial "race to cover the syllabus" rather than deep, conceptual understanding. As philosopher John Dewey noted, "Education is not preparation for life; education is life itself." A chaotic curriculum makes this life unstable.

  • Erosion of Trust:
    Frequent reversals and politicization reduce public trust in the education system's credibility and neutrality.

Striking the Right Balance: The Need for Evolution

This does not mean curriculum should never change. It must evolve, but in a stable and thoughtful manner. Core foundational knowledge in languages, mathematics, and sciences requires stability. The application, contemporary examples, and skill-based components need regular updating. The National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 itself emphasizes a flexible, multidisciplinary approach but warns against change for change's sake.

The Path to Stable and Meaningful Reform

To break the cycle of disruptive change, a systematic approach is needed:

  • Long-Term, Bipartisan Vision:
    Establish a National Curriculum Framework (NCF) through a wide consensus involving experts, teachers, and civil society. This framework should be reviewed only at fixed, longer intervals (e.g., every 10-15 years), not with every election cycle.

  • Phased and Piloted Implementation:
    Any new curriculum should be first tested in pilot schools. Feedback must be used to improve it before a slow, phased national rollout over 3-4 years.

  • Empower Teachers First:
    Massive investment in continuous, high-quality teacher training must precede curriculum change. A trained teacher can adapt to new content effectively.

  • Decentralization with Core Principles:
    States and schools should have the flexibility to adapt the national core to local contexts, languages, and needs, within a broad, stable framework.

  • Focus on Learning Outcomes:
    The goal should shift from "completing the syllabus" to achieving well-defined learning outcomes. This allows for different teaching methods within a stable curricular goal.

Conclusion: Consistency for Confidence

Frequent curriculum change is like constantly replanting a sapling; it never gets the stable ground needed to grow strong roots and bear fruit. Our students need a consistent, well-grounded educational experience to develop into confident and capable individuals. Reform is essential, but it must be deliberate, evidence-based, and teacher-centric. The aim should be to build an education system where change is a mindful evolution, not a disruptive revolution, ensuring that the classroom remains a sanctuary of steady growth and enduring learning.

Title: Understanding India’s Higher Education Challenges

Introduction

Higher education is the engine of a nation's progress, creating skilled people, new ideas, and informed citizens. India's higher education is marked by a troubling gap between scale and excellence. While we have top institutes like the IITs and IIMs, they are rare exceptions. Most students end up in colleges with poor infrastructure, outdated teaching, and little connection to the real world. As Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam said, “The purpose of education is to make good human beings with skill and expertise.”

Key Structural Issues

Several deep problems plague the system:

  1. Rote Learning Over Critical Thinking:
    The focus is on memorizing facts for exams, not on understanding, questioning, or solving problems. This kills creativity.

  2. Outdated Curriculum:
    Syllabi in many universities have not changed for years, leaving students without skills needed for modern jobs in technology, data, or management.

  3. Teacher Shortage and Quality:
    There is a huge lack of qualified teachers. Over 30% of professor posts in central universities are vacant. Many teachers are not trained in new teaching methods.

  4. Regulatory Overload:
    Multiple regulators (like UGC, AICTE) create complex rules that colleges spend more time following than improving teaching.

  5. Inequality in Access:
    Students from rural areas, poor families, or marginalized communities find it much harder to get into good colleges or finish their degrees.

This broken system leads to unemployable graduates. A 2023 report by ASSOCHAM found that less than 5% of Indian engineering graduates are fit for high-skill jobs. Millions of graduates end up in low-paying work or remain jobless, leading to frustration and wasted potential.

The government has launched reforms to address these gaps:

  • National Education Policy (NEP) 2020:
    This is a major step. It promotes flexibility (choice of subjects), vocational training, and a single regulator (Higher Education Commission of India).

  • Institutions of Eminence (IoE) Scheme:
    Aims to build 20 world-class Indian universities.

  • Digital Initiatives:
    Platforms like SWAYAM offer online courses to students everywhere.

However, the challenge is in implementation. Changing old habits in thousands of colleges, training teachers for the NEP, and ensuring states cooperate are huge tasks.

A Multi-Pronged Solution Strategy

To transform higher education, we need action on many fronts:

  1. Empower Universities:
    Give good universities more freedom to design courses, hire experts from industry, and set fees, while making them accountable for outcomes.

  2. Industry-Academia Link:
    Make it mandatory for courses to have input from companies through internships, guest lectures, and updated syllabi. Apprenticeship degrees should be encouraged.

  3. Focus on Teachers:
    Launch a massive national mission to train teachers in new pedagogies, fill vacancies, and improve pay to attract the best talent.

  4. Promote Research and Innovation:
    Increase funding for research in universities. Link funding to useful research that solves local and national problems.

  5. Bridge the Digital Divide:
    Ensure all colleges have high-speed internet and students can afford digital devices, so online resources benefit everyone, not just the urban elite.

  6. Inclusive Education:
    Provide strong scholarships, hostels, and language support for students from SC/ST, rural, and low-income backgrounds to ensure they complete their education.

Conclusion

India's higher education system stands at a crossroads. It can continue to be a factory producing degree-holders without skills, or it can transform into a catalyst for innovation and social mobility. The NEP 2020 provides the map for this journey. The success of this transformation depends on political will, investment, and a collective commitment to valuing quality over quantity. By empowering students to think, innovate, and adapt, we can build not just a skilled workforce, but a enlightened society capable of leading in the 21st century. The future of India depends on the classrooms of today.

India faces a critical paradox: a young population full of potential alongside a vast skills gap that limits employment and economic growth. Bridging this gap is a national priority.     Any technical training must be blended with communication, teamwork, and basic digital skills to create well-rounded, employable candidates.

  • Robust Outcome Tracking:

Conclusion: Investing in Human Capital

CSR funds for skill enhancement represent a powerful convergence of corporate responsibility and national need. By moving from fragmented charity to coordinated, outcome-focused partnerships, the corporate sector can play a transformative role in building a skilled, confident, and employable Bharat. 12. National policy for skill development

Unveiling Horizons: India's Odyssey through the National Policy for Skill Development

Introduction: The Skills Imperative

India stands at a unique demographic crossroads. With over 65% of its population under 35, it has a potential "demographic dividend." However, this dividend risks becoming a liability without a skilled workforce. A robust National Policy for Skill Development is, therefore, not just an economic need but a national imperative. It is the bridge between the aspirations of millions of youth and the demands of a modern, competitive economy.

Evolution and Vision: From PMKVY to NEP 2020

India's formal skill development mission gained momentum with the National Policy on Skill Development 2009, later revised in 2015. This led to the creation of the Ministry of Skill Development and Entrepreneurship (MSDE) and flagship schemes like the Pradhan Mantri Kaushal Vikas Yojana (PMKVY), which has trained over 1.4 crore youth. The policy's core vision is to empower the Indian workforce with employable skills and knowledge, making India the "skill capital of the world."

The National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 has further strengthened this by integrating vocational education into mainstream schooling from Class 6 onwards, breaking the traditional stigma associated with non-academic paths.

Key Pillars of the Policy Framework

The national policy is built on several strategic pillars:

  • Demand-Driven Training:
    Moving beyond arbitrary courses to skills actually needed by industry. Sector Skill Councils (SSCs), with industry representatives, help define these standards.

    Establishing the National Skills Qualification Framework (NSQF) to standardize training outcomes and ensure certificates are recognized across the country.

  • Scale vs. Quality Dilemma:

  • Social Stigma:
    Vocational training is still seen as a second choice to white-collar college degrees, affecting enrolment.

  • Fragmented Ecosystem:
    Multiple ministries and schemes (MSDE, MHRD, rural development) run parallel skilling programs, causing overlap and coordination gaps.

  • Financing Gaps:
    Sustained funding for high-quality infrastructure, trainer development, and trainee stipends remains a challenge.

India's struggle with unrecognized slums is a microcosm of the global challenge. Its experiments—both failures (forced evictions) and successes (Odisha's JAGA Mission granting land rights)—offer vital lessons. In a world where urban population will double by 2050, learning how to integrate informal settlements is not optional; it is the central task of 21st-century urbanism.

Conclusion: From Invisibility to Inclusion

Unrecognized slums are the most visible sign of our collective failure to build equitable cities. They are not the problem of the poor but a problem created by systems that exclude the poor. The path forward, from the alleys of Karachi to the hillsides of Caracas, is the same: recognition, rights, and resources. As the urban thinker David Harvey argued, the "right to the city" is a collective right to change the city. Ensuring that right for slum dwellers everywhere is the cornerstone of building a just, sustainable, and truly urban world.

20. The Unequal economic distribution in India

Title: Bridging the Divide: Navigating Unequal Economic Distribution in India

Introduction

India, one of the world's fastest-growing major economies, presents a stark paradox. While its GDP expands and its billionaires multiply, the fruits of this growth are concentrated in few hands. A vast section of the population remains on the margins, struggling for basic necessities. This severe and persistent inequality is not just an economic issue; it threatens social harmony, undermines democracy, and hampers sustainable development.

The Dimensions of Disparity

Economic inequality in India is multi-dimensional.

  • Income and Wealth Gap:
    According to the 'World Inequality Report 2022', the top 10% of Indians earn 57% of the national income and own 65% of the total wealth. The bottom 50% earn only 13% and own a minuscule 6%. This makes India one of the most unequal countries globally.

  • Rural-Urban Divide:
    Per capita income in urban areas is often more than double that in rural areas. This fuels mass migration to cities, straining urban infrastructure while leaving villages deprived of youth and enterprise.

  • Regional Imbalance:
    States like Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, and Gujarat contribute significantly more to the national GDP than states like Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, and Jharkhand. This creates a cycle where richer states attract more investment, widening the gap further.

  • Social Disparities:
    Inequality overlaps with caste and gender. SCs, STs, and women face systemic barriers in accessing education, credit, and quality jobs, perpetuating intergenerational poverty.

Root Causes of the Divide

Several factors contribute to this concentration of prosperity.

  • Skewed Growth Model:
    Post-1991 reforms focused on capital-intensive sectors (IT, telecom, services) over labour-intensive ones (like manufacturing). This created high-value jobs for a skilled minority but not enough for the masses with basic education.

  • Informal Sector Dominance:
    Over 90% of India’s workforce is in the informal sector, with no job security, social security, or fair wages. The pandemic lockdowns exposed their extreme vulnerability.

  • Unequal Access to Human Capital:
    The quality of public education and healthcare varies greatly. The poor rely on underfunded public systems, while the affluent opt for private facilities, creating a stark capability gap from childhood.

  • Policy and Cronyism:
    At times, policies like loan waivers for large industries or the previous `twin balance sheet' problem showed a bias towards big capital. Crony capitalism, where business success depends on proximity to power, distorts a level playing field.

Recent Evidence and Consequences

Current events underscore these divides. The farmers' protests (2020-2021) highlighted the distress in agriculture, a sector employing nearly half the workforce but contributing only 15% to GDP. The COVID-19 pandemic led to a "K-shaped recovery" – where large corporates and formal sectors rebounded sharply, while MSMEs, informal workers, and contact-based services were devastated. The rise in multi-billion dollar deals in sectors like telecom and retail contrasts with high unemployment rates among youth, a pressure point visible in agitations for reservations in government jobs.

The consequences are dire: social unrest, the erosion of trust in institutions, poor health and education outcomes, and ultimately, a drag on the country's full economic potential.

Possible Solutions: A Path to Inclusive Growth

A balanced approach requires action on multiple fronts:

  1. Boost Labour-Intensive Manufacturing:
    A successful 'Make in India' must focus on sectors like textiles, footwear, and food processing to create mass employment. Simplifying labour laws and improving infrastructure are key.

  2. Invest in Human Capital:
    Drastically increase spending on public education and healthcare to ensure quality for all, as recommended by the National Education Policy 2020 and National Health Policy 2017. The Ayushman Bharat scheme is a step in the right direction.

  3. Strengthen Social Security:
    Extend the coverage of schemes like Pradhan Mantri Shram Yogi Maan-dhan to all informal workers. Effectively implement the minimum wage laws.

  4. Progressive Taxation and Public Spending:
    Use wealth and inheritance taxes cautiously but effectively to fund social sectors. Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) schemes like PM-KISAN have shown that targeted welfare can reduce leakages.

  5. Empower Local Governments: Decentralise power and funds to Panchayati Raj Institutions for locally relevant development, as seen in the success stories of Kerala.

Conclusion

As Dr. B.R. Ambedkar warned, "In an unequal society, liberty and equality may become incompatible." India’s journey towards becoming a $5-trillion economy will be incomplete and unsustainable if it leaves millions behind. The challenge is to transition from a model of mere "wealth creation" to genuine "wealth distribution." This requires not just trickle-down economics but a proactive "pull-up" strategy that empowers the poorest. Only by building an economy where growth is broad-based and equitable can India realise its true democratic and humanitarian promise.

21. The Unequal economic distribution in the World

Introduction

Global economic progress since the Industrial Revolution has been immense, lifting billions out of poverty. Yet, its benefits are spread unevenly, not just between nations but also within them. Today, the world stands at a critical juncture where extreme wealth and persistent poverty exist side-by-side. This global inequality is a major source of social tension, political instability, and a barrier to achieving worldwide goals like sustainable development and climate action.

The Landscape of Global Inequality

Inequality today has two clear faces: inequality between countries and inequality within countries.

  • The Gap Between Nations:
    Historically, colonisation and unequal trade terms created a rich "Global North" and a poorer "Global South." While emerging economies like India and China have narrowed the gap in average incomes, disparities remain vast. For example, the per capita income of a high-income country can be over 50 times that of a low-income country.

  • Inequality Within Nations:
    According to Oxfam, the world's richest 1% have captured almost two-thirds of all new wealth created since 2020. This trend is visible everywhere, from the United States, where CEO pay is hundreds of times the average worker's salary, to India, where the top 10% hold over 60% of the nation's wealth.

  • The Pandemic Effect:
    COVID-19 worsened this divide. It was a "K-shaped recovery" globally. Billionaires saw their wealth surge due to rising stock and asset prices, while the world's poor, especially informal workers and women, faced job losses and deeper poverty, as seen in India's migrant worker crisis.

Key Drivers of Global Disparity

Several interconnected factors fuel this division.

  1. Globalisation's Uneven Playground:
    While global trade boosted growth, its rules often favoured developed nations. Rich countries protect their agriculture with subsidies, harming farmers in developing nations. Tax havens allow multinational companies and the ultra-rich to avoid paying fair taxes, draining resources from public services elsewhere.

  2. Technology and the Skills Gap:
    The digital revolution created huge wealth for tech giants and skilled professionals but automated many routine jobs. This "skill-biased technological change" leaves behind workers without advanced education, widening the wage gap. India's IT boom created millionaires but did little for its vast low-skilled workforce.

  3. Debt and Unfair Financial Systems:
    Poorer countries often borrow at high interest rates for development. When global crises hit, they spend more on debt repayment than on health or education for their people. Sri Lanka's recent economic crisis is a stark example.

  4. Climate Injustice:
    The poor in developing countries, who contributed least to global warming, suffer the most from climate change (e.g., floods in Pakistan, droughts in Africa). The cost of adapting to climate change strains their already limited resources.

Consequences: A World at Risk

Extreme inequality is destabilising. It breeds:

  • Social Unrest:
    Movements like "Yellow Vests" in France or protests over inequality in Latin America show public anger.

  • Political Polarisation:
    Leaders often exploit economic fears, leading to divisive politics and weakened democracies.

  • Stunted Development:
    High inequality hampers overall economic growth, as a large part of the population cannot afford to buy goods or invest in their future.

  • Global Insecurity:
    Poverty and lack of opportunity can become breeding grounds for conflict and mass migration, affecting global stability.

Towards a More Equal World: Possible Solutions

Addressing this requires coordinated global action and national will.

  1. Fair Global Taxation:
    Implement a global minimum corporate tax (as recently agreed by 140+ nations) to stop tax avoidance. Countries like India should lead calls for taxing digital giants fairly.

  2. Debt Justice:
    Reform global financial institutions to provide relief and concessional loans to developing nations facing climate and health crises.

  3. Equitable Trade:
    Make World Trade Organization (WTO) rules fairer for developing countries by reducing rich nations' farm subsidies and allowing protection for growing industries.

  4. Invest in People:
    Nations must prioritise spending on universal healthcare, quality education (like India's NEP 2020), and social security. This builds human capital and empowers the poor.

  5. Climate Equity:
    Developed countries must fulfil their commitment to provide $100 billion annually in climate finance to help developing nations adapt and use green technology.

Conclusion

As economist Thomas Piketty noted, when the rate of return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth, inequality tends to rise. The current global system risks creating a permanent cycle of disparity. The challenge is to move from a model that concentrates wealth to one that shares prosperity. For India, a nation with its own deep inequalities, advocating for a fairer world order is both a moral duty and a strategic need. The path forward lies in cooperative global governance, ethical capitalism, and unwavering national commitment to inclusive growth. Only then can we build a world where economic progress truly leaves no one behind.

India’s technology revolution is a powerful force that can drive the nation towards its ambitious goals. As Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam said, “Technology must be guided by the humanities.” The true measure of success will not be in the number of unicorns or digital transactions alone, but in how effectively technology reduces inequality, empowers the weakest, and solves India’s most pressing problems. By fostering inclusive innovation and building a robust regulatory framework, India can ensure that its tech revolution leads to a more prosperous, equitable, and knowledgeable society for every citizen.

24. Demographic dividend in India

Title: India's Demographic Dividend: Nurturing a Bright Future through Youth Power

Introduction

India stands at a unique moment in its history. With over 65% of its population under 35, it has the youngest and one of the largest workforces in the world. This “demographic dividend” presents a golden opportunity to accelerate economic growth and achieve sustainable development. However, this dividend is not automatic; it is a time-bound chance that requires urgent and strategic action to transform a young population into a productive, skilled asset.

What is the Demographic Dividend?

The demographic dividend refers to the economic growth potential that arises from a shift in a population’s age structure. This happens when the share of the working-age population (15-64 years) is larger than the non-working-age groups (children and the elderly). According to the United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA), India’s working-age population will continue to grow until at least 2041, offering a window of opportunity. For comparison, nations like China and Japan are now ageing, while India remains young.

India’s Opportunity: A Multi-Dimensional Advantage

A large, young workforce can benefit India in several ways:

  • Economic Engine:
    More workers mean higher production, savings, and investment. This can boost GDP growth significantly if these workers are employed productively.

  • Innovation and Entrepreneurship:
    Young minds drive technological adoption and start new businesses. India’s thriving startup ecosystem, with over 100 unicorns, is a direct result of this youthful energy.

  • Global Workforce:
    As developed countries age, India can become a key supplier of skilled labour and services to the world, enhancing its geopolitical and economic standing.

  • Social Progress:
    Educated and employed youth, especially women, can lead to smaller, healthier families, better education for children, and a break in the cycle of poverty.

Critical Challenges: The Risk of a Divided Demographic

If not harnessed, the demographic dividend can turn into a “demographic disaster.” The key challenges are:

  • The Skilling Crisis:
    The ASER 2022 report shows worrying gaps in foundational learning. Over 50% of youth lack formal vocational training. There is a mismatch between the skills youth have and the jobs the market needs, leading to high unemployment among graduates.

  • Jobless Growth:
    Despite economic growth, job creation remains inadequate. The PLFS data shows high unemployment rates among urban youth (over 15% for 15-29 age group). Growth in capital-intensive sectors does not create enough jobs for a large labour force.

  • Low Female Workforce Participation:
    India’s female labour force participation rate is worryingly low at around 30%. Social norms, safety concerns, and unpaid care work prevent half the population from contributing fully to the economy.

  • Regional and Social Imbalances:
    Southern states are already ageing faster than the northern ones. Also, youth from marginalized communities often lack equal access to quality education and job networks.

  • Health and Well-being:
    Malnutrition (stunting), mental health issues, and lifestyle diseases can reduce the productivity of the young workforce before it even enters the job market.

The Way Forward: Strategic Interventions

To realise the dividend, India needs a comprehensive, mission-mode approach:

  1. Education and Skill Revolution:
    Implement the National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 with focus on foundational literacy, critical thinking, and vocational training from school level. Scale up programs like PMKVY 4.0 to skill youth in Industry 4.0 technologies like AI, robotics, and green jobs.

  2. Boost Labour-Intensive Manufacturing:
    Promote ‘Make in India’ in sectors like textiles, electronics, and food processing to create mass employment. Simplify labour laws and improve infrastructure to attract investment.

  3. Empower Women Economically:
    Provide safe transport, affordable childcare facilities (creches), and flexible work options. Schemes like MGNREGA should focus more on creating community assets that reduce women’s care work burden.

  4. Focus on Health and Nutrition:
    Strengthen the Poshan Abhiyan to tackle childhood stunting. Integrate mental health services in primary healthcare through the Ayushman Bharat network to ensure a healthy workforce.

  5. Governance and Data:
    Use technology for better job matching through portals like ASEEM. Decentralise planning to address local employment needs effectively.

Conclusion

India’s demographic dividend is a powerful but perishable gift. As former President Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam envisioned, it can be the foundation of a developed ‘India@100’. The choice is clear: invest ambitiously in the human capital of our youth today, or face the social and economic costs of a disengaged generation tomorrow. The time for action is now. By converting its population advantage into a productivity advantage, India can script a story of inclusive and sustained prosperity for the world to see.

Introduction

Across the globe, nations are at different stages of a powerful demographic transition—the shift from high birth and death rates to low ones.     Many nations in Sub-Saharan Africa (e.g., Nigeria, Congo) have very young populations with high birth rates. While this promises a future dividend, it poses an immediate challenge of providing education and jobs to a vast youth cohort, which, if unmet, can lead to instability.

The Global Promise: Growth and Stability

When harnessed well, the demographic dividend can be a global force for good.

  • Engine for Global Growth:
    A productive young workforce in developing countries can become the factory and service hub for the world, lifting global GDP. The economic rise of China was powered by its demographic dividend in the late 20th century.

  • Driving Innovation:

  • Poverty Reduction:
    Successful harnessing of the dividend, as in parts of Southeast Asia, has historically led to rapid declines in poverty and improvements in human development indices.

Mismanagement of this demographic phase can lead to severe crises.

    Lack of opportunity in young nations and labour shortages in ageing ones drive irregular migration. This creates humanitarian challenges and political tensions in destination countries, evident in US-Mexico border issues and Mediterranean migration routes.

  • Global Inequality:
    A declining workforce in the rich world may concentrate capital and technology there, while poor nations struggle with unemployment, potentially widening the global wealth gap.

  • The Burden of Ageing:
    For ageing societies, sustaining pensions and healthcare for the elderly with fewer workers is a major fiscal challenge, threatening social contracts.

Realizing the dividend requires national action supported by international cooperation.

  1. Invest in Human Capital Globally:
    International institutions like the World Bank must prioritize funding for education, skill development, and healthcare in pre-dividend and dividend-phase countries. Quality education for girls is particularly crucial.

  2. Facilitate Orderly Migration:
    The world needs legal, managed migration pathways. Schemes for seasonal work visas and skill partnerships (like India’s recent agreements with Japan and Germany) can benefit both ageing and young nations.

  3. Promote Job-Centric Growth:
    Developing nations must focus on labour-intensive manufacturing and services. Global trade rules should support this. India’s Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme is a step to create manufacturing jobs.

  4. Technology for an Ageing World:
    Developed nations should invest in automation and AI to counter labour shortages, while sharing assistive technologies to support elderly care globally.

  5. South-South Cooperation:
    Countries like India, which is managing its own dividend, can share best practices in digital governance (Aadhaar, UPI) and public health (Co-Win) with African nations.

Conclusion

The global demographic dividend is not a guaranteed future but a fleeting opportunity that demands wise and timely action. For ageing nations, the challenge is to adapt and innovate. For youthful ones like India, the task is to empower and employ. The world’s demographic fate is interconnected. By fostering international cooperation on skills, migration, and equitable growth, we can ensure that this demographic transition becomes a source of shared global prosperity and stability, rather than a divide that deepens existing fractures. The dividend is a global test of our collective foresight.

26. The position of India against China

Title: India and China: Navigating a Complex Relationship on the Global Stage

Introduction

The relationship between India and China, the two most populous nations and civilisational neighbours, is one of the world’s most complex bilateral dynamics. It is a mix of deep economic ties, unresolved border disputes, and growing strategic competition. Since the 2020 Galwan Valley clash, the relationship has entered a new phase of persistent strain. India’s position against China today is thus not of simple opposition, but of calibrated strategic balancing—securing its interests while managing an unavoidable and powerful neighbour.

Areas of Competition and Conflict

India faces clear challenges from China across multiple fronts.

  • The Border Issue:
    The core dispute is the 3,488-km long Line of Actual Control (LAC). China’s aggressive infrastructure build-up and repeated transgressions, like in Galwan (2020) and Tawang (2022), challenge the status quo. India has responded with matching infrastructure and troop deployment, leading to a costly military standoff.

  • Economic Imbalance and Dependence:
    Trade heavily favours China, with a deficit of over $85 billion in 2022-23. China dominates critical sectors like electronics, APIs for pharmaceuticals, and renewable equipment, creating strategic vulnerability for India.

  • Strategic Encirclement:
    China’s “String of Pearls” strategy, including ports in Sri Lanka (Hambantota), Pakistan (Gwadar), and Myanmar, is seen as an attempt to surround India in the Indian Ocean. Its deep ties with Pakistan are a constant security concern.

  • Global Influence:
    China uses its economic might to influence global institutions and norms, often at odds with Indian interests. Its blocking of UN listings of Pakistan-based terrorists affects India’s security.

India’s Strategic Response and Strengths

India is not a passive player and has developed a multi-pronged strategy.

  • Military Posture and Alliances:
    India has shifted from mere border management to credible deterrence. It has ramped up infrastructure (e.g., Sela Tunnel), increased defence budgets, and focused on indigenous manufacturing under “Atmanirbhar Bharat.” Partnerships like the Quad (with US, Japan, Australia) and deeper ties with Europe provide strategic balance.

  • Economic Decoupling and Self-Reliance:
    Post-Galwan, India banned over 300 Chinese apps (like TikTok), tightened FDI rules, and promoted Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes to reduce import dependence, especially in telecom and electronics.

  • Diplomatic Outreach and Global Stature:
    India leverages its role as a responsible democratic power. It provides an alternative to China’s Belt and Road Initiative through projects like the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC). Its G20 presidency showcased its ability to bridge global divides.

  • Demographic and Digital Advantage:
    India’s young population and thriving tech ecosystem offer a long-term edge over China’s ageing society. Its Digital Public Infrastructure (UPI, Aadhaar) is a globally admired soft power tool.

Areas of Engagement and Interdependence

Despite rivalry, complete disengagement is neither possible nor wise.

  • Trade and Climate Change:
    China remains a major trade partner. Both nations cooperate in multilateral forums like the BRICS and on global issues such as climate change, where they share common concerns as developing countries.

  • People-to-People Ties:
    Cultural and historical connections provide a base for people-level understanding, though currently limited.

The Path Ahead: Firmness with Pragmatism

India’s future approach must be clear-eyed and resilient.

30. Fast track diplomacy and Para diplomacy

Title: Fast Track Diplomacy and Para-Diplomacy: India's Strategic Innovations on the Global Stage

Introduction

In today's rapidly changing world, traditional diplomacy—slow and confined to central governments—is often too rigid. To meet new challenges, nations are adopting more flexible and dynamic tools. Two such key instruments are Fast Track Diplomacy and Para Diplomacy. While the former speeds up decision-making at the highest level, the latter decentralises international engagement to local actors. Together, they make a country's foreign policy more agile, effective, and connected to people's needs.

What is Fast Track Diplomacy?

Fast Track Diplomacy refers to the use of direct, high-level, and swift political engagement to resolve urgent issues or seize immediate opportunities. It bypasses lengthy bureaucratic channels.

  • How it Works:
    It involves frequent visits by heads of state, quick hotline conversations, and special envoys sent for focused talks. The aim is to build personal trust and break deadlocks quickly.

  • Examples from India:
    Prime Minister Narendra Modi's surprise visit to Lahore in 2015 was an attempt at fast-track engagement with Pakistan. During the Ukraine crisis, India's high-level dialogues with both Russia and the West to secure national interests (like energy deals and citizen evacuation) showcased this approach. The quick finalisation of the India-UAE Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) in 2022 is another example.

What is Para Diplomacy?

Para Diplomacy, or sub-national diplomacy, is the international role played by local governments—states, cities, and regions. They engage with foreign counterparts directly on issues like trade, climate, and culture, within the framework of the national foreign policy.

  • How it Works:
    It includes sister-city agreements, state-level trade delegations, and cultural exchanges organised by local governments. For instance, the Tamil Nadu Global Investors Meet attracts foreign investment directly.

  • Examples from India:
    Gujarat has long engaged with Japan and China for investment. Punjab and Rajasthan have cooperated with neighbouring Pakistani provinces on water and cultural issues. Kerala’s engagement with Gulf nations on welfare of its diaspora is a key form of para diplomacy.

Significance and Advantages

Both tools add great strength to modern statecraft.

  • Speed and Flexibility:
    Fast track diplomacy allows for rapid crisis management, like during the evacuation from Ukraine (Operation Ganga). Para diplomacy lets states tailor partnerships to their local economic strengths (e.g., Karnataka in IT, Goa in tourism).

  • Building People-to-People Ties:
    Para diplomacy fosters grassroots connections through cultural festivals, academic exchanges, and local business links, making international relations more people-centric.

  • Complementing National Goals:
    They support central policy. While the Centre signs a broad trade deal, states can follow up with targeted roadshows to bring in specific investments, boosting the national 'Make in India' mission.

Challenges and Risks

However, these tools come with their own set of problems.

  • Lack of Coordination:
    Without proper alignment, a state's independent engagement (para diplomacy) could accidentally contradict national policy, sending mixed signals to other countries.

  • Over-Dependence on Leadership:
    Fast track diplomacy that relies too much on personal equations between leaders can be unstable. A change in government can disrupt ongoing initiatives.

  • Bypassing Democratic Scrutiny:
    Very quick high-level deals might not get enough discussion in parliament or with experts, potentially overlooking long-term consequences.

  • Resource Inequality:
    Richer states (like Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu) have more capacity for para diplomacy than poorer ones, which could increase regional imbalances within India.

The Way Forward: A Balanced Framework

To maximise benefits and minimise risks, a structured approach is needed.

  1. Create a Clear Policy:
    The Union Government should issue clear guidelines for states on para diplomacy, defining their scope and limits, ideally through a dedicated cell in the Ministry of External Affairs (MEA).

  2. Institutionalise Coordination:
    Regular meetings should be held between the MEA, other central ministries, and state officials to share information and synchronise efforts. The MEA’s States Division can play this role better.

  3. Build State Capacity:
    The Centre should help train officials from all states in international negotiation and protocol, ensuring wider and more effective participation.

  4. Use Fast Track Diplomacy Wisely:
    It should be used for breaking logjams, but followed by steady institutional work to make agreements lasting. It must also respect parliamentary oversight.

Conclusion

Fast Track Diplomacy and Para Diplomacy are essential upgrades for foreign policy in a complex world. They represent a shift from a single-actor, slow-moving model to a multi-level, agile system. For India, with its vibrant federal democracy and global aspirations, mastering these tools is crucial. By strategically combining the speed of high-level dialogue with the grassroots strength of its states, India can build a more responsive, inclusive, and effective diplomacy. This will not only secure its national interests but also strengthen its federal structure and deepen its global engagement.

31. Basic income and subsidy policy

Title: Balancing Basic Income and Subsidy Policies: Navigating Socioeconomic Equilibrium in India

Introduction

In the fight against poverty and economic insecurity, governments use different tools. Two important, but very different, ideas are subsidy policies and Basic Income. Subsidies are discounts or free items given by the government on specific goods like food, fuel, or fertilizer. Basic Income is a regular, fixed amount of money given by the government to every citizen, with no conditions attached. Both aim to help people, but they work in different ways and have different results. Finding the right mix is key for a country like India.

Understanding Subsidy Policy

Subsidies have been India's main way to support the poor and boost certain sectors.

  • How They Work:
    The government lowers the price of essential items. The Public Distribution System (PDS) provides cheap food grains. Subsidies are also given for cooking gas (LPG), electricity, fertilizers, and loans for farmers.

  • Main Goals:
    To make basic needs affordable, to support farmers' income, and to control inflation (price rise).

  • The Good:
    Subsidies provide immediate, targeted help. The National Food Security Act, 2013 covers about 80 crore people, ensuring food security.

  • The Challenges:
    They often have leakages and errors. Benefits sometimes go to non-poor people. Subsidies can also distort the market; for example, free electricity for farmers can lead to overuse of groundwater. They can be a huge financial burden. In 2023-24, India budgeted over ₹4 lakh crore for major subsidies.

Understanding Basic Income

Basic Income is a direct cash transfer to all citizens, regardless of their wealth or job status.

  • How It Works:
    Every adult receives a fixed amount regularly, say every month, directly into their bank account. It is universal and unconditional.

  • Main Goal:
    To provide a financial safety net, reduce extreme poverty, and give people the freedom to spend on what they need most.

  • The Good:
    It cuts out corruption and leakages by sending money directly. It respects individual choice—a family can use the money for food, healthcare, or a child's education as they see fit. It can simplify the welfare system.

  • The Challenges:

  • It is very expensive to give money to everyone. Critics say it might discourage people from working. There is also a worry that giving cash to the rich is a waste of resources that could be better targeted to the poor.

India's Experience: A Shift Towards Direct Cash

India has not adopted a full Basic Income, but it is moving towards similar ideas.

  • PM-KISAN:
    This scheme gives ₹6,000 per year to all land-holding farmer families. It is a form of direct income support, not linked to crop prices or production.

  • Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT):
    This is a big change. Now, subsidies for LPG, scholarships, and pensions are sent directly to people's bank accounts linked with Aadhaar. This has saved over ₹2.7 lakh crore by stopping fake beneficiaries.

  • State Experiments:
    Sikkim is planning a trial for a Universal Basic Income. Earlier, a charity-led trial in Madhya Pradesh showed positive results in health and education.

Finding the Right Balance: A Hybrid Model

A smart policy would use the strengths of both systems.

  1. Phase Out Inefficient Subsidies:
    Gradually replace subsidies that have high leakages (like on fertilizer) with direct cash transfers. The money saved can be used better.

  2. Keep Essential In-Kind Subsidies:
    For absolute basics like food, especially for the most vulnerable, in-kind subsidies through the PDS should continue. Food security cannot be left only to the market.

  3. Target Through "UBI for the Poor":
    Instead of a universal payment for all, India can think of a "Quasi-Universal Basic Income" for the bottom 50-60% of the population. This balances cost and coverage.

  4. Use Technology and Data:
    Improve the SOCIAL REGISTRY to accurately identify the poor and vulnerable for better targeting of both cash and kind benefits.

  5. Invest in Public Services:
    Cash transfers work best when people have access to good schools, hospitals, and markets. Basic Income money should add to, not replace, public investment in these areas.

Conclusion

The debate is not about choosing one over the other completely. Subsidies in kind ensure basic consumption, while direct cash transfers empower people and increase efficiency. India's journey through DBT and PM-KISAN shows a clear shift towards trusting its citizens with direct support. The future likely lies in a balanced model: a strong, reformed PDS for food security, combined with targeted direct income support for other needs. As economist Nobel laureate Abhijit Banerjee has argued, direct cash gives the poor both freedom and dignity. For India, the goal should be a system that is less wasteful, more respectful, and truly lifts people out of poverty for good.

32. Can subsidy eradicate poverty?

Title: Unraveling the Tapestry of Poverty: The Role of Subsidies in India's Pursuit of Economic Equity

Introduction

Poverty is a complex trap of low income, poor health, and little access to opportunities. Governments worldwide use subsidies—financial support on goods and services—as a key tool to fight it. In India, subsidies for food, fuel, fertilizer, and housing are a big part of the welfare system. But a critical question remains: Can subsidies alone erase poverty, or are they just a temporary relief? The answer is that subsidies are a necessary first step, but not a complete solution.

The Role of Subsidies: A Necessary Support

Subsidies play a vital role in protecting the poor and form a safety net.

  • Immediate Relief:
    They provide instant help by making basic needs affordable. The National Food Security Act (NFSA), which gives cheap grains to about 80 crore people, prevents hunger and starvation.

  • Protection from Shocks:
    Subsidies on items like cooking gas (Ujjwala Yojana) and fertilizers shield poor households from sudden price rises in the market.

  • Support for Livelihoods:
    Input subsidies in agriculture (like on seeds and power) help small farmers continue farming, which is their main source of income.

Without these, the condition of the poor would be much worse, especially during crises like the COVID-19 pandemic, when free food grains (Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana) were a lifeline.

Why Subsidies Are Not Enough to Eradicate Poverty

While crucial, subsidies have built-in limits that stop them from being a permanent solution.

  • A Crutch, Not a Cure:
    Subsidies treat the symptoms of poverty (like not having food today) but not the root causes (like no stable job or education). They offer consumption support, not a way to earn more.

  • Leakages and Inefficiency:
    A big part of subsidy money does not reach the intended poor due to corruption and errors in beneficiary lists. The Economic Survey has in the past noted significant leakages in the old fertilizer subsidy system.

  • Market Distortions:
    Some subsidies can harm the economy in the long run. For example, free electricity for farmers leads to overuse of groundwater, harming the environment. It also limits funds for better infrastructure.

  • The Fiscal Burden:
    Subsidies are very expensive. The Indian government spends lakhs of crores of rupees on them every year. This is money that could also be used to build schools, hospitals, and roads—things that create lasting escape routes from poverty.

  • No Empowerment:
    A subsidy decides what the poor should get (food, fuel). It does not give them the freedom of choice or the power to invest in their own future, like starting a small business.

The Way Forward: From Subsidies to Sustainable Solutions

To truly eradicate poverty, subsidies must be part of a larger, smarter strategy.

  1. Shift to Direct Cash Transfers:
    Replace inefficient subsidies with Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT), like the PM-KISAN scheme for farmers. This gives money directly, cuts leakages, and lets families spend on their most urgent needs—be it food, health, or education.

  2. Invest in Human Capital:
    The only permanent way out of poverty is through education and skills. Government spending must focus on quality schools and vocational training (like PMKVY) to make people employable.

  3. Focus on Job Creation:
    Policies must encourage job growth in labour-intensive sectors like manufacturing, textiles, and tourism. Make in India should aim to create mass employment, not just big factories.

  4. Improve Access to Finance:
    Help the poor access small loans (microfinance) and bank accounts (like Jan Dhan Yojana) to save and invest in income-generating activities.

  5. Targeted and Time-Bound Subsidies:
    Subsidies should be better targeted only to the needy and designed to be reduced over time, as people move up the economic ladder.

Conclusion

Subsidies are like a lifeboat—essential for keeping people from drowning in poverty, but not the ship that will carry them to the shore of prosperity. As economist Amartya Sen said, poverty is not just lack of money, but the lack of capability to live a good life. Subsidies protect some basic capabilities (like being well-nourished) but do not build others (like being educated or skilled). Therefore, while subsidies will remain a critical part of India's fight against poverty, they cannot win it alone. The final victory will come from a combined strategy: smart, direct welfare support today, and massive investment in people's health, education, and opportunities for tomorrow.

33. The debate over nationalism

Nationalism: A Complex Tapestry of Identity and Belonging

Introduction

Nationalism, or a strong feeling of pride and loyalty towards one's nation, is a powerful force in modern politics. In India, it has been the bedrock of the freedom struggle and post-independence unity. However, today, the meaning and expression of nationalism are fiercely debated. This debate centers on a key question: Is nationalism a positive, unifying force of shared progress, or does it risk becoming narrow, divisive, and used to silence critics?

The Case for Nationalism: Unifying and Empowering

Supporters see nationalism as essential for India's strength and unity.

  • A Unifying Force:
    In a diverse country with many languages, religions, and cultures, nationalism creates a shared identity as 'Indians'. It fosters a sense of common purpose, crucial for a developing nation.

  • Foundation of Sovereignty:
    It underpins national security and the protection of borders. The strong public response to events like the 2019 Balakot airstrike and the Galwan Valley clash is often seen as a reflection of patriotic unity.

  • Driver of Development:
    A sense of national pride can motivate citizens to contribute to the country's progress, from paying taxes to following laws. Campaigns like 'Swachh Bharat' (Clean India) appeal to this civic nationalism.

  • Cultural Confidence:
    It encourages pride in India's ancient heritage and civilizational values, projecting soft power globally.

The Criticisms: When Nationalism Turns Divisive

Critics argue that in practice, nationalism can take a harmful form.

  • Majoritarianism vs. Pluralism: The core fear is that nationalism can be defined narrowly around the culture of the majority community, making minorities feel like second-class citizens. Debates over citizenship laws, love jihad narratives, and food habits are seen as signs of this shift.

  • "Us vs. Them" and Suppressing Dissent: It can create binaries where critics of the government are labeled "anti-national." This was seen in reactions to some student protests or journalists' work, chilling free speech under the charge of disrespecting the nation.

  • Historical Distortion: Aggressive nationalism may promote a one-sided view of history, glorifying some aspects while ignoring others, harming honest academic debate.

  • Overlooking Real Issues: An excessive focus on symbolic acts of patriotism can distract from urgent problems like poverty, unemployment, and healthcare.

The Constitutional Ideal: Civic Nationalism

India's Constitution offers a balanced ideal—civic nationalism.

  • Idea of India:
    This model is based not on a single religion or ethnicity, but on shared values of democracy, secularism, equality, and justice. The national identity is tied to the Preamble of the Constitution.

  • Patriotism vs. Nationalism:
    Many argue for patriotism—love for the country and its people—over a rigid nationalism that demands uniformity. As Mahatma Gandhi said, "My nationalism is as broad as the world."

  • Inclusive Symbols:
    True national pride can celebrate all symbols—from ancient universities (Nalanda) to Mughal architecture (Red Fort) to modern ISRO missions.

The Way Forward: Reclaiming an Inclusive Idea

To heal divides, a consensus on healthy nationalism is needed.

  1. Anchor to the Constitution:
    Nationalism must be firmly rooted in the Constitution's principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity for all citizens, without exception.

  2. Promote Democratic Patriotism:
    Encourage pride in India's democratic institutions, diversity, and the peaceful resolution of differences. Respecting the national flag is important, but so is respecting the right to peaceful protest.

  3. Separate Nation from Government:
    A crucial distinction must be made. Criticizing a government's policy is not being anti-national; it is a democratic duty. The nation is its people, not just the ruling party.

  4. Focus on Inclusive Development:
    A nationalism that genuinely improves the lives of all citizens—through jobs, education, and healthcare—will naturally earn love and loyalty, without force.

  5. Educate for Critical Patriotism:
    School education should teach children to love the country while also thinking critically about its challenges, fostering responsible citizens.

Conclusion

The debate over nationalism in India is essentially a debate over the soul of the republic. The choice is between a nationalism of inclusion and one of exclusion. The founding fathers envisioned a nation where unity was woven from diversity, not imposed over it. As Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose said, "Nationalism is inspired by the highest ideals of the human race, satyam [truth], shivam [goodness], sundaram [beauty]." True Indian nationalism, therefore, must be large-hearted, secure in its civilizational ethos, and confident enough to embrace debate and difference. It should be a force that unites us in building a better future, not divides us by reliving past fragments.

34. The effect of global capitalism

Global Capitalism: Unveiling the Modern Prometheus

Introduction

Global capitalism, the worldwide system where trade, industry, and capital are controlled by private owners for profit, has been the driving force of the modern economy. Its spread, accelerated by globalization, has reshaped nations and lives. For a developing country like India, joining this system after 1991 has had profound and mixed results. It has created new wealth and opportunities but also deepened old inequalities and created new challenges.

The Positive Effects: Growth and Integration

The adoption of global capitalist principles has brought significant benefits to the world and India.

  • Economic Growth and Poverty Reduction:
    Global trade and investment have lifted millions out of poverty. According to the World Bank, extreme poverty has fallen globally from 36% in 1990 to about 9% today. In India, economic growth rates improved, helping reduce poverty.

  • Access to Goods, Services, and Capital:
    Consumers get better products at lower prices. Companies can access global markets and foreign investment. India’s IT sector, employing over 5 million people, is a direct result of this integration.

  • Technology Transfer and Innovation:
    Competition drives innovation. India has seen rapid adoption of mobile phones, internet, and digital services (like UPI), boosting productivity and convenience.

  • Job Creation:
    Global supply chains and foreign companies create employment, especially in sectors like services, automotive, and electronics.

The Negative Effects: Inequality and Instability

The same system has created deep problems and vulnerabilities.

  • Rising Inequality:
    The benefits are not shared equally. The Oxfam 2023 report states that the richest 1% in India now own over 40% of the country’s total wealth. Globally, CEOs earn far more than workers.

  • Race to the Bottom:
    To attract investment, countries may lower labour and environmental standards. This can lead to poor working conditions and harm the environment, as seen in some industrial clusters.

  • Market Volatility and Crisis:
    Local economies are now tied to global financial swings. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis and the recent inflation due to the Ukraine war show how external shocks can hurt ordinary people in India through job losses and higher prices.

  • Threat to Local Industries:
    Small farmers and manufacturers often cannot compete with cheap, subsidised imports, leading to distress. The Indian textile and toy sectors have faced such pressures.

  • Cultural Homogenization:
    Global brands and media can overshadow local cultures and traditions.

The Indian Experience: A Mixed Picture

India’s journey reflects this dual nature clearly.

  • Success Stories:
    The rise of a large middle class, world-class companies in IT and pharmaceuticals, and status as a startup hub are successes of engaging with global capitalism.

  • Ongoing Challenges:
    Despite growth, India faces high unemployment, a crisis in agriculture, and widespread informal work without security. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the deep vulnerability of migrant workers in this globalized system.

Towards a More Responsible Global Capitalism

The goal is not to end capitalism but to make it more fair and sustainable.

  1. Strong National Regulations:
    Governments must enforce strong labour laws, environmental rules, and fair tax policies. India’s Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) is a step to regulate big tech.

  2. Invest in Social Security:
    Build strong safety nets—universal healthcare, pensions, and unemployment benefits—to protect people from market shocks. Ayushman Bharat is an important scheme.

  3. Support Small Businesses and Farmers:
    Use policies and subsidies to help them become competitive, ensuring inclusive growth. PM-KISAN provides direct income support.

  4. Global Cooperation for Rules:
    Work for international agreements to stop tax avoidance by multinational companies, manage climate change, and ensure ethical supply chains. India’s push for a global minimum corporate tax is key.

  5. Promote Conscious Consumption:
    Encourage citizens and businesses to support local and ethical products, balancing global choices with local responsibility.

Conclusion

Global capitalism is like a powerful river. It can generate energy and fertility, but without proper channels, it can cause erosion and floods. For India, the task is to wisely harness its flow. This means building strong banks (regulations and institutions) to protect the vulnerable, and creating canals (social policies) to ensure the water reaches everyone. The future lies in advocating for a ‘stakeholder capitalism’ that values workers, communities, and the planet alongside profits. As India aims to become a developed nation by 2047, it must shape global capitalism with its values of equity and sustainability, ensuring that the system works for all, not just a few.

35. UN multilateralism and its role

Harmony in Diversity: The Melody of UN Multilateralism

Introduction

In a world facing complex challenges like climate change, pandemics, and conflict, no single country can find solutions alone. This is where multilateralism—nations working together through agreed rules and institutions—becomes vital. The United Nations (UN), founded in 1945, is the most important symbol of global multilateralism. Its role is to maintain peace, promote development, and protect human rights. However, its effectiveness today is caught between its noble goals and the hard realities of global power politics.

The Foundational Goals and Key Roles

The UN was built on three main pillars, as stated in its Charter: peace, development, and rights.

  • Maintaining International Peace and Security:
    This is the UN's primary goal. The UN Security Council (UNSC) can authorize peacekeeping missions, impose sanctions, and, in theory, take military action. Over the decades, UN peacekeepers have helped stabilize many conflict zones, from Cyprus to Congo.

  • Promoting Sustainable Development:
    Through bodies like the UNDP and specialized agencies like WHO and UNESCO, the UN coordinates global efforts on poverty, health, and education. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are its universal blueprint for development till 2030.

  • Protecting Human Rights and Humanitarian Law:
    The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) sets a global standard. Agencies like UNHCR protect refugees, while the ICJ adjudicates disputes between states based on international law.

Major Criticisms and Current Challenges

Despite its important work, the UN system faces serious criticism, especially in a changed world.

  • Paralysis of the Security Council:
    The veto power of its five permanent members (P5: US, Russia, China, UK, France) often leads to deadlock. This has been starkly visible in the Russia-Ukraine war, where the UNSC could not act due to Russia's veto. Similarly, actions on conflicts like Syria are blocked.

  • Representation Deficit:
    The UNSC reflects the world order of 1945, not 2024. Major contributors like India, Japan, Germany, and entire continents like Africa lack permanent seats, reducing its legitimacy and effectiveness.

  • Funding and Bureaucracy:
    The UN relies on voluntary contributions from members, making its work uncertain. It is also seen as a slow and costly bureaucracy at times.

  • Challenges to Multilateralism:
    Powerful nations are increasingly acting alone or in small groups (like the Quad or AUKUS), bypassing the UN on key security and economic issues.

India’s Engagement and Stance

India has been a firm supporter of the UN but also a vocal critic of its outdated structures.

  • As a Contributor:
    India is a top contributor to UN peacekeeping forces and actively participates in all its agencies. During the COVID-19 pandemic, India’s role as the ‘pharmacy of the world’, supplying vaccines, aligned with UN health goals.

  • As a Reformer:
    India leads the call for UNSC reform as part of the G4 nations. It argues that a more representative Council would make the UN more democratic and effective. India's successful G20 Presidency in 2023, which brought consensus, showcased its ability to drive multilateral outcomes.

The Path Forward: Reform and Renewal

For the UN to remain relevant, meaningful change is needed.

  1. Expand and Reform the UNSC:
    Include new permanent and non-permanent members from underrepresented regions like Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Limit the use of veto in cases of mass atrocities.

  2. Strengthen Cooperative Frameworks:
    The UN should better coordinate with other multilateral groups (like G20, AU, ASEAN) and the private sector to tackle cross-border issues like climate finance and digital governance.

  3. Focus on Global Public Goods:
    The UN should be the central platform for managing crises that affect all humanity—pandemics, climate change, cyber-security, and terrorism.

  4. Increase Accountability and Efficiency:
    Streamline its administration, and make funding more predictable and transparent. Member states must fulfil their financial commitments on time.

  5. Uphold its Founding Principles:
    The UN must consistently champion democracy, human rights, and international law, without bias or double standards, to regain moral authority.

Conclusion

The UN, despite its flaws, remains the world’s most universal platform for dialogue and collective action. As former UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan said, "More than ever before in human history, we share a common destiny. We can master it only if we face it together." In an era of new conflicts and old rivalries, the choice is not between having a UN or not having one. The real choice is between a weak, outdated UN and a reformed, representative, and resilient UN. For India, a strong multilateral system is key to its rise as a responsible global power. Therefore, while working within the UN, India must continue to lead the charge for its renewal, ensuring it can truly deliver on its promise of peace, progress, and justice for all.

36. Universal Civil Code

Title: Embracing Unity in Diversity: The Quest for a Universal Civil Code

Introduction

A Uniform Civil Code (UCC) means having one set of laws for all citizens in personal matters like marriage, divorce, inheritance, and adoption. Currently, different religious communities in India follow their own personal laws. Article 44 of the Constitution says the state should try to create a UCC. This idea sparks a major debate between the goals of national unity, gender justice, and the right of communities to practice their religion.

The Arguments in Favour of a UCC

Supporters believe a UCC is necessary for a modern, fair, and united India.

  • National Integration:
    One law for all citizens, regardless of religion, would strengthen a shared national identity. It would promote the ideal of "unity in diversity" by removing legally enforced differences.

  • Gender Justice:
    Many personal laws are seen as unfair to women. For example, Muslim personal law allows polygamy for men and gives them a stronger right to divorce. A uniform law based on equality could ensure equal rights for women in all families.

  • Constitutional Mandate:
    Article 44 is a clear directive in the Constitution. Implementing it would fulfill the vision of the founding fathers like Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, who saw separate personal laws as a block to national unity.

  • Simplification and Modernity:
    A single law would make the legal system simpler and less confusing. It would also modernize laws, moving away from practices that may not fit a 21st-century democratic republic.

The Concerns and Arguments Against a UCC

Opponents and skeptics raise important questions about timing, process, and impact.

  • Threat to Minority Rights:
    Many fear a UCC would force majority community norms on minorities, harming India's pluralism and the freedom of religion guaranteed by the Constitution.

  • Cultural Homogenization:
    India's strength is its diversity. Critics argue that a UCC could erase unique cultural practices of tribal and other communities, leading to a forced uniformity.

  • Political and Social Conflict:
    Implementing a UCC without wide agreement could create serious social tension and divide communities. It is often seen as a politically sensitive issue.

  • Practical Difficulties:
    Creating a single code that respects all traditions while ensuring justice is very complex. The law must balance many different customs.

The Current Situation and India’s Experience

India already has some uniform laws and states have taken steps, showing a mixed picture.

  • Existing Uniform Laws: Laws like the Special Marriage Act, 1954 allow inter-religious marriages under a common law. The Juvenile Justice Act provides a uniform process for child adoption for all citizens.

  • Goa’s Example: Goa has a common family law, inherited from the Portuguese, which applies to all. It shows that a UCC can work in practice, though in a small state.

  • Recent Developments: The state of Uttarakhand passed a UCC bill in 2024, becoming the first state post-independence to do so. Its implementation and effects are being watched closely.

The Way Forward: A Path of Dialogue and Inclusion

A rushed or forced UCC would be harmful. A careful, consensual approach is needed.

  1. Focus on Reform, Not Replacement:
    Instead of a sudden new code, the government could first work on reforming specific unfair practices within all personal laws, especially those affecting women’s rights.

  2. Widespread Public Consultation:
    A UCC must come from a long, sincere dialogue with all communities, religious leaders, legal experts, and women's groups. The Law Commission has already sought public views on this.

  3. Adopt a Gradual Approach:
    The law could start as a voluntary optional code. Citizens could choose to follow it or their personal law, as suggested by some experts. This would test the idea and build trust.

  4. Learn from State Initiatives:
    The experience of Uttarakhand and Goa should be studied carefully to understand the benefits and problems before thinking of a national law.

  5. Keep Gender Justice as the Core Goal:
    Any discussion on UCC must place the rights and dignity of women at the very centre. Reforms should first address clear inequalities in existing laws.

Conclusion

The debate on the Uniform Civil Code is, at its heart, a debate about India's future identity. It questions how to balance unity with diversity, and tradition with modern rights. A UCC imposed from the top down could shatter social harmony. But a code that emerges from a sincere, bottom-up dialogue, focused on justice—especially for women—could strengthen the republic. As the Supreme Court has noted in various cases, the goal should be a move towards equality. The path forward must be one of patience, empathy, and consensus, ensuring that the quest for uniformity does not become a tool for division, but a step towards a more just and equal India for all its citizens.

37. Agriculture in India

Title: Sowing the Seeds of Prosperity: A Kaleidoscope of Indian Agriculture

Introduction

Agriculture is the backbone of India's economy and society. It provides food for 1.4 billion people and livelihood for nearly half the population. Yet, it is a sector facing a deep crisis. Low incomes, climate change, and outdated methods have created severe distress, seen in farmer protests across the nation. The future of India depends on transforming this vital sector from a struggle for survival into a source of prosperity and sustainability.

The Importance and Challenges of Indian Agriculture

The sector's role is immense, but so are its problems.

  • Economic and Social Pillar:
    Agriculture contributes about 15-18% to India's GDP but employs over 45% of the workforce. This shows low productivity per worker. It is also key to national food security.

  • The Core Challenges:

    • Small Land Holdings:
      The average farm size is shrinking (now about 1.08 hectares), making it hard for farmers to earn enough.

    • Dependence on Monsoon:
      Over half the farmland lacks irrigation, making crops vulnerable to erratic rains and droughts.

    • Low Income and Debt:
      Farming is often not profitable. Many farmers are trapped in debt, a leading cause of distress.

    • Supply Chain Problems:
      Farmers get a small part of the price consumers pay. Middlemen, poor storage, and transportation waste lead to losses. Post-harvest losses are over 10-15% for many crops.

    • Environmental Damage:
      Overuse of water (for rice, sugarcane) and chemicals has degraded soil and drained groundwater in states like Punjab and Haryana.

Government Efforts and Their Mixed Results

The government has many schemes, with limited success.

  • Input Subsidies:
    Schemes for cheap fertilizer, power, and seeds (like PM-KISAN direct cash support) provide relief but also encourage wasteful use of water and chemicals.

  • Price Support:
    Minimum Support Price (MSP) for 23 crops is a safety net, but access is limited mostly to rice and wheat in a few states. This has also harmed crop diversity.

  • Infrastructure and Insurance:
    Programs like PM-AASHA for procurement and PM Fasal Bima Yojana for crop insurance aim to help, but implementation is weak.

  • Recent Reforms and Protests:
    The 2020 Farm Laws, aimed at freeing up trade, were withdrawn after massive year-long protests. Farmers feared they would end the MSP safety net, showing the deep mistrust in the system.

The Path Forward: Solutions for Sustainable Growth

A multi-step approach is needed to fix agriculture.

  1. Diversify and Add Value:
    Move beyond rice and wheat. Promote nutri-cereals (millets), fruits, vegetables, and dairy. Support food processing industries to create jobs and reduce waste. The "PM Formalisation of Micro Food Processing Enterprises (PMFME)" scheme is a good step.

  2. Improve Market Access:
    Strengthen Farmer Producer Organisations (FPOs) so small farmers can bargain better. Develop more modern mandis and create online markets (e-NAM) to connect farmers directly to buyers.

  3. Adopt Sustainable Practices:
    Promote water-saving techniques like drip irrigation and Natural Farming (as in Andhra Pradesh). This will save costs and the environment. The PM Krishi Sinchayee Yojana focuses on water conservation.

  4. Invest in Research and Technology:
    Use biotech for better seeds, and drones for monitoring crops. Provide real-time data on weather and prices via mobile phones.

  5. Secure Income, Not Just Prices:
    Explore models like Rythu Bandhu in Telangana (investment support) or income insurance to ensure a stable yearly income, reducing dependence only on MSP.

Conclusion

Indian agriculture stands at a crossroads. Continuing with the old model of cereal focus, input subsidies, and water-intensive farming is a path to ecological and economic ruin. The future lies in a shift towards "farming as an enterprise." This means treating farmers as entrepreneurs who grow what the market needs, using climate-smart techniques, and getting a fair share of profits. As M.S. Swaminathan, the father of India's Green Revolution, said, the aim should be to double farmers' income. This requires not just government policy but also the active participation of the private sector and farmers themselves. By making farming profitable and sustainable, we can ensure food security for the nation and dignity for the people who feed us.

38. Impaired food security in India

Title: Hungry Shadows: Unraveling Impaired Food Security in India

Introduction

Food security, as defined by the United Nations, means that all people, at all times, have physical and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food. Despite being one of the world's largest producers of food grains, India faces a serious paradox: it has overflowing government granaries, yet a large part of its population suffers from hunger and poor nutrition. This impaired food security is a complex challenge rooted in problems of access, affordability, and the quality of food, not just its production.

The Dimensions of the Challenge

India's food insecurity is visible in three key forms:

  • Chronic Hunger and Undernourishment:
    According to the UN's 'State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World 2023' report, over 16% of India's population is undernourished. The Global Hunger Index 2023 ranks India 111 out of 125 countries, indicating a serious level of hunger.

  • The Burden of Malnutrition:
    The problem is not just lack of food, but lack of the right food. The National Family Health Survey-5 (2019-21) shows that 35.5% of children under five are stunted (too short for their age), and 32.1% are underweight. This affects their mental and physical development for life.

  • Economic Access and Affordability:
    High food inflation, especially in pulses, vegetables, and oils, makes a balanced diet unaffordable for poor families. They often fill their stomachs with cheap cereals (rice, wheat) but miss out on proteins, vitamins, and minerals.

Key Reasons Behind Impaired Food Security

Several interconnected factors cause this situation.

  • Over-Reliance on Cereals:
    Government policies like the Minimum Support Price (MSP) and procurement heavily favour rice and wheat. This has led to less production of nutritious foods like pulses, millets, fruits, and vegetables, pushing up their prices.

  • Poor Access to Safe Water and Sanitation:
    Diseases from dirty water and poor hygiene (like diarrhoea) prevent children from absorbing nutrients from the food they eat, worsening malnutrition. This is a key link often missed.

  • Poverty and Inequality:
    The poorest households spend over half their income on food. Any shock—like job loss, health crisis, or high inflation—forces them to cut down on food quantity or quality.

  • Climate Change and Unstable Production:
    Erratic monsoons, droughts, and floods damage crops, reduce yields, and make food prices unstable, hurting both farmers and consumers.

  • Inefficiencies in the Food Distribution System:
    While the Public Distribution System (PDS) provides food security, leakage, corruption, and inclusion errors mean food does not always reach the neediest.

Government Efforts and Gaps

The government has large programs, but with mixed results.

  • The National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013:
    This is a rights-based law that gives cheap food grains to about 80 crore people. It has provided a safety net against hunger.

  • Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS) and Mid-Day Meal Scheme:
    These aim to improve child nutrition through cooked meals and take-home rations.

  • The Gaps:
    The focus is still mostly on calories from cereals. The quality of meals, especially in terms of protein (pulses, eggs) and micronutrients, is often poor. Also, schemes like ICDS face problems with delivery and the regularity of supplies.

The Way Forward: A Holistic Approach

Fixing food security needs action beyond just handing out grains.

  1. Diversify Food Production and Procurement:
    Encourage and procure millets, pulses, and oilseeds under MSP. Promote kitchen gardens for vegetables. This will improve both farmer income and diet quality.

  2. Fortify and Diversify Food in Safety Nets:
    Add fortified foods (with iron, vitamins) to PDS and school meals. Include items like eggs, pulses, and milk more regularly in ICDS and mid-day meals.

  3. Focus on Women and Children:
    Improve nutrition for adolescent girls and pregnant women to break the cycle of malnutrition. Effectively implement the POSHAN Abhiyaan (National Nutrition Mission).

  4. Link Food with Water and Health:
    Provide clean drinking water and promote toilets (Swachh Bharat) to prevent diseases that cause malnutrition.

  5. Use Technology for Better Delivery:
    Use Aadhaar-linked systems to reduce leakage in PDS. Use mobile apps for real-time monitoring of anganwadi centres and food stocks.

Conclusion

India's fight for food security has moved from the fear of famines to the hidden crisis of malnutrition. The challenge now is to ensure "nutritional security." As the father of India's Green Revolution, M.S. Swaminathan, said, we need to move from a "food security" mindset to a "nutrition security" mindset. This means making sure every citizen, especially every child, has access to a diverse and healthy diet. By fixing our farms, our markets, and our food safety nets, India can transform its impaired food system into one that truly nourishes its people and builds a healthier, stronger future for the nation.

39. UPI, BHIM and ADHAAR linkage and cyber security

Title: Fortifying the Digital Bridge: UPI, BHIM, Aadhaar Linkage, and the Quest for Cybersecurity

Introduction

India's digital payment system, led by the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) and apps like BHIM, has changed how money moves in the economy. Its success is closely tied to Aadhaar, the biometric identity system, which helps verify users. This powerful linkage has brought millions into the formal financial system but has also created a huge target for cyber criminals. Ensuring strong cyber security is now critical to protect people's money and trust in this digital revolution.

The Success Story: Interlinkage and Benefits

The combination of these systems has driven India's digital leap.

  • UPI and BHIM:
    UPI is a real-time payment system. BHIM (Bharat Interface for Money) is a government-made app using UPI. They allow easy, instant bank-to-bank transfers using a simple Virtual Payment Address (like name@bank).

  • Role of Aadhaar:
    Aadhaar provides a secure, unique identity. It is used for e-KYC (Know Your Customer) to open bank accounts (Jan Dhan) and link them for UPI. The Aadhaar Enabled Payment System (AEPS) lets people use biometrics for basic banking.

  • Impact and Scale:
    UPI processed over 11 billion transactions monthly in early 2024. It has made digital payments common for everyone, from street vendors to large businesses, boosting financial inclusion and transparency.

The Cyber Security Risks and Challenges

A system this big and connected naturally attracts risks.

  • Data Privacy and Breaches:
    The central database of Aadhaar, which holds biometric and personal data of over 1.3 billion people, is a prime target. Any major breach would be a national disaster.

  • Fraud and Social Engineering:
    Most cyber crimes are not high-tech hacks but phishing scams. Users get fake calls or messages tricking them into sharing UPI PINs or OTPs. According to the Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C), UPI-related frauds are a major concern.

  • Technical Glitches and Failures:
    System outages in banks or UPI networks can temporarily freeze people's money and shake public confidence.

  • Over-reliance and Exclusion:
    For those dependent on Aadhaar-based authentication, a fingerprint failure (due to hard work or age) can lock them out of their own money and benefits.

Measures Taken and Their Limits

The government and regulators have taken steps, but gaps remain.

  • Regulatory Framework:
    The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) is a key law to govern data use. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) sets strict security rules for banks and payment apps.

  • Security Features:
    UPI uses two-factor authentication (device binding + UPI PIN). NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) monitors transactions for fraud patterns.

  • Public Awareness:
    Campaigns like 'Cyber Jagrookta Diwas' (Cyber Awareness Day) warn people not to share passwords or PINs.

  • The Limits:
    Awareness is still low, especially in rural areas. Law enforcement agencies often lack the skills to track sophisticated digital frauds. The speed of criminal innovation often outpaces security updates.

The Path Ahead: Building a Secure Digital Fort

To protect the digital finance ecosystem, a multi-layered approach is needed.

  1. Strengthen the Law and its Enforcement:
    Implement the Data Protection Act effectively. Fast-track special cyber crime courts and train police to investigate digital fraud.

  2. Invest in Advanced Technology:
    Banks and NPCI must use Artificial Intelligence (AI) and machine learning better to detect and stop fake transactions in real time.

  3. Make Systems More Robust:
    Have regular security audits and "penetration testing" of banking and Aadhaar systems. Ensure strong backup systems to prevent failures.

  4. Launch Targeted Awareness Drives:
    Use simple, regional language campaigns on TV, radio, and SMS to teach people, especially the elderly, about common frauds. A clear motto should be: "Never Share Your PIN or OTP."

  5. Develop a Legal Redressal System:
    Create a simple, quick, and standard process for victims to report fraud and get their money back from banks, with clear accountability.

Conclusion

The linkage of UPI, BHIM, and Aadhaar is a revolutionary Indian success story in digital governance. However, its future depends entirely on trust. As India moves towards a 'Digital India' and a cash-light economy, cyber security cannot be an afterthought; it must be the foundation. The goal must be to build a system that is not only 'ease of living' but also 'ease of securing.' By combining strong laws, advanced technology, and an alert citizenry, India can protect its digital infrastructure. This will ensure that the digital payments revolution empowers every citizen safely, securing both their money and their faith in the system.

40. BRICS developmental bank and its policy

Title: Empowering Nations: BRICS Development Bank's Innovative Policies for a Brighter Future

Introduction

Established in 2015 by Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa (BRICS), the New Development Bank (NDB) emerged as a significant alternative to Western-dominated financial institutions like the World Bank and IMF. With an authorised capital of $100 billion, its core mandate is to mobilise resources for infrastructure and sustainable development projects in BRICS and other emerging economies. As a key founding member, India’s engagement with the NDB offers both strategic opportunities and policy challenges, particularly in its quest for sustainable growth and infrastructure development.

Core Policy Framework and Objectives

The NDB’s policy framework is built on principles of equality, mutual benefit, and national sovereignty. Unlike traditional lenders, it emphasises “country systems,” allowing borrowing nations to use their own environmental and social safeguards, thus speeding up project approvals. Its stated objectives are:

  1. Sustainable Infrastructure:
    Financing projects in clean energy, transport, irrigation, and digital infrastructure.

  2. Climate Action:
    Committing a significant share of its portfolio to renewable energy and adaptation projects.

  3. Local Currency Lending:
    Reducing dependence on the US dollar and mitigating exchange rate risks for borrowers—a major policy innovation.

A quote from its first President, K.V. Kamath, captures its ethos: “The bank will be a catalyst for change, promoting sustainable development as a shared responsibility.”

India’s Engagement: Opportunities and Progress

For India, the NDB aligns perfectly with national priorities like the National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP) and its ambitious climate goals. The bank has emerged as a valuable, supplementary source of funding.

  • Project Financing:
    The NDB has approved over $5 billion in loans for India, funding key projects like the Delhi-Meerut RRTS, Mumbai Metro, and renewable energy initiatives in Rajasthan and Karnataka.

  • Supporting National Missions:
    Its funding aids flagship programs like ‘Make in India’ (industrial corridors) and ‘Smart Cities Mission.’ For instance, a $500 million loan supports the Indore Metro Rail, enhancing urban mobility.

  • Rupee-Denominated Loans:
    The NDB’s policy of local currency lending is crucial. In 2020, it issued a ₹10,000 crore Masala Bond in India, financing projects in Indian Rupees and shielding them from forex volatility—a model of particular relevance amidst global currency fluctuations.

Multi-Dimensional Challenges and Critique

Despite its promise, the NDB faces structural and geopolitical challenges that impact its efficacy for India.

  1. Geopolitical Frictions:
    The Russia-Ukraine conflict has strained BRICS cohesion. Sanctions on Russia, a major shareholder, have complicated the NDB’s operations, temporarily freezing new projects in Russia and highlighting vulnerabilities to global politics.

  2. Scalability and Speed:
    With a portfolio of around $33 billion (as of 2023), it remains dwarfed by traditional MDBs. Project disbursement has sometimes been slower than anticipated, raising questions about its capacity to meet India’s vast financing needs.

  3. Governance and Standards:
    Balancing “country systems” with robust, high-quality safeguards to prevent environmental or social harm remains a work in progress. The bank must ensure projects are not just fast-tracked but also sustainable and inclusive.

  4. Expansion Dilemma:
    The NDB’s move to include new members like Bangladesh, UAE, and Egypt, while increasing capital, also risks diluting the original BRICS focus and could complicate decision-making.

The Way Forward: Policy Solutions for India

For India to maximise benefits, a proactive and nuanced policy approach is needed.

  • Strategic Advocacy:
    India should champion reforms within the NDB to further streamline processes, enhance transparency, and strengthen the local currency lending framework. Pushing for more Rupee-denominated transactions can bolster the currency’s internationalisation.

  • Project Pipeline Management:
    India must develop a ready pipeline of bankable, high-impact projects, especially in green infrastructure and climate resilience, to absorb NDB funding effectively. Aligning proposals with the NDB’s Sustainable Development focus is key.

  • Balancing Partnerships:
    India should strategically use the NDB as a complement, not a substitute, to other sources. Engaging with the Asia Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) and leveraging private capital through blended finance models can create a diversified funding ecosystem.

  • Diplomatic Bridge-Building:
    Within BRICS, India can act as a mediator to manage geopolitical tensions, ensuring the NDB remains a purely developmental, non-aligned institution. As External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar noted, “Multipolarity is best advanced by multiple offers of development partnerships.”

Conclusion

The New Development Bank represents a pragmatic rebalancing of the global financial architecture. For India, it is a timely instrument offering strategic autonomy, crucial infrastructure financing, and support for its green transition. While challenges of scale, geopolitics, and governance persist, a calculated and solution-oriented approach can help India harness the NDB’s potential effectively. By doing so, India can not only fuel its own development but also help shape an emerging model of cooperative, Southern-led multilateralism that is more responsive to the needs of the 21st century.

Introduction

An anti-Western attitude refers to a stance of opposition, criticism, or rejection of the political, cultural, and economic influence of Western nations, primarily the United States and Europe. This sentiment is not a single ideology but a complex spectrum of feelings seen globally, including in India. Historical and Political Roots

Historically, anti-Western feelings stem from the experience of colonialism and imperialism. Many nations, including India, faced long periods of foreign rule that exploited resources and suppressed local cultures. Post-independence, this history created a deep-seated caution against perceived neo-colonialism. During the Cold War, the non-aligned movement, led by figures like India's Jawaharlal Nehru, was a political expression of this desire for an independent path, separate from Western and Soviet blocs. As historian Vijay Prashad notes, the Global South often sought "the right to a future different from that offered by the West."

Cultural and Economic Dimensions

Culturally, anti-Western sentiment can arise from a fear of homogenisation, where global brands and media are seen as eroding traditional values and lifestyles. In India, debates over "Westernisation" versus "modernisation" are common. Economically, the sentiment is fuelled by perceived unfairness. Policies of institutions like the IMF and World Bank are sometimes viewed as imposing harsh conditions that favour Western interests. The 2008 global financial crisis, which originated in the West, further damaged the perception of Western economic models.

The Current Indian Context

In contemporary India, the attitude is nuanced and issue-based. It is not a blanket rejection but a selective assertion of sovereignty.

  • Strategic Autonomy:
    India's decision to continue buying Russian oil despite Western pressure over the Ukraine war is a prime example. It is driven by national interest, not ideology, highlighting a refusal to be dictated by Western foreign policy.

  • Digital Sovereignty:
    Bans on certain Chinese apps and debates on data localisation reflect a desire to control the digital economy, often seen as dominated by Western tech giants.

  • Cultural Assertion:
    There is a renewed emphasis on promoting Indian languages, knowledge systems, and products (e.g., the 'Vocal for Local' initiative). This is less about being "anti-West" and more about being "pro-India."

The Dangers and Drawbacks

However, a hardened anti-Western stance carries risks:

  1. Isolation:
    It can lead to diplomatic and economic isolation, cutting off access to technology, investment, and markets crucial for development.

  2. Missed Opportunities:
    Rejecting beneficial aspects of Western collaboration—in science, education, and green technology—can hinder progress.

  3. Internal Divisiveness:
    It can be used to label legitimate criticism or diverse ideas as "foreign" or "anti-national," harming healthy democratic debate.

Striking a Balanced Path: Possible Solutions

The solution lies not in being for or against the West, but in pursuing a balanced, strategic, and confident approach.

  • Strategic Issue-Based Partnerships:
    India should engage with the West on terms of mutual respect and benefit. The India-US initiative on Critical and Emerging Technology (iCET) is a good model, combining Indian talent with Western innovation for shared goals.

  • Confident Cultural Exchange:
    Rather than rejecting foreign culture, India should promote its own globally while being open to positive influences. As Swami Vivekananda said, "We must learn from the West, but not become the West."

  • Reform Global Governance:
    India should actively work to reform international institutions to make them more representative. This addresses the root cause of economic grievances rather than just opposing the West.

  • Focus on National Capacity:
    The ultimate safeguard is building domestic strength—in manufacturing (Atmanirbhar Bharat), technology, and defence. A strong, self-reliant India can engage with all blocs from a position of confidence, not defiance.

Conclusion

The anti-Western attitude in India and elsewhere is a symptom of a world in transition, where old power structures are being questioned. For India, a purely reactionary stance is neither practical nor beneficial. The high-scoring path forward is "strategic autonomy with selective engagement." India must be a confident civilisational state that learns from all, is dictated by none, and collaborates based on enlightened national interest. In a multipolar world, the goal should not be to oppose the West, but to ensure India’s voice, values, and developmental model find their rightful place on the global stage.

China’s rapid infrastructure build-up along the Line of Actual Control (LAC), including villages and bridges, has changed the situation on the ground. Beyond the border, China’s “String of Pearls” strategy, with ports in Pakistan (Gwadar), Sri Lanka (Hambantota), and Myanmar, is seen as a plan to surround India in the Indian Ocean. This creates a deep sense of strategic insecurity.

The Economic Dimension: Partner and Rival

Economically, the relationship is deeply tied but unbalanced. China is India’s largest trading partner in goods, with trade crossing $135 billion in 2022. However, India faces a massive trade deficit of over $100 billion, importing electronics, machinery, and chemicals while exporting mostly raw materials. This dependence became a vulnerability during the 2020 border stand-off, prompting India to ban hundreds of Chinese mobile apps (like TikTok) and tighten rules on investment. Global Governance: Competition for Influence

People-to-people ties and cultural exchanges, though reduced, form a base. As former Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi said, “India and China have a long history… we must build on the positive.” Multilateral diplomacy often requires them to work together.

India needs a balanced and smart policy to handle the China factor.

  1. Build Comprehensive National Power:
    The foundation is internal strength. This means faster economic growth (to match China’s scale), investing in technology (like semiconductors), and building robust infrastructure. The Atmanirbhar Bharat (Self-reliant India) campaign aims to create strong domestic supply chains.

  2. Smart Border Management:
    India must continue improving border infrastructure and military readiness for a long-term stand-off. At the same time, it should keep open channels for military and diplomatic talks to manage crises and prevent escalation.

  3. Economic Re-balancing:
    India should attract global supply chains away from China (“China Plus One”) by offering incentives. It must also strengthen its own manufacturing (Production Linked Incentive schemes) and export capabilities to reduce the trade gap.

  4. Strengthen Strategic Partnerships:
    To balance China, deepening ties with like-minded nations is key. The Quad (with US, Japan, Australia) is central to this, focusing on a free and open Indo-Pacific. Stronger ties with Europe and ASEAN are equally important.

  5. Engage in Multilateral Forums:
    India should use platforms like the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) to engage China directly, discuss differences, and cooperate where interests align, such as on terrorism or Afghanistan.

Conclusion

The China Factor is not a simple problem to be solved, but a permanent condition to be managed. India’s approach cannot be based on emotion but on cold national interest. It must combine military strength at the border with economic resilience at home and agile diplomacy abroad. The goal is not confrontation but the creation of a stable balance where India protects its sovereignty, secures its interests, and continues its own rise in the shadow of the Dragon. A strong, confident, and growing India is the best answer to the challenges posed by China’s ascent.

43. The pollution in urban areas

Title: The Haze Within: Navigating the Complex Realm of Urban Pollution

Introduction

India's cities are engines of growth, but they are choking. Urban pollution, a mix of dirty air, contaminated water, and solid waste, has become a severe public health and environmental crisis. As millions move to cities seeking a better life, they often find themselves trapped in a toxic environment that harms their well-being and slows economic progress.

The Multi-Dimensional Problem

Urban pollution is not one issue, but many interconnected problems.

  1. Air Pollution:
    This is the most visible threat. According to the World Air Quality Report 2023, Indian cities dominate the list of the world's most polluted. Delhi's severe winter smog, caused by vehicle fumes, industrial smoke, dust from construction, and farm fires in nearby states, is a yearly disaster. It leads to a high number of lung diseases, heart problems, and early deaths.

  2. Water Pollution:
    Most Indian cities dump untreated or partly treated sewage into their rivers and lakes. The Yamuna in Delhi and the Ganga in Kanpur are examples of biologically dead rivers in urban stretches. Industrial waste and chemical run-offs make this water unsafe for drinking or bathing, causing water-borne diseases.

  3. Waste Management Crisis:
    Cities generate over 150,000 metric tonnes of solid waste per day, as per government data. Much of it ends up in overflowing landfills like Ghazipur in Delhi or Deonar in Mumbai, which catch fire and pollute the air. Plastic waste clogs drains and harms wildlife.

Root Causes of the Crisis

Several factors combine to create this problem:

  • Rapid and Unplanned Growth:
    Cities have expanded faster than their capacity to manage pollution. There is a lack of green spaces, and public transport has not kept up, pushing people to use private vehicles.

  • Weak Enforcement:
    While laws exist (like the Air Act, 1981), enforcement is often poor. Industries may ignore rules, and old, polluting vehicles remain on roads.

  • Cross-Border Challenges:
    As seen with Delhi, pollution sources like farm fires are not limited to city borders, making it a regional issue that requires state cooperation.

  • Lifestyle and Awareness:
    Increasing use of plastic, waste burning, and a lack of citizen participation in segregation add to the problem.

Impacts on Health and Economy

The cost of pollution is enormous. The Lancet journal reported that air pollution alone caused over 1.6 million deaths in India in 2019. It lowers productivity as people fall sick, increases spending on healthcare, and scares away tourists and skilled workers. Polluted cities find it harder to attract investment, and the quality of life falls for everyone.

A Multi-Pronged Solution Strategy

Solving urban pollution needs action from the government, industries, and citizens.

  1. Strengthening Public Transport:
    The focus must be on clean, affordable, and efficient mass transit. The expansion of metro networks in cities like Nagpur and Chennai is a good step. Promoting electric buses and creating safe walking and cycling paths can reduce vehicle use.

  2. Clean Technology Adoption:
    A strict move to cleaner fuels for industry and vehicles is needed. The push for electric vehicles (EVs), supported by schemes like FAME India, must be faster. Waste-to-energy plants can help manage garbage and create power.

  3. Better Waste Management:
    Strictly following the rules of Swachh Bharat Mission 2.0 for waste processing. Citizens must separate wet and dry waste at home. Companies should be made responsible for the plastic they produce (Extended Producer Responsibility).

  4. River and Water Body Rejuvenation:
    Missions like the Namami Gange need to be implemented more effectively at the city level. Every city must have modern sewage treatment plants. Rainwater harvesting can help refill groundwater.

  5. Citizen-Led Action and Awareness:
    People must be made aware through campaigns. Simple actions like using public transport, saving energy, and planting trees can help. The "Red Light On, Gaadi Off" campaign in Delhi is a small but useful example.

Conclusion

Urban pollution in India is a complex crisis, but it is not impossible to solve. It requires a sustained "war on pollution" with the same seriousness as a war. The solution lies in moving from short-term fixes (like smog towers) to long-term systemic changes in how we build, travel, and consume. As the Supreme Court of India noted, citizens have a "right to clean air." By combining strong policy, smart technology, and active citizen participation, India's cities can transform from pollution hotspots into models of sustainable and healthy living. The clean air of our cities will be the true sign of a developed India.

44. Threatening diseases and health hazards

Title: Unseen Shadows: Confronting Threatening Diseases and Health Hazards

Introduction

India faces a complex health challenge, often called the ‘dual disease burden’. This means we are fighting old, infectious diseases and new, lifestyle-related diseases at the same time. This mix creates serious threats to public health, economic progress, and social stability.

The Double Burden of Disease

India’s health hazards can be divided into two main groups:

1. Persistent Infectious Diseases:
These are illnesses caused by germs like bacteria and viruses. They remain a major threat.

  • Tuberculosis (TB):
    India has the world's highest TB burden, with over 2.5 million cases yearly, as per WHO 2022. Drug-resistant TB is a growing worry.

  • Vector-Borne Diseases:
    Malaria, Dengue, and Chikungunya spread rapidly, especially during monsoons. For example, Delhi saw over 9,000 dengue cases in 2023.

  • Emerging Pandemics:

  • The COVID-19 pandemic showed how a new virus can disrupt the entire nation, exposing weaknesses in our health system.

2. Rising Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs):
These are not infections, but long-term illnesses linked to lifestyle.

  • Heart Disease & Diabetes:

  • India is called the 'diabetes capital' of the world, with over 100 million patients. Heart disease is a leading cause of death.

  • Cancer:
    Cases are rising, with tobacco use (like gutka) being a major cause for lung and mouth cancers.

  • Mental Health:
    Often ignored, issues like depression and anxiety affect over 10% of the population, worsened by the pandemic's stress.

Underlying Causes and Risks

Several factors make India vulnerable:

  • Weak Health Systems:
    Public hospitals are often overcrowded and lack resources, especially in rural areas. There is a shortage of doctors and nurses.

  • Environmental Factors:
    Polluted air in cities (like Delhi's smog) causes asthma and lung disease. Contaminated water spreads diarrhoea and hepatitis.

  • Lifestyle Changes:
    Less physical activity, more junk food, high tobacco and alcohol use are driving NCDs.

  • Social and Economic Gaps:
    Poor people suffer the most because they have less access to clean water, good food, and healthcare.

As Dr. K. Srinath Reddy notes, "India's health system is battling a war on two fronts, with an army designed for a different era."

The Wider Impact

These health threats have effects beyond hospitals:

  • Human Cost:
    They cause immense suffering and early death. Families lose their earning members.

  • Economic Cost:
    Illness reduces productivity and increases medical costs, pushing many into poverty. NCDs alone could cost India $6 trillion by 2030, as per a WHO study.

  • Social Strain:
    Epidemics can cause panic and stigma, as seen during COVID-19.

A Multi-Pronged Solution Strategy

To tackle this, we need a strong and smart plan:

1. Strengthen Public Health Systems:

  • Increase health spending to at least 2.5% of GDP, as promised in the National Health Policy.

  • Boost the Ayushman Bharat scheme to provide wider financial protection and improve Primary Health Centres (PHCs) for early care.

2. Focus on Prevention:

  • Launch strong campaigns against tobacco, sugar, and salt. The 'Eat Right India' movement is a good step.

  • Make cities more walkable, with parks and clean air, to encourage activity.

  • Ensure clean drinking water and better sanitation for all under the Jal Jeevan Mission.

3. Use Technology and Innovation:

  • Use telemedicine (like e-Sanjeevani) to reach remote areas.

  • Promote digital tools for tracking diseases (like the Integrated Health Information Platform) and managing patient records.

4. Build a Skilled Health Workforce:

  • Train more community health workers (ASHA workers).

  • Increase the number of medical colleges, especially in under-served states.

5. Foster Public-Private Partnerships (PPP):

  • Work with private companies for better health infrastructure and vaccine research.

Conclusion

India's fight against threatening diseases is a critical part of its journey to becoming a developed nation. The goal is not just to treat sickness but to build a society where good health is the norm. By investing in strong public health systems, focusing on prevention, using technology, and ensuring everyone has access to care, India can overcome this double burden. A healthy population is the true foundation of a strong and prosperous India. As the ancient saying goes, "Sarve Santu Niramayah" – let all be free from illness. This should be our guiding principle.

Religion holds a deep place in Indian society. However, in a diverse, democratic nation, religious beliefs must evolve to embrace reason, ethics, and social harmony.     Welcoming questions and aligning beliefs with scientific understanding. As Swami Vivekananda said, “We want to lead mankind to the place where there is neither the Vedas, nor the Bible, nor the Quran... but this does not mean destruction.”

  3. Social Reform:
    Using religious principles to challenge social evils like caste discrimination, gender inequality, and intolerance. The 19th century reformers like Raja Ram Mohan Roy and Jyotiba Phule did this by re-interpreting texts to oppose sati and promote education for all.

Relevance in Contemporary India

Progressive religious thought is crucial for modern India’s challenges:

  • Countering Fundamentalism:
    It acts as a buffer against rigid, violent interpretations of religion that threaten national unity. It promotes the idea that all paths to truth are valid.

  • Gender Justice:
    It questions patriarchal interpretations of religious laws. For instance, the ongoing debate on women’s entry into temples (like Sabarimala) and mosques calls for a progressive reading of tradition.

  • Scientific Temper:
    It helps reconcile faith with science, whether on matters of health, environment, or education. This is vital in fighting superstitions and promoting rational thinking.

  • Environmental Ethics:
    Many progressive movements highlight the teachings of stewardship and respect for nature found in all religions, supporting sustainable development.

Despite its importance, promoting progressive thought faces hurdles:

  1. Orthodox Opposition:
    Established religious authorities often resist change to protect their influence and traditional power structures.

  2. Political Instrumentalisation:
    Religion is often used for political gain, promoting division over harmony. This makes progressive voices seem weak or “anti-traditional.”

  3. Literalism:
    A literal reading of ancient texts, without considering historical context, can justify outdated social practices.

  4. Lack of Education:
    Widespread religious illiteracy makes people vulnerable to extremist views rather than nuanced, progressive interpretations.

Pathways to Fostering Progressive Thought

A multi-level approach is needed to encourage progressive religious beliefs:

  • Education Reform:
    The school curriculum should include comparative religion and ethics, teaching the reformist history within all faiths. As per NEP 2020, education must develop “rational thought, scientific temper, and ethical reasoning.”

  • Platform for Moderate Voices:

  • Grassroots Interfaith Dialogue:
    Encouraging local community projects where different religious groups work together on issues like poverty or cleanliness (as seen in the Bhumi project in Bengaluru) builds practical harmony.

  • Role of Influencers:
    Religious leaders with large followings should be encouraged to speak on climate change, gender equality, and health from a faith perspective, as Pope Francis has done.

Conclusion

For India to truly thrive as a 21st-century nation, the spirit of its faiths must be one of progress, not regression. Progressive religious thought is not a threat to tradition but its true fulfilment—ensuring faith remains a source of comfort, ethics, and unity, not conflict. By empowering reformist voices, strengthening constitutional morality, and fostering an educated citizenry, India can build a society where religion and reason walk hand in hand. This will honour its ancient ideal of “Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam” (the world is one family) and secure a just, peaceful, and modern future for all its citizens.

Title: Climbing the Ladder: Social Mobility in India and Beyond

Introduction

Social mobility refers to the movement of individuals or groups between social and economic levels. It is the measure of a society's openness and fairness. High mobility means a child can dream of a better life than their parents. For a diverse democracy like India, enabling this mobility is not just an economic goal, but a test of its constitutional promise of justice and equality.

The State of Social Mobility in India

India presents a mixed picture. On one hand, economic growth has created new opportunities.

  • Positive Signs:
    The rise of a large middle class, success stories in IT and services, and targeted policies like reservations have enabled some upward movement. Reports suggest poverty has reduced, allowing basic income mobility.

  • Persistent Challenges:
    However, deep-rooted barriers remain. A 2020 World Economic Forum report ranked India 76th in Social Mobility Index, lower than many peers. A child's caste, family wealth, and birthplace still heavily decide their future. As per NSSO data, a child from a top 20% family is 20 times more likely to go to college than one from the bottom 20%. This shows opportunity is not equal.

Key Barriers to Mobility

Several interconnected factors limit mobility in India:

  1. The Caste Shadow:
    Despite laws, caste discrimination affects access to education, jobs, and social dignity in rural and urban areas.

  2. Unequal Education:
    The quality gap between a rich private school and a poor government school is vast. This unfair start affects learning outcomes and future earnings.

  3. Economic Inequality:
    Wealth gives huge advantages—better healthcare, networks, and safety nets. The poor often lack capital to start businesses or take risks.

  4. Regional and Rural-Urban Divide:
    A child born in a village in Bihar has far fewer chances than one born in Delhi or Bengaluru, due to weaker infrastructure and fewer jobs.

  5. Gender Disparity:
    Social norms restrict women's mobility, workforce participation, and control over resources, limiting half the population's potential.

Other nations offer lessons. Nordic countries (like Denmark, Finland) rank high due to strong public education, healthcare, and social security. The United States, once a symbol of the "American Dream," now sees declining mobility due to rising inequality and high education costs. China has used rapid industrialization to lift millions out of poverty, but political controls limit other freedoms. The key lesson is that state policy (tax, education spending) is crucial in shaping mobility.

To build a more mobile society, India needs action on many fronts:

  1. Revolutionise Early Childhood Care and Education:

  2. Skill for the Future:
    Link education with market needs through schemes like Skill India. Promote apprenticeships so youth can earn while they learn.

  3. Broaden Affirmative Action:
    While caste-based quotas continue, consider adding economic criteria to reach the poor from all communities. Focus on improving access to quality education rather than just jobs.

  4. Build Basic Security Nets:
    Strengthen MGNREGA, public healthcare (Ayushman Bharat), and food security (PDS). This gives poor families stability to invest in their children's future.

  5. Create Equal Opportunity Zones:
    Incentivise industries to set up in poorer states. Improve rural infrastructure (roads, internet) to bridge the geographic divide.

  6. Promote Entrepreneurship:
    Simplify rules and provide micro-credit, especially for women and SC/ST entrepreneurs, to create wealth at the grassroots.

Conclusion

True social mobility is the ladder that turns a rigid society into a vibrant, just, and dynamic nation. For India, with its ancient burdens of caste and new challenges of inequality, building this ladder is its most important task. It requires not just economic growth, but a conscious, continuous effort to level the playing field through education, opportunity, and empowerment. As Dr. B.R. Ambedkar envisioned, we must create a society based on "liberty, equality, and fraternity." When every child, regardless of birth, can climb as high as their talent and hard work will take them, India will have truly secured justice for all its citizens.

Title: Unleashing the Muse of Ingenuity: Exploring the Realm of Artificial Intelligence

Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is no longer science fiction. It is the technology that allows machines to learn, reason, and solve problems like humans. From suggesting movies to driving cars, AI is changing our world. For a nation like India, with its vast young population and digital goals, AI presents a unique mix of great opportunities and serious challenges.

What is AI? Understanding the Basics

In simple terms, AI is the intelligence shown by machines. It includes systems that can understand language (like Alexa), recognise images (like Facebook tagging photos), and make decisions (like Google Maps finding the best route). Tools like ChatGPT show how AI can write and chat like a person. The core of modern AI is Machine Learning, where computers learn from huge amounts of data without being directly programmed for every task.

The Promise: AI as an Opportunity for India

AI can be a powerful tool for India's development:

  1. Transforming Public Services:
    AI can make governance smarter. It is already used in crop disease prediction for farmers, forecasting floods, and improving the accuracy of the Ayushman Bharat health scheme.

  2. Boosting the Economy:
    AI can add an estimated $500 billion to India's GDP by 2025, as per a NASSCOM report. It can create new jobs in data science, make factories more efficient, and help Indian startups innovate.

    AI can help diagnose diseases from scans, personalise education for students in remote areas, and manage city traffic and pollution.

India needs a clear national strategy to maximise AI's benefits and reduce its harms.

  2. Invest in People, Not Just Tech:
    The focus must be on mass reskilling. Programs like the FutureSkills Prime initiative must train millions in AI-related skills. Education should promote critical thinking, not just rote learning.

  3. Make AI for All (AI4All):
    Develop AI tools in local Indian languages. Use AI in agriculture, healthcare, and education to solve problems for the common citizen, not just the rich.

  4. Promote Research and Innovation:
    Support Indian startups and universities in AI research. Create test-beds for safe AI development, learning from global models.

  5. International Cooperation:
    Work with global forums to set rules for responsible AI use, especially in areas like weapons and deepfakes.

Conclusion

AI is a transformative force, akin to fire or electricity—a tool whose value depends on how we use it. For India, the goal cannot be to stop AI, but to steer it wisely. We must harness its power to build a more efficient, healthy, and educated society, while actively guarding against its risks to jobs, privacy, and fairness. By putting people at the centre of its AI policy, India can ensure that this powerful technology becomes a ladder for inclusive growth, not a wedge for greater division. 48. Emerging Threats to India’ Internal Security

Title: Unmasking Shadows: Emerging Threats to India's Internal Security

Introduction

Internal security means protecting a nation from threats within its own borders. While traditional challenges like militancy in Jammu & Kashmir or Left-Wing Extremism persist, India now faces a new set of complex, emerging threats. These non-military dangers exploit technology, society, and environment, posing a serious risk to national unity, economic stability, and public safety.

The Cyber Threat: An Invisible Warfront

Cyber attacks are now a top security concern. India faced over 13 lakh cyber incidents in 2022, as per CERT-In data. These range from hacking of critical infrastructure (like the 2022 attack on AIIMS servers), financial frauds, to ransomware attacks on hospitals and power grids. State-sponsored hackers target government data and create social discord by spreading fake news. As our lives become more digital, a major cyber attack can cripple banks, transport, and communication systems.

Terrorism and Radicalisation in New Forms

Terrorism has evolved beyond traditional groups.

  • Lone-Wolf Attacks:
    Inspired by online propaganda, individuals or small groups carry out attacks using simple tools, as seen in some recent cases of violence.

  • Use of Technology:
    Terror groups use encrypted messaging apps and drones for smuggling and surveillance. The 2019 drone attack on Jammu Air Force station was a warning.

  • Online Radicalisation:
    Social media platforms are used to spread extremist ideas, recruit youth, and incite violence, creating a security threat from within communities.

Communal and Social Polarisation

Deepening social divides are a major threat to internal peace. Hate speech, misinformation on social media, and mob violence can quickly lead to large-scale riots, damaging social harmony. Events like the 2020 Delhi riots show how engineered polarisation can break law and order. This constant tension diverts police resources and erodes public trust in the state.

Organised Crime and Narco-Terrorism

Organised crime networks dealing in drugs, illegal weapons, and human trafficking fund other illegal activities. India’s location near the ‘Golden Crescent’ (Afghanistan-Iran-Pakistan) makes it vulnerable. Drug money is often used to fund terrorism—a link called narco-terrorism. The seizure of 3,300 kg of drugs off the Gujarat coast in 2022 highlights the scale of this threat.

Climate Change as a Security Threat

Climate change is increasingly seen as a ‘threat multiplier’. More frequent and severe disasters like floods and cyclones strain resources and can lead to mass migration. Water shortages and loss of farmland can cause conflicts between communities and states, adding to existing tensions.

The Way Forward: A Multi-Pronged Strategy

To counter these new-age threats, India needs a modern, coordinated, and smart approach.

  1. Strengthen Cyber Defences:
    Create a strong cyber command, train more cybersecurity experts, and build secure digital infrastructure. The National Cyber Security Strategy 2023 is a needed step in this direction.

  2. Counter-Extremism through Community Policing:
    Security agencies must work closely with communities to build trust and identify early signs of radicalisation. Programs to rehabilitate and reintegrate misguided youth are crucial.

  3. Regulate Social Media:
    Enforcing laws against online hate speech and fake news is needed, while balancing it with freedom of speech. Tech companies must be made accountable.

  4. Enhance Coordination:
    Improve intelligence sharing between central agencies (like IB, RAW) and state police through better technology. The Multi-Agency Centre (MAC) should be strengthened.

  5. Border and Coastal Security:
    Use technology like sensors, drones, and satellites to secure land and sea borders against smuggling and infiltration. The Coastal Security Scheme needs more resources.

  6. Build Climate Resilience:
    Integrate climate action with security planning. Prepare disaster management plans that include strategies to prevent climate-induced conflicts.

Conclusion

India’s internal security landscape is changing rapidly. The new threats are less visible, more widespread, and exploit our interconnected world. The old tools of law and order are not enough. The solution lies in a whole-of-society approach that combines advanced technology, alert citizens, coordinated agencies, and smart policies. As the former National Security Advisor, Shivshankar Menon, noted, security is finally about “the defence of the idea of India.” Protecting this idea in the 21st century requires guarding not just our borders, but also our cyberspace, social harmony, and environment. A secure India is the foundation for its rise as a global power.

50. Crisis of credibility in Indian Electronic Media

Title: Dimming Truths: Navigating the Crisis of Credibility in Indian Electronic Media

INTRODUCTION

Electronic media in India, once hailed as a pillar of democracy, now faces a deep crisis of public trust. From a trusted source of information, it is increasingly seen as a source of noise, bias, and spectacle. This erosion of credibility threatens not just the media’s own standing but the health of India’s democratic conversation, where an informed citizenry is essential.

THE MANIFESTATIONS OF THE CRISIS

The signs of this crisis are visible daily:

  • Sensationalism Over Substance:
    The race for higher ratings, or Television Rating Points (TRPs), has made news a form of entertainment. Loud debates, where participants shout over each other, have replaced calm discussion. Tragic events are often turned into dramatic, repetitive spectacles, losing respect for those affected.

  • Political Bias and Polarization:
    Many news channels are seen as openly supporting or opposing specific political parties. This creates "echo chambers," where viewers only hear opinions that match their existing beliefs. The line between news anchors and political campaigners has often blurred.

  • Spread of Misinformation:
    In the rush to break news first, fact-checking is sometimes ignored. False or unverified claims, especially on sensitive social issues, can spread rapidly, causing real-world harm and social tension.

  • Corporate and Owner Influence:
    The interests of large business groups that own many media houses can shape news coverage. Important stories about economic issues, environment, or labour rights may get less attention if they conflict with these interests.

  • Declining Investigative Journalism:
    Expensive and risky deep investigations into corruption or power are becoming rare. Instead, there is a focus on cheaply produced, high-volume opinion-based programming.

WHY HAS THIS HAPPENED? UNDERSTANDING THE CAUSES

Several factors have led to this situation:

  1. The TRP Business Model:
    Advertising revenue depends on viewership. This creates pressure to make news dramatic and emotionally charged to attract more eyes, even if it means sacrificing balance.

  2. Ownership Patterns:
    The concentration of media ownership in a few hands limits the diversity of voices. As veteran journalist P. Sainath notes, "You get the media that the corporate structure permits."

  3. Political Alignment:
    Close relationships between some media owners and political powers can lead to soft coverage for allies and overly critical reporting for opponents.

  4. Social Media Pressure:
    The 24/7 news cycle, driven by trends on social media platforms like Twitter (now X), forces channels to react quickly, often without proper verification.

THE CONSEQUENCES: A WEAKENED DEMOCRACY

The cost of this crisis is high:

  • Public Mistrust:
    A 2023 Reuters Institute report found that trust in news in India is low, at only 41%. Many citizens are turning to social media, which can be even less reliable.

  • Polarized Society:
    Biased media deepens social and political divides, making constructive debate almost impossible.

  • Distraction from Real Issues:
    Constant focus on noisy debates draws attention away from critical issues like climate change, healthcare, education, and rural distress.

  • Undermines Democratic Accountability:
    When media fails to act as a watchdog, those in power face less scrutiny.

THE PATH TO RESTORING TRUST: POSSIBLE SOLUTIONS

Reclaiming credibility requires effort from media houses, regulators, and the public:

  • For Media Houses:
    They must voluntarily return to core principles. This means investing in ground reporting, reinstating the roles of public and chief editors to ensure quality control, and creating clear divisions between the newsroom and the business side. Labels like "Paid Promotion" or "Opinion" must be clearly shown.

  • Strengthening Regulation:
    The self-regulatory body, the News Broadcasters & Digital Standards Authority (NBDSA), needs more power and wider membership. Penalties for violations should be meaningful. Cross-ownership rules (between media and other industries) could be reviewed to prevent conflicts of interest.

  • Role of the Public:
    Citizens must actively support independent journalism through subscriptions. Media literacy is crucial to help people identify biased or false news. Choosing to watch balanced debates and diverse channels can also create demand for better content.

  • Promoting Public Service Broadcasting:
    Strengthening impartial and robust public broadcasters like Doordarshan and All India Radio can provide a trusted benchmark for private media.

  • Legal Reforms:
    Laws against defamation should not be used to silence journalists. At the same time, laws can be framed to ensure greater transparency in media ownership.

CONCLUSION

The credibility crisis in Indian electronic media is a serious threat to informed democracy. The fourth pillar is showing cracks. However, this is not a point of despair but a call for correction. The solution lies in a shared commitment: media houses must choose public service over profit, regulators must ensure fair play, and citizens must reward integrity with their viewership. As the saying goes, "Freedom of the press is not just for the person who owns one." It is a freedom for every citizen to receive fair, factual, and fearless information. Only by restoring this trust can Indian media fulfill its true democratic duty.
